# FORGE G5 — Red-Team: Execution Failure Modes (Pilot.BM)

**Role:** G5 Red-Team. **Date:** 2026-06-10.
**Mandate:** surface ≥5 real, reproducible *execution* failure modes of actually building + launching this site. G4a already attacked the premise (CE-1 through CE-6). This gate does NOT repeat premise attacks — it finds the failure modes that survive even if the owner accepts the G4a reframe and commits to building.

---

## Failure Modes Table

| # | Mode | Trigger / Repro | Severity | Mitigation — or Accepted-Risk Waiver |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **FM-1** | **`.bm` domain is structurally ineligible** | The `.bm` ccTLD is administered by the Bermuda Government's Department of E-Commerce and restricted to entities with a demonstrable Bermuda nexus — typically a registered Bermuda company or a work-permit holder. A US-resident individual with no Bermuda legal entity cannot register a `.bm` domain through the standard path. Repro: attempt registration at bm-registry.bm → application rejected at eligibility check. The **fallback** `.com` (pilotbermuda.com, pilot-bm.com, etc.) is available but the repo/brand is already named "Pilot.BM" — the `.bm` name is the brand. | **High** | **Mitigation:** settle this as the literal first action before any branding investment (Phase 0 pre-gate, both plans say so). If ineligible: commit to a `.com` slug that still signals Bermuda clearly. Fallback is workable but must be decided *before* design-system work begins or the brand identity is built around a domain that can never be obtained. Residual risk: `.bm` brand equity built via social / SEO under a `.com` address; acceptable if decided now, expensive if discovered after launch. **Not an unmitigated blocker** — provided the settling step happens this week. |
| **FM-2** | **Affiliate programs reject the site at zero traffic** | Viator, GetYourGuide (GYG), and CJ/Awin (the Booking.com gateway) apply editorial + traffic review at approval. Viator's affiliate program (via Partnerize/CJ) and GYG both require a *live, content-complete site* for manual review; they have discretion to reject or hold new applications from sites with <1k sessions/mo. CJ/Awin specifically runs a "site quality review" (live site + privacy policy + content volume check) that typically takes 1–5 business days — and the reviewer can reject or "pend" with no explanation and no SLA on reconsideration. OpenTable's affiliate program requires a minimum *restaurant booking* volume threshold before committing to the $1–2/reservation model; at zero traffic it may offer a subthreshold flat rate or require re-negotiation. Repro: submit affiliate applications during Phase 0 (pre-content) → CJ/Awin rejects or holds → Phase 1 launches without Booking.com affiliate revenue. | **High** | **Mitigation:** do NOT submit affiliate applications before the site has content at a staging URL. Submit during Phase 1 (Visitors MVP) with the Getting Around page and at least 5–10 content pieces live on a publicly accessible URL. For the window between Phase 0 completion and affiliate approval: use direct Booking.com links (no commission but no broken UX) and hard-code the `/go/:slug` redirect to a non-affiliate deep-link as placeholder. **Do NOT count affiliate revenue in Month 1–2 projections** — assume a 4–8 week approval lag as baseline. GYG is typically faster than CJ/Awin; apply GYG first to have at least one tours affiliate live at launch. This is manageable but must be sequenced correctly; both plans currently assume affiliates "day 1" without accounting for approval latency. |
| **FM-3** | **Booking.com "Bookinggeddon" repeat: CJ/Awin terminates small affiliates without warning** | In May 2025 Booking.com gutted its small-affiliate direct program and forced smaller publishers through CJ/Awin. The research synthesis (§A, affiliate table) already flags this. The **second-order risk** that both plans inherit but don't fully address: CJ/Awin itself maintains a minimum-earnings threshold — publishers generating less than a threshold (CJ historically $25–50/quarter, exact current threshold `[unverified]`) can be auto-deactivated after 3–6 inactive months. A new site that takes 3–6 months to reach any booking traffic is structurally at risk of CJ deactivation before the first commission clears. Repro: launch → Booking.com CJ/Awin link approved → low traffic in months 1–3 → CJ auto-deactivates the publisher account → `/go/hotel-*` links 404 on the Booking.com deep-link because the affiliate params are now invalid → silently earns $0 and may leak trust signal to Google. | **Medium** | **Mitigation (already partially present):** Plan A's first-party `/go/:slug` redirect table is the correct architectural response — a dead affiliate param in one row is a 5-minute fix, not a site-wide failure. **Harden it:** (1) Log 302 redirects + add a weekly automated check that follows each `/go/*` slug and verifies the destination returns 200 (not a CJ/Awin error page). (2) Maintain a direct (non-affiliate) fallback URL per slug as the `fallback_url` column — if the affiliate param is deactivated, the redirect falls back to the non-affiliate URL rather than breaking. (3) Don't count Booking.com CJ affiliate revenue until the account has survived ≥1 active-quarter check. Accepted residual: if CJ de-activates, Booking.com revenue is zero for the deactivation+reinstatement cycle (~4–8 wks); `/go/` architecture limits the blast radius. |
| **FM-4** | **Stripe directory-billing edge cases break listing state** | Three concrete failure modes in the billing spine: **(a) Listing claimed by the wrong party.** The self-serve "claim your listing" flow (email link → Stripe Checkout) has no identity verification — anyone who receives or guesses the claim link can take over a business's listing and start a subscription under that business's slot. A competitor or malicious actor claims a rival's free listing, pays for Featured, then edits it to defame. **(b) Subscription lapses → listing silently degrades.** A business's card fails; Stripe retries (3–4 attempts over 7 days) then marks the subscription `past_due` → `canceled`. If the webhook handler has a bug or misses an event, the `listing.tier` stays `featured` in Postgres while Stripe has `canceled` — the business gets Featured placement without paying, indefinitely. **(c) Proration + mid-cycle upgrade.** A business upgrades from Featured ($30) to Premium ($50) mid-cycle. Stripe prorates correctly but the webhook receives `customer.subscription.updated` with the new `plan.id` — if the handler only checks for `checkout.session.completed`, the tier change is never applied in Postgres. The listing stays Featured while Stripe bills for Premium, creating a support/trust incident. | **High** | **Mitigations per sub-mode:** **(a) Claim fraud:** add a time-limited signed token (HMAC) to the claim link (not just a UUID); log all claim events with claimant email + IP; add a manual review step for the first 20 claims (one email confirmation from the business owner's domain). **(b) Webhook reliability:** use Stripe's webhook idempotency key; add a nightly reconciliation job that queries `stripe.subscriptions.list()` for all known `stripe_subscription_id`s and syncs `listing.status` and `listing.tier` against live Stripe state — this is the "defense in depth" that catches missed webhook events. **(c) Tier sync on upgrade/downgrade:** handle ALL of `checkout.session.completed`, `customer.subscription.updated`, `customer.subscription.deleted`, and `invoice.payment_failed` in the webhook. Write an integration test (Stripe test-mode + a real webhook) that exercises each event type and asserts the correct `listing.tier` in Postgres. None of these are exotic — they're standard Stripe billing hygiene that both plans assumed without specifying. Ship the reconciliation job before go-live. |
| **FM-5** | **A Mover acts on a stale regulated figure and the site gets named** | Trigger: the Bermuda government changes the SHB mandatory health premium (currently $400.31/adult/mo), a payroll-tax rate, or a work-permit fee. The compensation calculator and cost-of-living pages still show the old figure. A Mover budgets a Bermuda relocation using the calculator, accepts a job offer, moves, and discovers their real health insurance cost is 20% higher than the calculator showed. They post about it publicly (LinkedIn / Reddit / Bermuda forums), naming Pilot.BM as the source of the wrong figure. Even if the disclaimer ("verify at gov.bm") is visible, the **reputational repro** is: "that fancy Bermuda guide told me the wrong number and I moved my family for it." This is also a potential small-claims vector in some jurisdictions. The site's Movers positioning as "genuinely accurate vs. brochure-ware" makes a public accuracy failure *more* damaging than it would be on a generic travel site. | **High** | **Mitigation (partially addressed in both plans, but incompletely):** Plan A's `verified_on` field + freshness CI that flags pages >12 months stale is correct but insufficient — **SHB premiums change annually, payroll-tax rates can change mid-year**. The mitigation must be: (1) Set the staleness trigger on regulated figures to **90 days, not 12 months**, for all Mover tools and cost-of-living pages. (2) Each ruleset JSON/YAML that feeds the calculator carries a `source_url` + `next_review_date` field that the CI job checks; a page whose `next_review_date` has passed is a **build warning** (not a failure — you don't want to block the site over it) but generates an email to the owner immediately. (3) Add a **prominent "last verified [date]" badge** directly on the calculator output (both plans mention `verified_on` but it's in the footnotes — put it in the tool's output, adjacent to the figures that rely on it). (4) The disclaimer modal ("verify at gov.bm") that fires on first calculator load is the correct final backstop — keep it, but it reduces not eliminates the liability. **This is an unmitigated High if the staleness CI threshold stays at 12 months.** Reduce to 90 days; that makes it adequately mitigated. |
| **FM-6** | **Google HCU / indexing sandbox on a thin-but-growing niche travel site** | Trigger: Pilot.BM launches with 30–40 pages (the Phase 1 corpus), submits a sitemap, and begins publishing at 2 pieces/week. Google's Helpful Content Update (HCU, confirmed in the research: a comparable travel blog dropped 37k→12k sessions post-HCU) evaluates the site in the first 1–4 months. The signal Google uses for niche travel sites post-HCU: topic authority (do you cover the topic deeply, or is this thin content with affiliate links?), E-E-A-T signals (who wrote this? is there a named author with verifiable Bermuda expertise?), and "purpose of the site" (is this primarily affiliate-link arbitrage, or genuinely helpful?). A new site with 30 pages, no author bio, high affiliate-link density on `/visit/stay` and `/visit/do`, and a directory that looks like a yellow-pages clone — even if the content is good — presents a pattern Google's HCU classifiers are trained to penalize. Repro: site launches → Google indexes it → ranks it briefly → HCU classifier fires (typically 6–18 months post-launch on new niche sites) → organic traffic drops 50–70% → directory sales pitch loses its proof → cascade failure (CE-1 from G4a, but the *trigger* here is a concrete HCU event, not just general risk). | **Medium** | **Mitigation:** (1) **Named author with genuine expertise signal.** The owner has real financial/regulatory expertise about Bermuda — that is E-E-A-T. Create an author page with the owner's credentials (financial analyst, specific Bermuda regulatory work) and link it from every article. This is the single highest-leverage HCU defense a new site can make. (2) **Affiliate link density control.** Keep affiliate CTAs to ≤2 per article; nofollow all affiliate links (Google recommends this for affiliate sites and it removes an HCU risk signal). The `/go/:slug` redirect architecture already provides the right centralization — add the `rel="nofollow sponsored"` attribute. (3) **The Getting Around page is the HCU defense.** A deeply-researched, unique, tool-enriched page on a topic no other site covers well is exactly the "genuinely helpful" signal the classifier rewards. Build this page to 3,000+ words with the interactive tool, real transport cost data, and a clear author voice — not thin affiliate content. (4) **Do not launch with <20 substantive pages.** A 30-page launch corpus is defensible only if all 30 are deep; Plan B's "skeleton" Movers/Residents content is an HCU risk if the skeletons are thin (200-word pages with disclaimers and a directory embed). Keep skeleton pages private/noindex until they have depth. |
| **FM-7** | **Bermuda imagery rights failure: "high-end" requires photography the owner doesn't have rights to** | Trigger: the owner sources Bermuda imagery from Google Images, tourism board websites, or Unsplash for the launch design (this is the most common "just get the site live" path). Multiple failure modes: (a) Bermuda Tourism Authority (BTA) images are licensed to BTA for promotional use by approved tourism partners — using them on a commercial directory site without a partnership agreement is a breach. (b) Generic Unsplash Bermuda photography is competitively available to every site, including gotobermuda.com — it erodes the "high-end" differentiator immediately. (c) A rights-holder finds their image on the site → DMCA takedown → the hero image of the homepage disappears 48 hours before a major content push → trust/brand damage. (d) Getty / Shutterstock standard licenses explicitly prohibit use on sites where the image is "the primary value" (i.e., a directory listing page showing a business's venue is arguably using the image as primary value). | **Medium** | **Mitigation:** (1) **BTA partnership outreach.** Apply for the Bermuda Tourism Authority's approved-partner status before launch — this unlocks licensed BTA imagery and also gives the site legitimacy for local business sales outreach. The BTA has a digital partners program; a high-end editorial site is exactly the partner profile they want. (2) **Budget for original photography.** Even one 2-day on-island shoot by a Bermuda-based photographer (cost estimate: $500–1,500 USD for a half-day commercial license) produces 30–50 rights-cleared images that no competitor can use — this is the genuine "high-end" moat. This is an unbudgeted cost in both plans' infra tables; add it as a one-time Phase 1 line item. (3) **For MVP before photography is in hand:** use Wikimedia Commons (CC-licensed Bermuda imagery, attribution required) for the launch skeleton and build a photography replacement sprint for Month 2–3. Do NOT use Unsplash as the permanent solution. |
| **FM-8** | **Solo-owner bus-factor / cadence lapse: what visibly breaks first** | Trigger: the owner has a high-workload quarter (financial analyst work peaks), a health event, or simply the post-launch fatigue that hits 3–4 months into a 12–16 hr/wk content sprint. Content publishing drops from 2 pieces/week to 0 for 4–6 weeks. Specific cascade: (a) **Events calendar goes stale first** (weekly curation gap → residents see an empty or outdated events list → credibility loss with the highest-frequency returning audience). (b) **Regulated figures lapse** (if the 90-day freshness gate isn't wired, SHB/tax figures age silently). (c) **Directory listing renewals go un-chased** (businesses with `past_due` subscriptions need a human follow-up email within 7 days to avoid churn; a 4-week lapse means 100% churn of any past-due listings). (d) **SEO ranking decay** — Google's freshness signal penalizes sites where publication cadence drops sharply; the Getting Around page can coast on its depth, but the cluster pages around it decay. The **first visible failure is the events calendar** — it's the most publicly dated content type, and a stale events list signals site abandonment to both users and Google. | **Medium** | **Mitigation (structural, not motivational):** (1) **Scope the events calendar to be auto-fed where possible.** The Bermuda Tourism Authority publishes an events feed; scraping or partnering with Bernews (who runs an events API informally) means the events calendar survives a 4-week owner absence with partial automation. Don't build a fully manual calendar. (2) **Directory renewal emails are Stripe's job.** Stripe's Billing already sends `past_due` notification emails to the subscriber automatically — the owner doesn't need to manually chase them if the email copy is good. Wire this in Phase 1, not Phase 2. (3) **Identify the one page the site can't afford to let die** (Getting Around) and set a "never let this go >90 days without a data refresh" calendar reminder from day 1. (4) **The 90-day freshness CI gate (from FM-5's mitigation)** is also the bus-factor canary — if the owner hasn't touched regulated pages in 90 days, the CI warning surfaces it automatically without requiring discipline. |

## Unmitigated High-Severity Blockers — Verdict

**One High-severity mode remains inadequately mitigated unless a specific change is made:**

**FM-5 (Stale regulated figure → liability event):** Both plans use a 12-month freshness threshold (Plan A's CI staleness gate; Plan B's quarterly cycle). Bermuda immigration/fee/tax figures change on a schedule of months, not years (WFB program closed Feb 2025, policy revised Oct 2025 — two changes in 8 months). A 12-month staleness gate on the compensation calculator is structurally too loose. **This loops back to the plan: change the freshness threshold on all Mover tool rulesets to 90 days and make the CI warning immediate (email to owner on same day as flag, not batched weekly).** Once that change is incorporated, FM-5 is adequately mitigated.

All other High-severity modes (FM-1, FM-2, FM-4) have concrete mitigations available:
- FM-1 (`.bm` domain): settle this week; `.com` fallback is fully adequate.
- FM-2 (affiliate approval lag): sequence applications *after* content exists; don't count affiliate revenue in Month 1–2.
- FM-4 (Stripe billing edge cases): implement reconciliation job + claim-token hardening before go-live; this is standard billing engineering, not exotic.
