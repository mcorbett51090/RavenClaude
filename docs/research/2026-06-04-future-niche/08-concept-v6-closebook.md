# Closebook — Concept Document v6 — post-panel (endorse-with-changes, 5/5)

*An AI workbench that makes a finance team's own qualified reviewer dramatically faster at the recurring, deadline-bound parts of close and cash forecasting — the reviewer still owns and signs every number, the AI does the legwork with a verifiable source-and-control trail under each figure, and every figure carries a "show your work" record you can hand a lender, board, or auditor. Sold as a hands-on consulting service by a financial-analyst founder. The honest default is a one-person business sized to one person's calendar (Track A). On top of that default sit three named, gated, opt-in ambition levers (Track B) that remove the calendar ceiling without reintroducing any of the risks four critic rounds killed — pulled only when a stated gate is met, never baked into the base-case math.*

> **A note on the name.** Through v5 this concept was called "Closebook + Leash." The five-seat panel ruled the two-noun compound a positioning liability for the buyer we actually serve (see §0 and §3). **The buyer-facing name is now "Closebook" alone.** "Leash" survives only as *internal / developer vocabulary* — the name of the governance layer's machinery and of the gated Track-B developer fork. This is the only place "Leash" appears in a buyer-facing sense, and it is noted here precisely so the change is a recorded decision, not a silent drift.

## 1. One-line pitch

**Closebook gets more close out of the controller you already have — a fully-sourced, cross-system close and 13-week cash draft your own reviewer signs in hours instead of days, for a fraction of the cost of the next finance hire.**

> **The honest qualifier leads, and it names who signs:** the AI drafts; **the buyer's own qualified reviewer signs.** This is never "CFO-grade output" on its own — it is *the buyer's reviewer producing their normal signed output faster*, because they review a fully-sourced, deterministically-computed draft instead of assembling it by hand. **That signer is never Matt** (a financial analyst, not a CPA) **and is never a CPA we supply.** If a company has no such reviewer, it is not a customer we can serve today. This single-signer rule is the entire liability posture, so it leads.
>
> **And the honest ceiling leads too:** by default this is a hands-on consulting service whose capacity is one person's calendar. v6 does not promise software-style scale in the base case, because the load-bearing asset (cross-system reconciliation) is built bespoke per customer, not as a general engine (§7). **Ambition is available — but as a set of gated, opt-in levers (Track B, §10) layered on top of that honest default, never assumed into the base-case math.**

## 2. Why this shape (honoring the judges)

All three internal judges scored the two concrete-deliverable plays highest and proposed the same merge: lead with the finance deliverable (a pain the buyer already pays for), embed governance as the trust mechanism that deliverable already needs.

- **Verdict's** gateway-API model is dev-facing and folds in only as **Track-B Lever 3** (governance-IP productization, §10), gated hard, not the lead.
- **Aegis's** evidence-pack export grafts in as the governance layer's enterprise face — *the human-behavior trail, not a binder documenting our own framework.*
- **Forge** is the **internal delivery method** (`/wrap` → `contribute-finding` → FORGE), not a sellable thing. Verified built: `commands/wrap.md`, the contribution skills, `skills/forge-pipeline`.

> **Why this pairing leads over the other 22 plugins — an honest answer, not "the judges said so."** The internal-panel score is a *design* signal, not a *market* signal. The market reasons this pairing is the wedge: (1) it sits on **Matt's only native-habitat domain** — he *is* a financial analyst, not a Salesforce admin or a freight broker, so finance is the one domain where founder credibility is real, not borrowed; (2) close + cash is a **calendar-forced, recurring, already-budgeted** chore (§3); (3) the rare governance assets need a *high-stakes, human-signed, auditable* deliverable to matter, and finance is the cleanest such deliverable in the catalog. **One service, one buyer, one substrate.**

## 3. The buyer and the purchase trigger

**The buyer is precisely defined: a company that already employs a qualified finance reviewer** — a controller, accounting manager, or fractional CFO who today signs the close — **and whose books span more than one system** (an accounting suite plus a separate billing/payroll/expense tool), so the close is a cross-system assembly job, not a one-tool button. Practically, the **~50–150-person company where a controller exists but is the bottleneck**, not the 20-person founder-does-finance shop.

**Why we deliberately exclude the no-reviewer company:** the entire risk mitigation is "deterministic compute + a *qualified human signs*." If no qualified human exists in-house, the only way to supply one is to contract a CPA to sign work they didn't produce — which no competent CPA accepts for a marginal fee. v6 refuses to build on that dependency. The no-reviewer market reopens only if the deterministic engine ever becomes defensible enough to stand without a same-firm signer — a future bet, not a launch claim.

### 3a. The trigger that actually bites THIS buyer — reviewer-capacity strain, not a deal-at-risk deadline

**v5's most important correction, carried forward.** Earlier versions inherited a "forced event" trigger — fundraise diligence, covenant loan, SOC window, board demanding a clean 13-week cash view *they can't produce*. But that acute, "we cannot produce this and a deal is at risk" pain lives at the **under-reviewed** firm — the one without enough qualified reviewer capacity, which we *exclude*. A 50–150-person company that already has a controller signing a cross-system close every month **already clears those deadlines today** — surviving them is literally the controller's job. Selling that buyer a $30k+ engagement against a deadline they already beat is selling a vitamin at a painkiller's price. It won't convert.

**So v5/v6 picks the trigger that is genuinely acute for the buyer it chose: the reviewer is the bottleneck, and the alternative is spending money to relieve them.** The wallet opens when:

- **Close is eating 5–10 days of the controller's month** and the company is growing, so the squeeze is getting worse, not better.
- **A new system was just added** (new billing platform, an acquisition, a new payroll provider) and the cross-system stitch the controller does by hand just got harder.
- **The board or a lender moved to a tighter cadence** (monthly cash reporting, faster close) — not a one-time deal, an *ongoing* expectation the current process can't sustain without the controller working nights.
- **The realistic next step is otherwise to hire** — a $110–160k fully-loaded senior accountant, or another tool the controller still has to drive. Closebook is sold explicitly as **the cheaper, faster alternative to that hire** — but honestly, with the math shown (§3d), including the cases where a hire actually wins.

> **The honest demand thesis, matched to the buyer:** the pain is "our existing reviewer is maxed out and the company is asking them for more" — a recurring, budgeted, comparison-against-a-hire decision, not a one-time crisis. It converts at the price of *part of an FTE*. We are not promising to rescue a collapsing close; we are making a known-good close cost less reviewer time than it does today.

**The losing trigger (instrument it).** The single fastest way to learn the positioning is wrong is if warm finance-operator intros consistently respond *"our controller already handles this fine."* That means the capacity-strain trigger isn't acute enough in the contacted segment — and the fix is to **tighten the ICP toward companies that JUST added a system or JUST got a tighter board/lender cadence**, not to lower the price. Instrument the first 10 conversations for which sub-trigger was present, and let that data re-aim the ICP rather than guessing.

**The governance trigger is secondary until verified.** v1 leaned on a cyber-insurance / questionnaire trigger (ISO GenAI E&O exclusions Jan 2026), sourced only to secondary aggregators. **Until re-verified against the primary ISO filing and actual carrier language, the governance trigger is a "closes-faster nice-to-have," not a co-equal pillar.** The business case stands on reviewer-capacity strain alone.

> **Quarantined statistics:** the upstream "34%-more-confident-when-wrong" and "47%/22% monitoring" figures are unverified secondary-aggregator numbers and must **never** appear in buyer-facing material without primary-source verification.

### 3b. Why "make your existing reviewer faster" beats the reviewer's own in-tool AI

The buyer's controller already owns the GL and increasingly has **Excel Copilot or their accounting suite's native AI** drafting in-tool. So why pay for Closebook? **The value is not "draft a number faster in one tool" — it's "assemble, source, and make defensible a package that spans tools and ends in a signed artifact a third party will accept."** An in-tool assistant helps inside the one system its data sits in. It does **not**:

1. **Stitch across systems.** The painful part of a real close is reconciling the accounting suite against billing, payroll, and expense — different schemas, different cutoffs. Closebook builds per-customer reconciliation scripts for exactly that seam (§4c). An Excel-native agent presupposes the consolidated workbook already exists — which *is* the work.
2. **Produce a third-party-facing evidence trail.** A lender/board/auditor wants "show me how this number was built and who controlled the AI that touched it." In-tool AI produces a number, not a lineage card + a pre-action Stop + a decision log.
3. **Carry a governance posture the auditor asks about.** "What is your AI allowed to do, and what did you block?" — the governance layer answers this; a drafting assistant cannot.

> **The data-handling edge — the sharpest distinction (panel enhancement).** Excel Copilot / accounting-suite native AI run *inside* the customer's own tenant on live data — a higher data-exposure surface than the buyer may realize. A consultant who works **only from scoped, deletable exports** and returns a **signed deletion attestation** (§4d) can credibly say: *"your most sensitive financial data never touches a third-party AI's training-eligible context, and we prove its deletion."* That is exactly the assurance story a security-conscious controller wants — and a story the incumbents structurally cannot tell. This converts the data-handling work from a gap into a differentiator.

> **The honest hedge:** if Microsoft or an accounting suite ships **cross-system consolidation + an AI-action audit trail**, this wedge narrows fast. So the offering is **consulting-first** (§7), renting a window, not claiming a fortress.

### 3c. The internal sale — arming the bottleneck to get budget

**The economic buyer is not the person who feels the pain.** At an 80-person company the controller — the maxed-out reviewer we target — almost never owns a $30–38k+ discretionary spend. The **CFO, VP Finance, or owner** does. A pitch that wins the user but has no path to the approver dies in procurement. So v6 plans for a **two-stakeholder sale**:

- **The user / champion = the controller.** They feel the pain, they validate the deliverable, and they carry the case internally.
- **The approver = the CFO / owner.** They hold the budget and sign the PO. The first sales motion targets the approver **with the controller as the internal champion** — not the controller alone.

**Closebook ships the champion a one-page business case in the approver's language.** The template (a fill-in-the-blanks page Matt provides during the sales motion) states, in the CFO's terms:

- **Hours saved × loaded controller rate** — e.g. "close drops from 8 days to 2; that's ~6 controller-days/month × [loaded daily rate] = $X/yr of reclaimed senior capacity."
- **Avoided-hire cost** — "the alternative is a $110–160k fully-loaded senior accountant; this engagement delivers the throughput gain at a fraction of that, with no headcount" (with the honest TCO comparison, §3d, attached).
- **Faster close → faster board/lender reporting** — the value to the board of a clean monthly cash view produced on time.
- **A clear ask** — the specific package, price, and the bounded first step (the Mapping Assessment, §7a) so the approver's first "yes" is small.

The point: give the controller the ammunition to walk into the CFO's office and win the spend against either a permanent hire or "just work the tools harder." Without this page, the sale stalls at exactly that door.

### 3d. The buyer-side TCO comparison — including when a hire wins

v5 asserted "cheaper than a $110–160k hire" but never computed it at the buyer's decision table. A CFO runs that math in 90 seconds, so v6 shows it honestly — including the rows where the engagement **loses** to a hire. (A CFO trusts the vendor who shows them when *not* to buy.)

| Buyer's option | Year-1 all-in (illustrative) | What they get | When this wins |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Closebook engagement** | First Close $30–38k + retainer ($5–9k/mo if taken). Engagement-only ≈ $30–38k; engagement + 6-mo retainer ≈ $60–92k; + full-year retainer ≈ $90–146k | A signed, fully-sourced, cross-system close + 13-week cash, reviewer keeps the signature, no headcount, reusable bespoke scripts the buyer owns | Capacity need is **acute now and likely <12 months**; **no headcount slot** available or wanted; **board/lender wants speed immediately**; the buyer values an **owned, re-runnable script asset** + an audit-ready trail |
| **Hire a senior accountant** | $110–160k fully-loaded (salary + benefits + overhead), recurring every year | Permanent owned capacity, institutional knowledge, flexes to other finance work, no single-person-vendor dependency | The need is **permanent and broad**; the company wants **owned capacity** it controls; there is **budget and a headcount slot**; the work extends well beyond close + cash |
| **Do nothing / work the tools harder** | $0 incremental | The controller keeps grinding; close stays at 5–10 days; in-tool AI helps inside one system only | The capacity strain is **mild or temporary**, the board cadence is **not tightening**, and the controller has slack |

> **The honest reading:** the engagement wins on the **<12-month, no-headcount, speed-now** profile and on the value of an owned re-runnable asset plus an audit trail. It **loses to a permanent hire** when the need is durable, broad, and the company wants owned capacity it isn't dependent on one external person for. v6 states both, because the central value claim has to be *demonstrated where the buyer evaluates it*, not asserted — and because conceding the loss cases is what makes the win cases credible. (Note the single-person-dependency caveat is real and is mitigated by the buyer-owns-the-scripts continuity clause, Risk #13.)

## 4. The offering — what is real day one vs. what gets built

This separates **the supervised analyst service that works today** (LLM reasoning over GL exports, the buyer's reviewer signs) from **the engineering that makes it faster and more defensible** — which is *built bespoke inside engagements*, not as a pre-built product.

### 4a. Day-one deliverables (drafted by the workbench; the buyer's reviewer signs)

The buyer provides exports (CSV/JSON) from each system. With Matt-plus-the-agents producing the draft and **the buyer's own reviewer signing**, they receive:

1. **Month-end close package draft** — variance commentary and board-pack exhibits, drafted from their actuals across systems. The agent reasons from the verified `month-end-close`, `variance-commentary`, `board-pack-composition` playbooks; the **numbers are computed by deterministic scripts the agent writes and the reviewer re-runs** (§4c), not free-typed by the LLM.
2. **13-week direct-method cash forecast draft** — covenant-headroom view with action triggers.
3. **Driver-based operating forecast** and, in a live transaction, a **DCF/valuation view**.
4. **SOC/audit-prep control walkthrough** drafted to satisfy an auditor on review.
5. **The "show your work" lineage card under every figure** — source ledger rows → transformation applied → who signed → timestamp. *(Day one this is a **human-authored source trail**: the reviewer's recon workpaper rendered as a card. It does not yet include a tribunal verdict; see §4b.)*

### 4b. The governance layer ("Leash," internally) — scoped honestly

The rare governance assets are real (verified: `guard-destructive.sh`, `guard-web-access.sh`, `guard-recursive-spawn.sh`, the `thing-decide.py` tribunal, `comfort-posture.yaml`, the `/ragnarok` kill switch). **But the tribunal has only ever adjudicated marketplace-development decisions, and no wiring connects a finance action to a Sága record.** So:

| Governance feature | Status today | What's needed |
|---|---|---|
| **The Stop** — block the workbench from an irreversible/high-stakes action *on the provided exports and locally-staged files* (restate a closed-period working file, overwrite a locked-period file, touch credentials) with a plain-English reason | **Real** — guard hooks fire on tool calls now. **Scope is precise:** it controls the *workbench's own* actions on local/staged files, **NOT the customer's live ERP/GL/banking systems** (the tooling never connects to those) — see Risk #14 | Define finance-specific "high-stakes action" patterns (e.g. any write to a locked-period working file) |
| **The dial** (Strict / Balanced / Exploratory) | **Real** — `comfort-posture.yaml` + `set-posture` | Map the three levels to finance-relevant tool scopes |
| **Kill switch** (`/ragnarok`) | **Real** | None |
| **Gated AI judgment with a logged verdict under a finance number** | **Aspirational** — the tribunal works but has never judged a finance call, and the lineage card isn't wired to it | Route a defined class of finance judgment calls through `thing-decide.py`, link the Sága record to the figure's lineage card. *Until this ships, the lineage card sells only the human-authored source trail — itself real and valuable — and this capability is **never** demoed as live (Risk #15).* |
| **Evidence pack / questionnaire crosswalk** (Aegis-grafted) | Buildable from the decision log | Map to one real insurer + one vendor-security (SIG/CAIQ-style) questionnaire + NIST AI RMF / ISO 42001 clauses; **add a sanitization layer** that strips internal injection-guard markers before render (Risk #15) |

### 4c. The middle layer — bespoke, customer-specific reconciliation scripts (NOT a general engine)

**This is the sharpest reframing, and it directly answers the "you can't build BlackLine solo in 4 months" objection.** The gap between "an LLM that has read good accounting prose" and "numbers a reviewer will sign" is a deterministic calculation/reconciliation layer. v4 called this "the product" and budgeted a *general* cross-system engine as a 4-month solo build. **That is the wrong thing to build and v6 does not build it:**

- A general engine that auto-reconciles *any* SMB's idiosyncratic QuickBooks/Bill.com/Gusto exports — solving cross-system identity resolution with no shared key, per-system cutoff/timing, currency, partial postings, and per-customer schema variation — is a multi-year, large-team problem (BlackLine, FloQast). An LLM "just writing the join logic" over an unseen customer's messy exports is *exactly* where determinism breaks: the agent guesses the join key, the mapping, the timing rule. **One person does not build that in four months, and v6 stops claiming it.**

- **What v6 actually builds is bespoke, per-engagement.** For each customer, Matt + the agent (as a coding assistant) hand-build reconciliation/variance/cash-roll scripts **tailored to that customer's specific systems and chart of accounts**, which **the buyer's reviewer reads and re-runs**. The determinism comes from the script being fixed, reviewed, and reproducible *for that customer* — not from a general engine that generalizes across strangers' books. The cross-system-identity problem is solved **once per customer, by a human deciding the join rules with the agent's help**, not auto-solved at runtime.

- **The scripts are hand-built Python/SQL, model-agnostic at runtime.** This is also the platform-dependency hedge (Risk #12): the *numbers* do not depend on Claude being available or cheap at run time; only the *build assistance* does. A reviewer re-runs deterministic code, not an LLM.

- **The reusable asset is a pattern library, not a product.** What `/wrap` deposits back into the marketplace are **scaffolds and recurring patterns** — common COA shapes, typical QuickBooks↔billing tie-out structures, JE-pattern templates — that make the *next* bespoke build faster to assemble. They do **not** make the next customer's reconciliation automatic; a human still confirms the mapping. **Company A's mapping does not make Company B's reconciliation free; it makes Matt's next build modestly faster to hand-assemble.** That is a real, compounding efficiency — but it is consulting leverage, not software leverage.

- This is what makes a number defensible — **reproducible by the buyer's reviewer**, not generated. It directly answers the existential liability (Risk #1): LLM-generated numbers are the danger; **a fixed, reviewer-re-run script plus the reviewer's signature is the mitigation.**

> **Backed by ten verified finance knowledge files** (ASC 718/805/740, WACC sourcing, accrual/cutoff discipline, FP&A models, variance triage) — so the *reasoning* draws on canon. The *arithmetic* is deterministic, hand-built, customer-specific code. Reasoning from canon ≠ computing the number, and bespoke ≠ general — v6 keeps all three separate on purpose.

### 4d. Data handling & confidentiality (the missing P0 — now a sales asset)

The entire offering rests on customers handing over general-ledger exports — payroll-bearing, customer-list-bearing, transaction-level financial data. A finance buyer's own vendor-security questionnaire (the very SIG/CAIQ-style questionnaire the evidence pack maps against) will ask exactly how that data is handled. v5 had no answer; v6 states the posture concretely **and turns it into a differentiator that pre-answers the questionnaire:**

1. **Isolated, encrypted per-engagement storage — NOT the shared working tree.** Customer GL exports are scoped to a **dedicated, encrypted-at-rest volume or a separate repo per client**, never the shared RavenClaude development working tree (which hosts MCP servers and background agents). Each engagement's data is isolated from every other engagement's.
2. **Stated retention window + mandatory deletion + a deletion attestation.** Data is retained only for the engagement's defined window, **deleted at engagement close**, and the buyer **receives a signed deletion attestation** — a document they can file against their own questionnaire and show their auditor.
3. **A DPA / confidentiality + data-handling addendum** in the engagement contract — a one-page agreement covering scope, retention, deletion, and confidentiality, so the data posture is contractual, not informal.
4. **A hardened build environment.** The build environment (Codespace, MCP servers, background agents) is configured so customer financial data is **not exposed to third-party tool calls or uncontrolled web egress.** The existing **`guard-web-access.sh` egress backstop** is the asset to point at: it is the in-loop control that blocks the workbench from sending data to unapproved destinations.

> **Why this is a sales asset, not just hygiene:** working *only* from scoped, deletable exports — and returning a signed deletion attestation — is a confidentiality story the in-tool incumbents structurally cannot tell (§3b). It pre-answers the buyer's security review in the first meeting, turning what would have been a deal-killing gap into a reason to choose Closebook. (See Risk #11 for the residual exposure and the lawyer/E&O conversation it implies.)

## 10. Track B — the ambitious variant, and the gates that unlock it

**The panel ruled, 5/5: Closebook must carry an explicit, gated Track B.** v5 was correctly sized as the *default* but over-corrected into "solo permanently" — a conclusion the critic loop was entitled to *propose* but not to *impose* over Matt's explicit "no constraints on ambition (partners/funding/team allowed if value justifies)." The decisive insight, raised independently by four of five seats: **the binding constraint is delivery weeks, not demand, positioning, or liability** — and that is exactly the constraint a second human absorbs without reintroducing any killed risk.

**How the two constraints reconcile.** Matt asked for both "no ambition ceiling" and "simple to understand." They are not in tension when **ambition is a set of named, gated, opt-in levers stacked on top of the simple honest default — not baked into the base-case math.** Track A (§1–§9) runs by default and stays fully honest. Track B is what Matt *deliberately triggers* when a named gate is met. **The master gate for ALL of Track B is the measured build-hour reduction v6 already commits to instrumenting (§8)** — so no new measurement apparatus is needed; the trigger for "go ambitious" is the same data the base plan already produces. Levers are sequenced cheapest-to-test first.

**Lever 1 — an origination / referral partner who sells while Matt delivers (Year 1, near-zero cost — test first).**
Formalize a fractional-CFO / outsourced-controller firm (already named in Risk #6) as a **revenue-share origination partner.** Selling then stops competing with Matt's scarce delivery weeks, and the single-channel sourcing concentration is de-risked at the same time. **No headcount on Matt's books, no liability change.** *This is the lowest-cost ambition lever — pull it first.*
**Gate:** none beyond a willing partner — it's a Year-1 experiment that also serves Track A's pipeline.
**Survives all four prior critiques:** it adds no general engine, no Year-1 steady-state-math error, no resurrected subscription, no trigger change.

**Lever 2 — a second delivery person: a builder, NOT a signer (Year 2, after build-hours proven).**
An apprentice analyst / contract senior-controller does mapping + bespoke script-build **under Matt's review.** This attacks the *actual* ceiling (delivery weeks) and roughly doubles delivery capacity. **The liability posture is untouched — the buyer's reviewer still signs; the second person attests nothing and is a builder, not a signer**, so no CPA-credential dependency is introduced. It doubles as the Risk #13 continuity mitigation: *"the moat is Matt's documented method + a small bench,"* not Matt alone.
**Gate:** **≥4 full-price closes delivered AND measured build-hour reduction AND a 6-month cash buffer.** Funded from Year-2 anchor margin, not speculative capital.
**Survives all four prior critiques:** still bespoke (not a general engine); a Year-2+ move (no steady-state-math-in-Year-1 error); doesn't resurrect the subscription; doesn't change the trigger.

**Lever 3 — productize the governance IP for the developer / compliance buyer (Year 2+, hardest gate — where "funding allowed if value justifies" actually applies).**
This honors the research synthesis's #1 finding (S1: the binding in-loop tribunal is genuine white space; T1 was the top territory; the recommended posture was *"lead with finance, productize the governance IP as the IP the consulting sells"*). v5 kept the proving ground (the finance wedge) and quietly cancelled the thing being proved (the IP) — a strategic under-allocation. Lever 3 re-grafts the **Verdict gateway-API** as a separable product for the **developer/compliance buyer** (a different buyer from the finance retainer base). **The Aegis/Leash evidence-pack micro-line for its *original* non-finance buyer (5–200-person insurance-renewal/questionnaire owner) is the lighter sibling here** — gated separately on verifying the insurance/E&O trigger against the primary ISO filing (Risk #8); not counted as revenue until verified.
**Gate (the hardest, and the Security seat supplies the binding clause):** **≥3 recurring finance-judgment decision categories route cleanly through `thing-decide.py`** AND **a design partner's reviewer confirms the lineage-card-with-verdict is decision-useful** AND **the two unshipped assurance layers — multi-tenant isolation + tamper-evident logging — are real and independently pen-tested.** Those two layers are the only things between the solo service and a defensible product, and the things a solo founder is least equipped to harden alone — so they are the precondition for any team/funding here.
**Why funding fits *here* and not the service:** capital buys **speed-to-market in a genuine ~12–18-month race** for the governance white space; funding the *service* just buys back Matt's calendar, which the pattern library already does more cheaply. **If Matt ever takes outside money, this is the line item it funds.**

> **Track B does not change Track A's numbers.** Every figure in §7–§8 is Track-A-only. Track B is a series of cheap call-options layered on top — Matt learns whether ambition pays before committing scarce capital or headcount, rather than betting the runway on a pivot.

## 11. The 3 questions only Matt can answer

1. **Does Matt actually want Track B, or is the honest solo ceiling the life he's choosing?** The panel can prove the ceiling is *removable* without reintroducing a killed risk — but "removable" is not "should be removed." A ~$130–190k Year-2 solo lifestyle business with no team to manage, no payroll, no partner to answer to is a legitimate, deliberate endpoint. Adding a second person, an origination partner, or outside capital changes the *kind* of business and the *kind of life* it is. This is a genuine values call about how Matt wants to spend his time, not a question the math decides.

2. **What is the real Year-1 runway figure ([N] months, household burn [B], income held [H], required draw [D]), and is Matt willing to commit it?** The plan specifies the *arithmetic* and the GO/NO-GO gate (§7a), but only Matt knows his actual numbers and his family's appetite for an income dip during the build year. **The plan does not start without this**, and only Matt can supply it.

3. **Is Matt willing to personally underwrite the liability surfaces the ambitious path requires — the data-confidentiality/DPA exposure now (Risk #11), and the E&O posture if Lever 3 ever ships an attestation or a multi-tenant product holding others' financial data?** The single-signer rule keeps Matt off the *numbers* liability, but holding customer GL exports and any Track-B productization create real legal exposure that needs a lawyer conversation and possibly E&O coverage — a cost and risk-appetite decision the panel cannot make for him.
