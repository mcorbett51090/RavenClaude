# Staff Wages as a Percent of Collections Is the Overhead Dial

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Practice overhead / staffing economics
**Applies to:** `dental-practice`

---

## Why this exists

Staff wages (clinical and administrative, excluding doctor compensation) are the largest single overhead line in a dental practice — typically 25–30% of collections. [unverified — training knowledge] Because wages are both the largest cost and the most operationally sensitive (changes affect patient care delivery immediately), they are the primary dial to turn when overhead is out of range. However, they are also the line most often cut without first asking whether the practice is overstaffed relative to patient volume or understaffed and suppressing production. Cutting wages without diagnosing the driver — volume, productivity, or redundancy — creates a clinical and retention problem that is more expensive than the cost saved.

## How to apply

Before making any staffing decision driven by overhead, measure wages as a percentage of collections and compare to benchmark.

```
Staff wage health check:
Metric: Total staff wages (excluding doctor pay and benefits) ÷ Collections × 100

National benchmarks [unverified — verify against ADA or MGMA current data]:
  High performers:  22–25% of collections
  National median:  25–28% of collections
  Concern:          >30% of collections

Diagnosis before action:
If wages are >28%:
  Step 1: Is collections low while staff count is appropriate?
    → Collections problem, not a staffing problem — address production and collections first
  Step 2: Is staff count higher than volume supports?
    → Diagnose: patient visits per staff FTE, chair-hours per assistant FTE
    → Benchmark: typically 1 assistant per 2 chairs, 1 front desk per $500k–$600k production
       [unverified — varies by practice model]
  Step 3: Is wages/hour above market for the local labor pool?
    → Compare hourly rates to regional dental wage surveys before cutting staff

Action ladder (lowest blast radius first):
  1. Reduce overtime before FTE reductions
  2. Adjust hours to align with schedule utilization
  3. Natural attrition for non-critical roles
  4. FTE reduction only after steps 1–3 are exhausted and collections root cause is ruled out
```

**Do:**
- Report staff wages as a percentage of collections on the monthly overhead dashboard.
- Check whether a high wages-to-collections ratio is driven by low collections before reducing staff — cutting a $50k assistant to save 8% overhead while the real problem is a 92% collection ratio is the wrong surgery.
- Run the staff-to-chair and staff-to-production ratios alongside the wages percentage to confirm over- or understaffing.

**Don't:**
- Use a dollar budget for wages without also tracking the percentage-of-collections metric — a flat wage budget in a growing practice produces an artificially low metric, and vice versa.
- Include doctor compensation (owner or associate) in the staff wages line for overhead benchmarking — it belongs in a separate compensation line.
- Cut clinical staff hours during peak schedule periods — the production loss typically exceeds the wage savings.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Start-up practices and practices onboarding a new associate may intentionally run above 30% for 6–12 months while production ramps. Flag the period and set a ramp-to-benchmark timeline.
