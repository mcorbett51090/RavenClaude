# Treatment Plan Sequencing Is the Hidden Case Acceptance Driver

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Case acceptance / treatment planning
**Applies to:** `dental-practice`

---

## Why this exists

When a patient receives a treatment plan with a large total fee presented all at once, the psychological weight of the full number — not the value of each individual procedure — drives the decision to delay or decline. Sequencing the plan into logical phases (urgent/foundational → restorative → elective/cosmetic) with a fee presented per phase, not in total, substantially improves acceptance rates for complex multi-procedure plans. The patient's "yes" decision becomes about a manageable first phase, not a commitment to the entire plan. Practices that present all restorative, cosmetic, and maintenance treatment simultaneously as a single number leave significant accepted-work on the table.

## How to apply

Establish a default sequencing protocol for complex treatment plans:

```
Treatment plan sequencing framework:
Phase 1 — Urgent / foundational (present first, schedule immediately)
  - Active infection, pain, emergency extractions
  - Periodontal treatment if active disease
  - Large caries / broken teeth affecting function

Phase 2 — Restorative (present after Phase 1 is accepted and scheduled)
  - Crown and bridge work on structurally compromised teeth
  - Implant placement (after healing from Phase 1 if applicable)
  - Multi-surface direct restorations

Phase 3 — Elective / cosmetic / maintenance (present after Phase 2)
  - Whitening, veneers, cosmetic enhancement
  - Orthodontic referral if applicable
  - Elective implants replacing asymptomatic missing teeth

Presentation rules:
  - Quote Phase 1 fee only at the first presentation
  - Revisit Phase 2 at the Phase 1 completion appointment, not before
  - Use insurance breakdown by phase, not in total
  - Document: what was presented, patient response, follow-up date
```

**Do:**
- Train clinical staff and the front desk on the sequencing framework so the patient receives a consistent story from the hygienist, dentist, and checkout.
- Build the treatment plan presentation script around the patient's own stated concern first — connect Phase 1 to their reason for the visit.
- Offer a written summary with the Phase 1 scope and fee for patients who need to discuss with a spouse or review finances.

**Don't:**
- Present a comprehensive "everything wrong" list without sequencing — it maximizes sticker shock and minimizes acceptance.
- Discount Phase 1 treatment to close the case; discounting signals that the original price was inflated, not that care is genuinely needed.
- Skip the phase sequencing for "simple" 2-3 procedure plans — even a $2,800 crown and two-surface composite plan benefits from leading with the clinical reason, not the fee.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Patients with dental benefits expiring at year-end who want to complete as much treatment as possible may want the full plan sequenced and scheduled aggressively — in that case, present the full sequence with the insurance benefit application mapped to each phase.
