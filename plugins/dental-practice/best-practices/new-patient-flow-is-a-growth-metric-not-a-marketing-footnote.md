# New Patient Flow Is a Growth Metric, Not a Marketing Footnote

**Status:** Primary diagnostic
**Domain:** Practice growth / patient acquisition
**Applies to:** `dental-practice`

---

## Why this exists

New patient volume sets the long-run trajectory of a dental practice. A practice running below its replacement threshold — typically 20–30 new patients per doctor per month — is slowly attriting its active-patient base even if current production looks healthy. [unverified — training knowledge] The gap between leaving patients and incoming new patients is a lagging indicator: production holds for 12–18 months while the active-patient count quietly erodes, and by the time production drops, the pipeline is already hollow. Treating new patients as a marketing metric rather than an operational one means the signal arrives too late.

## How to apply

Track new patient volume as a monthly operational metric alongside production and collections — not in the marketing report.

```
New patient health check:
- Target: 20–30 new patients per active doctor per month (benchmark; verify vs. your segment)
- Active patient definition: seen in the past 18 months
- Track monthly: new patients seen (not scheduled — seen)
- Track quarterly: active patient headcount trend (up / flat / down)
- Track annually: new patient source breakdown (referral, web, paid, recall)

Red flags:
  - New patients < 15/doctor/month for 3+ consecutive months
  - Active patient count declining YoY despite stable production
  - New patient volume flat while paid-advertising spend increasing (cost per new patient rising)
```

**Do:**
- Report new-patient count on the same scorecard as production per hour and collection ratio.
- Segment new patients by source monthly — referral-driven practices have different risks than paid-acquisition-driven ones.
- Set a replacement threshold alert: if new patients fall below attrition for 2 consecutive months, escalate to the practice owner.

**Don't:**
- Conflate "new patient calls" or "appointments scheduled" with "new patients seen" — no-show and cancellation rates distort the scheduled number.
- Treat a temporary new-patient surge from a promotion as a structural improvement; track the 12-month rolling average.
- Ignore the active-patient count as a lagging validator of the new-patient trend.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Practices intentionally capping new patients (associate onboarding, limiting capacity) are operating to a deliberate ceiling — the metric still applies but the target changes. Fee-for-service practices in saturated urban markets may run sustainably at lower new-patient counts with higher ACT per patient.
