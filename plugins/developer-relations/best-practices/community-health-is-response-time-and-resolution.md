# Community health is response time and resolution, not member count

**Status:** Strong default
**Domain:** Developer community
**Applies to:** `developer-relations`

---

## Why this exists

A 5,000-member forum where questions sit unanswered for a week is unhealthier than a 200-person one
that answers in an hour. Member count is a vanity metric — it only goes up and never forces a
decision. Worse, an owned community you can't staff *advertises neglect*: a developer who posts and
hears nothing concludes the product is abandoned. Health is measured by how fast and how well the
community answers.

## How to apply

**The health metric set:**
- **Median first-response time** — how long until a human responds.
- **Resolution rate** — share of questions that reach a resolved answer.
- **Unanswered-question rate** — the leak; the number that should keep you up at night.
- **Member-answered rate** — questions resolved by *members*, not staff (the advocacy signal).

**Do:**
- Set a response-time + resolution SLA before opening an owned channel.
- Sponsor an existing community (Stack Overflow tag, an established Discord) if you can't staff a fast SLA.
- Spotlight and elevate the members who already answer — build the contributor ladder.

**Don't:**
- Headline community *size* in a report.
- Open an owned Discord/forum you can't staff to its SLA.
- Treat a large, dead channel as an asset — it's a liability.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- A **brand-new** community legitimately tracks growth for a bootstrapping window — but pair it with a
  response-time commitment from day one, and switch the headline to health as soon as there's traffic.
- A **read-only / announcement** channel isn't a Q&A community and isn't judged by resolution — judge
  it by whether the right developers see the announcements.
