# Teach, don't market at developers

**Status:** Strong default
**Domain:** Developer content & advocacy
**Applies to:** `developer-relations`

---

## Why this exists

Developers detect a sales pitch instantly and disengage. "Revolutionary," "best-in-class," "leverage
our synergies," and "game-changing" are read as noise at best and dishonesty at worst. The unit of
DevRel that works is a thing that helps a developer *do their job today* — a runnable example, a clear
concept, an honest comparison. Trust is the growth strategy, and trust is earned by teaching.

## How to apply

**The competitor-docs test:** would this sentence survive, unchanged, in a competitor's
documentation? If yes, it's teaching (transferable, honest). If it only works in *your* docs because
it's an ad for your features, cut or rewrite it.

**Do:**
- Lead with the developer's problem and a transferable lesson; let the product appear as the *example*.
- Make honest comparisons — say where a competitor is genuinely better (developers verify; honesty earns trust).
- Show the rough edges; the source that admits limitations is the one developers cite.

**Don't:**
- Use marketing superlatives in a getting-started or advocacy artifact (the hook flags
  "revolutionary / best-in-class / synergy / game-changer").
- Write a "tutorial" whose real purpose is a feature tour.
- Hide a known limitation a developer will hit in five minutes anyway.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- A **launch announcement** legitimately describes what's new and why it's exciting — but it still
  pairs the claim with the runnable path to use it, and it avoids empty superlatives.
- **Buyer-facing** marketing (to non-developers) is `marketing-operations`' domain and plays by
  different rules; this rule governs *developer-facing* artifacts.
