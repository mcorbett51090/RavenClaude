# Invest at the weakest funnel stage, not the loudest channel

**Stance:** Locate the **weakest stage** of the activation funnel by its leading
indicator and invest there — don't pour effort into more awareness when the real leak
is activation.

## Why

The most common DevRel misallocation is doing *more awareness* — more posts, more
talks, more social — when the funnel's actual leak is *activation*: developers arrive
but never reach first success. Adding awareness spend on top of a broken
getting-started step just fills a leaky bucket faster, and it *feels* productive
because output goes up while the retained-developer count doesn't move. The discipline
is to read the activation funnel ([`../knowledge/developer-experience-playbook.md`](../knowledge/developer-experience-playbook.md))
— signup → first call → first app → retained — find the lowest-converting step, and
put the next unit of effort *there*, even when it's the less glamorous work (auditing
a quickstart rarely feels as good as landing a keynote).

## In practice

- Diagnose the **weakest stage by its leading indicator** (quickstart completion rate,
  time-to-first-success, first-response time, return rate) *before* recommending work.
- Prefer the **cheapest fix at the leak** — e.g. a `getting-started-audit` when
  activation is the bottleneck — over launching a net-new content initiative.
- Re-locate the leak each planning cycle; it moves as you fix it.
- A genuinely zero-awareness new product is the exception — but confirm activation
  works on the developers you *do* get before scaling reach.

## Smell

A quarterly plan that answers every funnel problem with "do more content," a content
calendar that's all top-of-funnel while retention is flat, and effort flowing to the
stage that's easiest to produce output for rather than the one that's actually broken.
