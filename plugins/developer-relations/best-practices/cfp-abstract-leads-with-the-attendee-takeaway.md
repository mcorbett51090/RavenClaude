# A CFP abstract leads with the attendee takeaway

**Status:** Strong default
**Domain:** Conference talks / CFP
**Applies to:** `developer-relations`

---

## Why this exists

Program committees reject product pitches, and attendees tune them out. A CFP abstract that opens with
the speaker's title or the product's name is answering the wrong question. The reviewer and the
attendee both buy one thing: **what will I be able to do after this talk?** Lead with that, and the
talk gets accepted *and* lands.

## How to apply

**The first-sentence test:** if the abstract's first sentence is about you or your product, rewrite it
to be about what the attendee will be able to do.

**Do:**
- Open with the attendee takeaway ("you'll leave able to diagnose and fix cache stampedes").
- State 2–4 concrete takeaways — each an *action* the attendee gains ("build/diagnose/avoid X"), not
  "understand X."
- Match the track and level explicitly; write reviewer-fit notes.
- Use the product as the *example* the lessons came from, not the subject of the talk.

**Don't:**
- Lead with "At <Company>, we built…" — that's the example, not the hook.
- Use "learn about" / "an overview of" as a takeaway (too vague to accept).
- Over-scope the slot; reviewers reject talks that promise more than 30 minutes can deliver.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- A **keynote** or **company-track** slot at a vendor's own conference has different norms — there a
  product narrative is expected. This rule governs *general* CFP submissions to community conferences.
- A **lightning talk** compresses to a single takeaway — still lead with it, just one.
