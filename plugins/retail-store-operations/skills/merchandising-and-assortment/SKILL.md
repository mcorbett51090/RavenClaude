# Merchandising and Assortment

## Shelf space is finite capital
Measure space productivity — sales and margin per facing / per linear foot — by SKU and by category. A SKU that doesn't earn its space is borrowing it from one that would. Allocate facings by productivity and role (traffic-driver vs. margin-driver), not by vendor pressure or SKU count.

## Rationalize the assortment to the customer's decision
Cut the duplicative tail (it splits demand and starves the winners), protect the destination items, fill the real gaps. More SKUs is not more sales — it's more complexity. Name the sales-at-risk on every cut and the inventory-clearance handoff for the cut SKUs.

## Markdown is a decision, not a default
Trigger markdowns on sell-through and weeks-of-supply, not the calendar alone. The first markdown is the cheapest — depth compounds with time. Set the first-markdown trigger and depth, the step-down schedule, and the terminal-clearance point. Before discounting, check whether slow sell-through is an assortment problem (wrong SKU), not a price problem.

## Price is a ladder
Keep the opening / mid / premium price points legible; the markdown sits inside that ladder. State the elasticity assumption on any price move and route the demand/elasticity model to `applied-statistics`.

## The planogram is where strategy meets the floor
Space follows productivity and role. Hand floor execution of the planogram/markdown to `store-operations-lead`.

## Output
An assortment / planogram / markdown plan: space productivity per SKU or category, the cut/keep/add decision with sales-at-risk, the sell-through-triggered markdown cadence, and the price ladder — each tied to margin. Hand clearance of cut/overstocked SKUs to `inventory-and-replenishment-planner`.
