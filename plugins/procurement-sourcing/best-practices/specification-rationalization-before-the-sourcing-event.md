# Specification Rationalization Before the Sourcing Event

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Procurement & sourcing / demand management
**Applies to:** `procurement-sourcing`

---

## Why this exists

The most durable savings in procurement come before the RFx is published — from eliminating or simplifying the specification, not from negotiating harder against it. Over-specification is endemic: IT buys laptops to a performance tier that the users' actual workload does not justify; facilities buys a premium cleaning product when the standard grade meets the hygiene standard; engineering specifies a tolerance tighter than the process requires. Each over-spec locks the category into a narrow supply base, limits competition, and floors the price. A 10% specification rationalization often delivers more savings than a competitive bid event run on the original spec — and it is permanent, not renegotiable at the next renewal.

## How to apply

Run specification rationalization as a structured pre-sourcing step, not an afterthought:

**Four-question rationalization checklist (one per SKU/line):**

| Question | Answer | Action if "Yes / No" |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Does the specification map to a documented business requirement? | Y / N | No → flag for challenge |
| 2. Can a standard-grade alternative meet the functional requirement? | Y / N | Yes → test alternative |
| 3. Is the quantity / consumption rate justified by actual usage data? | Y / N | No → reduce quantity |
| 4. Is the specification unique to this organization, or is it industry-standard? | Unique / Standard | Unique → challenge source |

**Rationalization opportunity matrix:**

| Category | Common over-spec | Savings type |
|---|---|---|
| IT hardware | Performance tier above workload need | Grade-down → 15–30% cost reduction |
| MRO / facilities | Brand-specific where equivalent exists | Brand consolidation → 10–25% |
| Professional services | Seniority level above task need | Staff-mix change → 15–40% rate reduction |
| Logistics | Expedite / premium service for non-urgent freight | Service-level right-sizing → 20–50% |
| Packaging materials | Custom dimension / finish above functional need | Standardization → 10–20% |

**Do:**
- Involve the internal customer (engineering, IT, facilities) in the rationalization conversation before going to market — a category manager who changes the spec without end-user buy-in will face maverick spend after the contract is signed.
- Document the functional requirement separately from the specification — the spec is one way to meet the requirement, not the only way.
- Pilot alternatives before the RFx on high-spend lines: a tested equivalent is more persuasive to stakeholders than a theoretical one.
- Track the savings from rationalization separately from negotiation savings in the savings waterfall — they are a different and often more durable intervention.

**Don't:**
- Rationalize specifications in safety-critical, regulated, or quality-system-controlled categories without formal engineering or compliance sign-off — the cost of a recall or regulatory violation far exceeds the procurement savings.
- Use specification rationalization as a way to exclude incumbent suppliers from competition — the goal is a better spec for the business, not a sourcing lever dressed as demand management.
- Conflate specification rationalization with value engineering — this is about removing over-specification, not redesigning the product.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Categories under active quality management systems (ISO, FDA, cGMP) may have specifications that are regulatory requirements, not preferences — these are not candidates for rationalization without regulatory review. Commodity categories where the market sets the standard (steel grades, chemical purity levels) do not have meaningful over-spec — the market grades are the spec.
