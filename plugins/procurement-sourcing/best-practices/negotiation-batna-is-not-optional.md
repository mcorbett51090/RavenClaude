# Know Your BATNA Before Entering Any Negotiation

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Sourcing / negotiation
**Applies to:** `procurement-sourcing`

---

## Why this exists

A negotiator who does not know their Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement (BATNA) has no walk-away point and therefore no real leverage. Without a BATNA, every concession the supplier refuses is a test of willingness rather than a test of alternatives — and suppliers who probe for this quickly learn to hold the line on price and terms, knowing the buyer will eventually capitulate. The BATNA is not a bluff; it is a credible alternative that you have actually prepared. The preparation discipline (identifying, developing, and pricing the alternative) is what makes the negotiation leverage real.

## How to apply

Prepare the BATNA before the first negotiation session, not during it.

```
BATNA Preparation — Required Steps
────────────────────────────────────────
Step 1 — IDENTIFY ALTERNATIVES
  For this category and this supplier, what are the realistic alternatives?
  a) Alternative supplier (named, qualified, and with a price indication)
  b) Specification change that reduces the scope or substitutes the input
  c) Delay or reduce volume (demand-management play per §3 #7)
  d) Insource (feasible? cost estimate?)
  e) Do without / defer

Step 2 — DEVELOP AT LEAST ONE ALTERNATIVE
  A BATNA is only real if you have actually done the work to make it executable:
  - Alternative supplier: get a quote (or a rough ROM) — an untested
    alternative is a bluff, not a BATNA.
  - Specification change: get stakeholder sign-off that the alternative is acceptable.
  - Insource: get a credible internal cost estimate.

Step 3 — PRICE THE BATNA
  What is the total cost of switching to the BATNA, including:
  - Transition cost / switching cost
  - Quality/risk delta vs. incumbent
  - Timeline risk
  This gives the walk-away price: the point below which the BATNA is preferable.

Step 4 — SET THE WALK-AWAY POINT
  Walk-away = BATNA total cost + acceptable risk premium.
  This is the number you do not disclose, but below which you walk.
```

**Do:**
- Complete Step 2 before entering negotiation — an unexecuted alternative is not a BATNA, it is a threat.
- Brief your executive sponsor on the BATNA and the walk-away before the negotiation, not during it.
- Reassess the BATNA if the negotiation runs over multiple sessions; market conditions and alternatives change.

**Don't:**
- Disclose your BATNA or walk-away point in the negotiation — the supplier will price up to it.
- Enter a negotiation with a single supplier and no BATNA, especially for a strategic category — this is the lowest-leverage position available.
- Confuse the target price (what you want) with the walk-away price (the BATNA-floor) — they are different numbers.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- **Sole-source monopoly** where no alternative genuinely exists — the BATNA is the do-without or delay scenario; even a weak BATNA disciplines the negotiation by framing the cost of no deal.
- **Emergency sourcing** where time constraints eliminate alternatives — document the emergency, waive the BATNA prep, and accept that leverage is absent; at minimum, document what a future BATNA would look like to frame the next renewal.
