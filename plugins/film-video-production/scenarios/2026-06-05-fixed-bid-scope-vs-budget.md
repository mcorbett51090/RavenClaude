## Problem

A production company won a commercial on a **fixed bid**. Through pre-production the client kept adding: a second location, two more hero product shots, a social cut-down "while you're at it." The producer felt the budget tightening but didn't have a clean way to say "that's out of scope" — and was on track to deliver the extras out of the contingency and the margin, silently.

## Context

- Segment: commercial, fixed-bid (not cost-plus), agency client, ~$280k [ESTIMATE] all-in.
- Constraint: the bid was built to a top-sheet (good — section 3 #1), but the **deliverables spec was thin** — "a :30 and social cut-downs" with no count, no aspect-ratio list, no revision-round cap (the section 3 #6 gap). With the product undefined, every "small ask" looked free.
- The contingency was a real 10%-of-BTL+post line [verify-at-use — industry convention], but it was sized for production risk (weather, overtime), not for absorbing scope growth.

## Attempts

- Tried: separated the two things the producer had been conflating — *contingency* (a reserve for the agreed scope's risk) vs *scope growth* (new work). The added location and the two hero shots were not risk; they were a bigger job. Spending contingency on them would leave the agreed scope unprotected. Outcome: reframed the asks as change orders, not contingency draws (section 3 #4 — contingency is managed, not raided).
- Tried: priced each add against the top-sheet. The second location was a company move + a half-day of crew at loaded cost (overtime exposure + fringe), not "just a location fee"; the social cut-downs were post + delivery-spec work (versioning, captions, QC), not free exports. Defining the delivery spec first (section 3 #6) made each add a line item with a number. Outcome: a defensible change-order figure per ask.
- Tried (the move that worked): locked a **deliverables spec** (exact deliverable count, aspect ratios, master formats, captioning, and a revision-round cap) and attached it to the bid as the scope baseline, so anything beyond it was an explicit change order with its own number — surfaced to the client at request time, not discovered at wrap.

## Resolution

The squeeze was a **scope-vs-budget** problem rooted in an undefined product (section 3 #6), made worse by treating contingency as a slush fund for new work (section 3 #4). Defining the deliverables spec turned "small asks" into priced change orders, and protected both the contingency and the margin.

**Action for the next producer hitting this pattern:** on a fixed bid, the deliverables spec **is** the scope boundary — define it first and in detail (counts, ratios, masters, captions, revision-round cap), because anything you can't point to on a spec is a change order you're about to absorb. Never fund scope growth from the contingency — the contingency protects the *agreed* scope's risk; new work gets a new number the client sees at request time.
