# Above-the-line and below-the-line are a control boundary, not just a budget format

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Film & video production / budgeting / production management
**Applies to:** `film-video-production`

---

## Why this exists

Above-the-line (ATL) costs — creative talent, rights, and producer fees — are largely locked before production begins and driven by deal memos and negotiated agreements. Below-the-line (BTL) costs — crew, equipment, locations, lab, and post — are managed daily and are the primary variable expense pool. Conflating the two in budget analysis leads to a critical mistake: treating an ATL overage (a writer fee escalation, a star's travel rider) as if it can be managed by BTL cuts, or treating a BTL overrun as a negotiated problem when it is an operational one. Each side has a different decision-maker, a different approval path, and a different leakage mechanism.

## How to apply

Maintain ATL and BTL as separate control accounts throughout production:

```
Budget control summary — [Project] — [Date] — Cost report #____

ABOVE THE LINE
  Account | Description          | Budget   | Actual/Est | Variance | Status
  1000    | Story/Rights         | $___     | $___       | $___     | ____
  1100    | Producer fees        | $___     | $___       | $___     | ____
  1200    | Director             | $___     | $___       | $___     | ____
  1300    | Cast                 | $___     | $___       | $___     | ____
  ATL TOTAL                      | $___     | $___       | $___

BELOW THE LINE
  Account | Description          | Budget   | Actual/Est | Variance | Status
  2000    | Production staff     | $___     | $___       | $___     | ____
  2100    | Camera               | $___     | $___       | $___     | ____
  2200    | Locations            | $___     | $___       | $___     | ____
  2300    | Post production      | $___     | $___       | $___     | ____
  BTL TOTAL                      | $___     | $___       | $___

CONTINGENCY                      | $___     | $___       | $___
GRAND TOTAL                      | $___     | $___       | $___
```

When reporting variance, always separate ATL and BTL totals. An ATL overage routes to the executive producer / studio; a BTL overage routes to the line producer for day-level operational response.

**Do:**
- Lock ATL costs from deal memos before finalizing the BTL budget — ATL creep is the most common source of budget re-scoping mid-production.
- Route all ATL change conversations to the executive producer or department head who holds the deal; do not try to offset ATL overages with BTL cuts without explicit approval.
- Report BTL variance weekly on the cost report with a line-by-line cause; lump-sum BTL variance is not actionable.

**Don't:**
- Combine ATL and BTL totals in a summary that hides the split — the split is the first thing a financier, studio, or completion bond company will ask for.
- Treat contingency as a BTL sub-account — it is a top-level reserve accessed across the whole budget, not a BTL cushion.
- Build an ATL estimate from market averages without a deal-memo basis; ATL cost is negotiated, not averaged.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- Ultra-low-budget micro-productions where a single producer performs multiple ATL and BTL functions may collapse the distinction for practical purposes, though the cost tracking should still separate negotiated fees from operational costs.
