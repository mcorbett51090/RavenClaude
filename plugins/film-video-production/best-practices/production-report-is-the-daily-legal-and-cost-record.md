# The daily production report is the legal and cost record — issue it every shoot day

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Film & video production / production management / cost reporting
**Applies to:** `film-video-production`

---

## Why this exists

The daily production report (DPR) is the contemporaneous record of everything that happened on a shoot day: scenes shot, pages completed, cast and crew called and worked, overtime incurred, incidents, and production notes. It is the document a completion bond company, an E&O insurer, or a payroll auditor reaches for first when they need to verify what happened on any given day. A production that issues DPRs inconsistently — or reconstructs them from memory at wrap — has no legally contemporaneous record. A reconstruction is not the same as a same-day DPR, and a sophisticated dispute partner (a bond company, an investor, or a crew-member claiming undocumented overtime) will know the difference.

## How to apply

Issue the DPR before the wrap of each shoot day, or at minimum within 24 hours:

```
Daily Production Report — [Project] — Day __ of __ — [Date]

SCHEDULE
  Scheduled scenes:  ____     Actual scenes completed: ____
  Scheduled pages:   ____     Actual pages completed:  ____
  Previous total pages:        ____
  Total pages to date:         ____

CREW CALL / WRAP
  General crew call:  ____    Last crew out:  ____
  Meal 1:             ____    Meal 2:  ____
  Camera roll:        ____    Camera wrap:  ____

CAST (per actor)
  Actor | Character | Call | Makeup | Set | Dismissed | OT reason
  _____ | _________ | ____ | ______ | ___ | _________ | _________

OVERTIME / VIOLATIONS
  Type | Persons affected | Amount | Authorized by
  ____ | ________________ | ______ | _____________

INCIDENTS / DELAYS
  Time | Description | Cost impact estimate
  ____ | ___________ | ____________________

NOTES (director, DP, 1st AD)

ISSUED BY:  ____  DATE/TIME:  ____
```

**Do:**
- Issue the DPR on the day — "we'll catch up" DPR backlogs become a compliance problem if a crew injury, an equipment claim, or an overtime dispute surfaces later.
- Include every delay and its stated cause; the delay log is the evidence base for a force-majeure clause, a weather-day claim, or a schedule-extension justification.
- Archive every DPR in the production office and supply copies to the production accountant — the DPR drives the cost-report update.

**Don't:**
- Omit overtime incidents because they are embarrassing; undocumented OT is a payroll audit risk.
- Use the DPR as a place to narrate or editorialize about personnel — it is a legal record; factual descriptions only.
- Reconstruct DPRs at the end of production; a reconstruction cannot be contemporaneous and is a different document.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- Purely documentary productions shooting over extended periods with a very small crew may use a simplified field log; the core fields (scenes covered, call/wrap, incidents, issues) still apply.
- Archival re-shoot days added to a completed production should be documented with a DPR keyed to the re-shoot date, not the original production schedule.
