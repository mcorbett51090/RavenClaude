# Location agreements must be signed before the scout — not before the shoot

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Film & video production / locations / legal / risk
**Applies to:** `film-video-production`

---

## Why this exists

A location scout without a signed location agreement (or at minimum a signed location release for the scout visit) creates a liability exposure from the moment the crew steps on the property. More practically: a production that schedules a shoot day around a location before the agreement is signed is negotiating from the weakest possible position — the schedule already depends on the location, which the location owner knows. Last-minute location changes after a schedule is locked add a day rate in company move costs and crew overtime. The location agreement also governs what the production is permitted to do on the premises, which the art department and the line producer need to know before any practical decisions are made.

## How to apply

Treat location agreements as a pre-production gate, not a paperwork trail:

**Before the scout:**
- Obtain a signed location-scout release (a simplified one-day agreement) that covers the scout visit, photography of the premises, and a non-disclosure provision.

**Before the location appears in the schedule:**
- Execute the full location agreement covering:
  - Permitted dates and hours
  - Permitted activities (construction, smoke, wet work, driving on grounds, etc.)
  - Certificate of insurance naming the property owner as additional insured
  - Damage deposit and damage liability cap
  - Restoration requirements (return to original condition with a photographic baseline)
  - Holdover / extension rate if the shoot day runs long
  - Exclusion zones (rooms, areas the production cannot access)
  - Crew access hours (separate from shoot hours)
  - Signatory authority confirmation (who is authorized to sign for the property owner)

**Do:**
- Photograph the location in its pre-production state before the art department touches it — this is the restoration baseline and the damage-dispute record.
- Confirm the signatory has authority to bind the property owner; a location agreement signed by a property manager without authority may be unenforceable.
- Include the holdover rate in the budget — overages at a location with a defined holdover rate are a known-cost overage, not a surprise.

**Don't:**
- Begin any art department prep work (set dressing, painting, rigging) before the location agreement is signed, even if the verbal is solid.
- Schedule the shoot day in the production report until the agreement is signed — a verbal confirmation is not a confirmed location.
- Release the location deposit before the restoration walkthrough is complete and signed off.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- Studio stages under a master rental agreement may be governed by that agreement for individual shoot days; confirm whether the master agreement requires a per-project rider.
- Public locations (streets, parks, government property) are governed by filming permits issued by the relevant authority, not private location agreements; the permit is the functional equivalent and must be secured before the scout for the same reasons.
