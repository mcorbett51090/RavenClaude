# Budget music clearance as a hard cost — it is not optional and it will not be free

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Film & video production / post / music / clearance
**Applies to:** `film-video-production`

---

## Why this exists

Music clearance — securing sync and master use licenses for every copyrighted piece of music in the final cut — is the single most common source of unexpected post-production cost on productions that use existing music. Editors who cut to their favorite tracks create a "temp love" problem: the director and client approve the cut against music the production cannot afford to license or cannot clear in time for delivery. Re-scoring or re-editing after picture lock is rework that costs time and money. A distributor who discovers an un-cleared music cue after delivery can refuse to accept the deliverable or require the production to recall and re-deliver.

## How to apply

Build music clearance into the budget and the editorial plan from the start:

```
Music clearance plan — [Project] — [Date]

Distribution target:              ____  (affects license scope needed)
Music clearance budget:           $____

For each music cue in the cut:
  Cue # | Title | Artist | Publisher (sync) | Label (master) | Sync fee est. | Master fee est. | Status
  _____ | _____ | ______ | ________________ | ______________ | _____________ | _______________ | ______

Total estimated clearance cost:   $____
Budget confirmed:                 Y/N
Music editor / clearance supervisor: ____
```

Set a "no temp music from commercial recordings" editorial policy at the start of post, or establish a temp-to-perm discipline: any commercially recorded piece cut in must have a documented clearance attempt or a licensed alternative secured before picture lock.

**Do:**
- Use a music supervisor to negotiate fees; unsolicited clearance requests from productions without a supervisor routinely receive double the going rate.
- Budget both the sync license (the publisher, who controls the composition) and the master license (the label, who controls the recording) as separate line items; they are separate negotiations with separate parties.
- Plan for festival-only licenses as a lower-cost first step if theatrical distribution is not yet confirmed; festival rights can be upgraded, but only if the option is included in the original license.

**Don't:**
- Cut final to commercially licensed temp music and then discover the master is unavailable for sync (legacy contracts, estate disputes, label refusal) — this is the most common clearance failure.
- Assume a blanket performing rights organization (PRO) license (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC) covers sync; PRO licenses cover public performance, not synchronization to picture.
- Defer the clearance conversation to the end of post — publishers and labels have their own timelines, and rush clearance fees are significantly higher.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- Projects using exclusively licensed music from a production music library (royalty-free or blanket-licensed) have no per-cue clearance obligation for those tracks; confirm the library license covers the intended distribution.
- Commissioned original scores have a different rights structure (work-for-hire or co-ownership); the clearance rule applies to any commercially recorded source material the composer used as a reference or interpolation.
