The shift

From assembling the close to approving it.

The close today

  • Pull the trial balance, wrangle it into statements by hand or across three tools.
  • Reconcile every account, eyeball each tie-out.
  • Write flux commentary line by line.
  • Chase sign-offs over email; the control lives in a policy doc, not the workflow.
  • Assemble the package. Then start next month.

With the autopilot

  • One command produces statements, reconciliations, and the flux table.
  • Tie-outs auto-pass within materiality; only breaks are flagged.
  • The package is submitted into a sign-off workflow that enforces segregation of duties.
  • You get a single review surface and clear it. Next month is the same command.
  • Instantiate it again for the next entity by adding a config — no rebuild.

The governed cycle

Eight steps. The last three are yours.

Order matters here — each step gates the next, and the machine stops where your judgment begins.

  1. Ingest the trial balance

    A CSV today; a live pull from QuickBooks, NetSuite, Sage Intacct, or Xero on the roadmap.

  2. Validate the mapping

    Every account must map to a statement line. The run blocks on an unmapped account rather than guessing.

  3. Produce the statements

    Income statement, balance sheet, and a clearly-labelled draft cash flow — tested on the subtotals, not just that it balances.

  4. Reconcile & flux

    Book vs sub-ledger tie-outs and period-over-period movement, both filtered to your materiality threshold.

  5. Assemble the package

    A self-contained close package you can open offline or email to a reviewer.

  6. Submit

    The automated cycle ends here. It only submits — it can never approve its own work.

  7. Review & approve

    A different person than the preparer, above the threshold. The workflow refuses a self-approval outright.

  8. Lock

    Sealed with an out-of-band token and written to an append-only, tamper-evident audit log.

The part that actually matters

Controls enforced in code, not written in a policy.

In 2025 an AP automation agent released roughly $92,000 in payments despite documentation saying it couldn't. That is the difference between a control that is documented and one that is enforced. Here the segregation of duties is the code path — the same person cannot prepare and approve a material package. Try it, and the workflow refuses:

$ close_state.py --run-dir ./close approve --actor alice --threshold 100000
REFUSED (SoD): actor 'alice' is the preparer and package amount
500,000.00 >= threshold 100,000.00. A different approver is required.
 
# and the audit trail cannot be quietly rewritten:
$ close_state.py --run-dir ./close verify
FAIL: line 1: hash mismatch (event 'submit' tampered)

Every state change — submit, review, approve, reject, lock — is appended to a hash-chained log. Edit any past line and the chain breaks; verify catches it. That is the audit backbone a controller signs their name against.

Where it sits among the tools you know

Not a bigger BlackLine. A different shape.

BlackLine and Trintech own the SOX controls backbone — and cost six figures and six months to stand up, a poor fit below roughly $250M in revenue. FloQast and Numeric auto-match reconciliations to 90–98% and let you review by exception. Ramp, Vic.ai, and BILL confidence-gate accounts payable. Each is excellent at its one lane.

None of them runs ingest → reconcile → close → statements → controls → locked-and-approved as a single governed cycle for a lean mid-market team. That seam is the product.

And the pitch stays honest: producing statements from a ledger is a commodity — every accounting system already does it. The value is the governed cycle, the enforced controls, and the reusable per-entity mapping — delivered as plugin artifacts you install in minutes.

What it is not

The honest edges.

A controller signs their name to these numbers, so here is exactly where the line is — stated plainly rather than buried.

  • It does not replace the judgment that produces the trial balance.

    Accruals, cutoff, estimates, and the reconciliations that turn a raw GL into a reconciled TB stay yours. It governs and reports the close; it doesn't invent the accounting.

  • Local-tier sign-off is tamper-evident, not tamper-preventing.

    Identity is config-asserted until it's wired to your identity provider. The hash chain detects edits; auditor-grade segregation arrives with the warehouse tier.

  • Statements from a trial balance alone are not audit-traceable.

    They're badged as such. Drill-through from a statement line to the underlying journal entries comes with the live ELT connection.

  • Nothing posts to your ledger or moves money.

    Review-and-approve by design. It proposes; you dispose.
