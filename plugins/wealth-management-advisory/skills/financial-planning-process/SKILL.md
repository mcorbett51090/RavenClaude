# Financial Planning Process

**Purpose:** help the advisor run a disciplined, CFP-aligned financial-planning engagement — from
goals discovery through plan documentation — so that every recommendation is grounded in the
client's situation and the rationale is recorded.

---

## The six-step operating loop

### Step 1 — Establish the relationship and scope

- Agree on the scope of the engagement: comprehensive plan, focused planning (retirement only,
  estate only, insurance audit), or ongoing advisory with annual plan refresh.
- Identify the client's key relationships: spouse/partner decisions, adult children on accounts,
  trustees, accountants, estate attorneys. Document who is in the room and who is not.
- Set the engagement letter or service-agreement terms to reflect the scope.

### Step 2 — Collect data and goals

**Quantitative data to gather:**
- Assets: brokerage, retirement accounts (401k, IRA, Roth), real estate, business interests, 529s,
  annuities, cash/emergency fund.
- Liabilities: mortgage(s), student loans, auto, credit cards, business debt.
- Income: W-2, self-employment, rental, pension, Social Security estimates.
- Insurance: life (type, face, CSV), disability (benefit, elimination, definition), LTC, umbrella.
- Employee benefits: HSA/FSA, employer match, deferred comp, ESPP/RSU vesting schedule.
- Estate documents: will/trust currency, beneficiary designations (especially IRAs/401k), POA,
  healthcare directive.

**Qualitative goals to discover:**
- Retirement date and income target (pre-tax vs. after-tax; include healthcare costs).
- Legacy and charitable intent.
- Major upcoming expenditures (education, second home, business purchase).
- Attitude toward risk: what does a 30% portfolio decline feel like at this life stage?
- Life-event horizon: children leaving home, inheritance expected, business exit timeline.

### Step 3 — Analyze and evaluate

Run the planning issues by life stage:

| Life stage | Key issues |
|---|---|
| **Accumulation** | Savings rate (15–20% of gross as a rule of thumb [verify-at-use]), debt priority (high-rate first), disability gap, term life coverage, beneficiary audit |
| **Pre-retirement (5–15 years)** | Social Security claiming strategy, Roth conversion window analysis, RMD exposure at 73, Medicare Part B IRMAA exposure, long-term care decision, concentrated position / company stock, catch-up contributions |
| **Retirement** | Income sequencing (Social Security, pension, RMDs, portfolio withdrawals), sequence-of-returns risk management, withdrawal rate sustainability, QCD at 70½, Medigap/Medicare Advantage decision, RMD aggregation if multiple IRAs |
| **Legacy** | Estate document currency (wills, trusts, POA), beneficiary designation review (non-probate assets pass outside the will), charitable giving strategy (DAF, QCD, charitable remainder trust framing), step-up in basis planning |

### Step 4 — Develop recommendations

For every recommendation, document:
1. **The planning issue** addressed.
2. **The client's current situation** relative to that issue.
3. **The recommended action** and the alternatives considered.
4. **The rationale** — why this path best serves the client's objectives given their situation.
5. **The trade-offs** — what the client gives up or takes on.
6. **The suitability note** — confirm this is appropriate for the client's risk profile, time
   horizon, and financial situation (route to `advisory-compliance-advisor` for Reg BI clearance
   when a product recommendation is involved).

### Step 5 — Present recommendations

Structure the presentation for the client's comprehension level:
- Lead with the goals check: "Here is where you are vs. where you said you want to be."
- Group recommendations by planning issue, not by product.
- Use plain English; reserve the technical detail for an appendix.
- For projections: state the assumptions explicitly; frame as scenarios, not predictions.
- For Monte Carlo outputs: present as a probability range (e.g., "in 80% of 10,000 scenarios, the
  portfolio supports your income target to age 90") — never as a "success rate" prediction.
- Mandatory: past-performance disclosure whenever historical returns are referenced.

### Step 6 — Implement and monitor

- Prioritize actions: urgent (beneficiary update, missing insurance) → high-value (Roth conversion,
  debt payoff) → ongoing (savings rate, rebalancing).
- Assign owner and deadline for each action item.
- Set the next review trigger: calendar (annual), life-event (job change, inheritance, divorce,
  death of spouse), or portfolio-drift trigger.
- Update the plan documentation after each review cycle.

---

## Anti-patterns

- A plan outline without a goals-discovery step — it's a product pitch, not a plan.
- Recommendations without documented rationale — compliance exposure.
- Monte Carlo presented as certainty rather than probability framing.
- Projections with no stated assumptions — undisclosed assumptions are model risk.
- A plan that doesn't address the client's highest-anxiety issue first.
