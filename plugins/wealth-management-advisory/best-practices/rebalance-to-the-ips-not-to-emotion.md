# Rebalance to the IPS, not to emotion

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Portfolio management, RIA practice
**Applies to:** `wealth-management-advisory`

---

## Why this exists

The most common rebalancing failure is not forgetting to rebalance — it is rebalancing (or not
rebalancing) in response to recent market performance rather than in response to the IPS mandate.
An advisor who delays rebalancing a stock-heavy portfolio because "equities are still running" is
not portfolio managing — they are market timing in the client's account, without the client's
knowledge or consent. An advisor who rebalances out of equities after a sharp decline because
"the client is nervous" is responding to client emotion rather than the client's documented
long-term mandate.

Both failures violate the purpose of an IPS: to create a written governing document that removes
discretionary drift-management from the emotion of the moment. The IPS is the client's written
answer to the question "how do you want your money invested over your planning horizon?" — not
their answer to "how do you feel about the market this quarter?"

The pattern holds even when tax costs are real. Tax-managed drift-back (using cash flows and
harvesting to correct drift gradually) is a legitimate technique — it is still grounded in the
IPS target. What is not legitimate is indefinite delay of rebalancing with no plan to return to
the target.

## How to apply

**Do:**

- Traverse the rebalance-now-or-not decision tree in `knowledge/advisory-decision-trees.md`
  before every rebalancing decision.
- Set IPS tolerance bands at the time of plan creation and treat a breach as an automatic review
  trigger — not a judgment call about market conditions.
- Use tax-managed drift-back (cash flows, RMD redirects, harvesting) as a tax-efficiency lever,
  not as a rebalancing deferral mechanism.
- Document the rebalancing decision (or the deliberate hold) and the rationale at the time.
- When a client is anxious after a decline, acknowledge the emotion and then re-anchor to the IPS:
  "Your IPS was written for exactly this kind of period. Let me show you where you are relative
  to your tolerance bands."

**Don't:**

- Let a portfolio run outside IPS tolerance for multiple quarters because "the market looks like
  it will keep going."
- Defer rebalancing into equities after a decline because the client "doesn't want to buy more
  right now" — this is the behavioral error the IPS is designed to prevent.
- Make a rebalancing decision without referencing the IPS target and tolerance bands.
- Rebalance without considering the tax consequences — but equally, don't use tax as a permanent
  veto on restoring the mandate.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- When a major life event (retirement date moved up, large inheritance, health crisis) changes the
  client's actual objectives and risk capacity — the right response is to update the IPS, not to
  rebalance to a target that no longer fits. Update the IPS first; then rebalance to the new target.
- When the client is within 90 days of a significant planned distribution (college tuition draw,
  home purchase, retirement date), letting a portfolio run slightly outside bands to avoid selling
  at a temporarily depressed price may be a sound de-risking judgment — document it as such.
- In a discretionary managed account with a specific strategy mandate (e.g., a risk-parity or
  tactical allocation strategy), the rebalancing trigger may be defined by the strategy rather than
  a static IPS — the discipline is the same (follow the mandate, not the emotion), but the
  mechanism differs.
