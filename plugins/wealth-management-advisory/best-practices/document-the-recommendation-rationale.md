# Document the recommendation rationale

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Advisory compliance, financial planning, RIA practice
**Applies to:** `wealth-management-advisory`

---

## Why this exists

A recommendation without a written rationale is an exposure — both regulatory and relational. From
a regulatory standpoint, the Reg BI care obligation and the fiduciary duty of care require that
a recommendation be based on the client's best interest; documentation is the only evidence that
the analysis actually occurred. From a practice standpoint, a rationale in the client file protects
the advisor when a client later disputes the recommendation or a regulator asks why a specific
product was chosen.

The absence of a written rationale also degrades planning quality: advisors who must articulate
why a recommendation is appropriate, in writing, catch weaknesses in their own reasoning that a
verbal conversation does not surface. The act of writing the rationale is itself a quality check.

Under Reg BI's compliance obligation and the Advisers Act's Books and Records Rule (Rule 204-2),
advisors are required to maintain records that evidence the basis for their recommendations.

## How to apply

Every recommendation — in a financial plan, a portfolio review, a proposal, or a meeting follow-up
— must include:

1. **The planning issue or client need** addressed by the recommendation.
2. **The client's current situation** relevant to that issue (account balance, tax bracket, time
   horizon, existing coverage, current allocation, etc.).
3. **The recommended action** — specific and actionable.
4. **The rationale** — why this recommendation is appropriate for this client's specific situation
   and objectives. This is the substance: the connection between the client's situation and the
   recommendation's benefit.
5. **The alternatives considered** — what other reasonable options were evaluated and why they were
   not chosen (or why they were ranked lower).
6. **The trade-offs** — what the client gives up or takes on with the recommended path.

The rationale need not be lengthy. One well-written paragraph that answers "why this, for this
client, now" is sufficient. A table with a column for each of the above elements works for plan
documentation.

**Do:**

- Write the rationale at the time of recommendation, not after the fact.
- Tie the rationale to the specific client's documented investment profile, not a generic
  description of the product's attributes.
- Retain the rationale in the client file per the applicable recordkeeping period (minimum 5 years
  under Rule 204-2; confirm firm policy [verify-at-use]).

**Don't:**

- Write "client was informed and agreed" as a rationale — that is consent, not rationale.
- Describe the product's features without connecting them to the client's specific situation.
- Defer writing the rationale until it is needed for a compliance exam or a complaint.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- Educational conversations that do not result in a recommendation do not require a rationale
  document — though a brief note in the CRM of what was discussed is always best practice.
- For routine, ongoing portfolio management actions within the parameters of a signed IPS (e.g.,
  a calendar rebalancing within IPS tolerance bands), a brief trade note referencing the IPS is
  sufficient — a full recommendation rationale is not required for each trade execution.
