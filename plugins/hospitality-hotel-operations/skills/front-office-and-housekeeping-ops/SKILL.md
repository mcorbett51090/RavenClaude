# Front-Office & Housekeeping Operations

## Map the guest journey as one system
Booking → arrival → stay → departure → post-stay is owned end-to-end. A defect in one stage (a slow check-in, an unactioned maintenance ticket) surfaces downstream as a review and a lost repeat stay. Map the journey, find the bottleneck/defect, and fix the stage that produces it.

## The PMS is the system of record
Room status, rate, folio, and guest profile live in the PMS. A workflow run off a side spreadsheet drifts and fails the guest at check-in — a "ready" room that isn't is the costliest defect. The housekeeping ↔ front-desk room-status handoff is where the journey breaks; fix that flow first.

## Housekeeping is a throughput problem
Treat it as flow, not a dirty-room list: rooms-cleaned-per-shift productivity, pre-arrival prep, and a rooms-ready-by-check-in target. Measure the turn rate against the arrival peak, not the day total.

## SOP authoring — the decision made well once
A good SOP removes the per-incident judgment call (late check-out, room move, walk-protocol, lost-and-found, incident handling) so any staff member executes it consistently and the guest gets the same answer at 3pm and 3am.

## Labor to the curve, protect the floor
Schedule front-desk and housekeeping to the occupancy forecast (route the forecast to `revenue-manager` / `applied-statistics`), matching coverage to the arrival peak and the turn load — but never below the service floor a clean room and a staffed desk require. Maintenance is revenue: an out-of-order room is unsellable inventory, so the report → triage → fix → return-to-sellable loop is an availability lever.

## Output
A guest-journey map with the defect named, the SOP fix, the labor schedule against the forecast, and the maintenance loop — each tied to the KPI it moves (check-in time, rooms-ready rate, labor cost vs. service floor). Route pricing to `revenue-manager`, the review loop to `guest-experience-analyst`, F&B to `restaurant-operations`.
