## Problem

A 220-room city hotel overbooked a sold-out conference weekend by 14 rooms on a hunch that "weekends always no-show." Far fewer guests no-showed than assumed, the house filled, and the night team had to walk 9 arriving guests — several of them loyalty members and one a corporate-account decision-maker — with no plan for where to send them, what to comp, or who had authority to authorize it. The walks happened at 11pm, improvised at the desk, and three of the walked guests left one-star reviews naming the property by the next morning.

## Constraints context

- Overbooking was set by gut ("weekends no-show"), not by a forecasted no-show/late-cancel rate for that date type — and a sold-out conference weekend is exactly when no-show rates fall.
- There was no operations-owned walk-protocol: no list of partner hotels, no defined comp/transport, no comp-authority tier, no script — the front desk invented all of it live.
- The yield upside from the marginal rooms was never weighed against the expected walk cost plus brand damage; overbooking was treated as free upside.

## Attempts

- Tried: walking guests on a first-come-improvised basis. Failed — without a protocol the desk sent guests to a more expensive hotel with no transport arranged, comped inconsistently, and walked high-value loyalty members because nobody had a do-not-walk priority list.
- Tried: an apology email and points after the fact. Failed — a post-hoc comp doesn't undo an 11pm walk to a stranger's front desk; the reviews and the lost corporate account stuck.
- Tried: re-sizing overbooking to the *forecasted* no-show rate for the date type (which for sold-out conference dates was near zero), and building an operations-owned walk-protocol *before* overbooking again — partner-hotel list, pre-negotiated rate + transport, comp-authority tiers, a do-not-walk priority (loyalty / corporate / long-stay), and a script. This worked.

## Resolution

Tying the overbook size to the forecasted no-show rate for the specific date type stopped the over-aggressive overbooking on exactly the dates where no-shows are rarest. On the next genuinely no-show-prone date the property overbooked modestly and the rare walk was executed gracefully — a pre-arranged comparable hotel, transport, a standard comp the front desk could authorize without escalation, and a follow-up — turning a broken promise into a recoverable one. The do-not-walk priority list kept the highest-value guests in-house.

## Lesson

Overbook only to a forecasted, owned no-show rate for the date type — and a sold-out high-demand date is when no-shows fall, not rise. Never overbook without an operations-owned walk-protocol defined in advance: who walks, where, the comp, the transport, the do-not-walk priority, and the follow-up. A walk is a broken guarantee; the overbook size is the revenue-manager's, the walk-protocol is operations'.
