## Problem

A 160-room resort under margin pressure cut housekeeping and front-desk hours with a flat across-the-board headcount reduction to hit a labor-cost target. The cost line improved on the next P&L — and then the review score dropped half a star over two months: rooms weren't ready by check-in on high-arrival days, the desk had a 20-minute queue at the afternoon peak, and the repeat-booking rate softened. The labor "win" had simply moved the cost off the P&L and onto the guest, and then onto the top line as lost repeat business.

## Constraints context

- The cut was a flat headcount reduction, not a schedule shaped to the occupancy curve — it trimmed peak hours and trough hours equally, so the arrival peak and the room-turn window lost coverage they couldn't spare.
- There was no defined service floor: no stated minimum for "a clean room by check-in" and "a staffed desk at the arrival peak," so the cut had nothing to stop it from going too deep.
- Labor was managed purely as a cost line, disconnected from its effect on the review score and the repeat rate it was quietly eroding.

## Attempts

- Tried: holding the flat cut and pushing staff to "work faster." Failed — speed can't manufacture coverage that isn't scheduled; rooms still missed check-in on high-arrival days and the queue persisted at the peak.
- Tried: restoring all the cut hours. Failed in the other direction — it gave back the entire saving including the genuinely over-staffed trough hours, so margin pressure returned with no net service gain.
- Tried: re-shaping the schedule to the occupancy curve — staffing the arrival peak and the room-turn load to the forecast, flexing down only the trough hours, and defining an explicit service floor (rooms ready by check-in, desk staffed at peak) below which no cut goes. This worked.

## Resolution

Scheduling to the curve instead of a flat headcount recovered most of the labor saving from the genuine trough hours while restoring coverage where the load actually was. Rooms-ready-by-check-in climbed back, the afternoon queue cleared, and the review score and repeat rate recovered over the following two cycles. The explicit service floor became the line the cost target was not allowed to cross — labor stayed a controllable cost, but no longer one paid for by the guest.

## Lesson

Labor is the biggest controllable cost and the biggest service lever — schedule to the occupancy forecast, matching coverage to the arrival peak and the turn load, but never cut below the service floor a clean room and a staffed desk require. A flat across-the-board cut ignores the curve; a labor win that drops the review score and the repeat rate is a loss that just moved the cost onto the guest.
