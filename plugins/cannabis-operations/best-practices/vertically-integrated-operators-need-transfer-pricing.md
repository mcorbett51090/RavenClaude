# Vertically integrated operators must set internal transfer prices

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Cannabis operations / COGS / 280E
**Applies to:** `cannabis-operations`

---

## Why this exists

A vertically integrated operator (cultivation + manufacturing + distribution + retail) that moves product between its own licensed entities without documented arm's-length transfer prices creates two compounding problems: it lacks the evidence needed to defend COGS allocation under 280E, and it opens the company to a transfer-pricing adjustment that can unwind every tax position retrospectively. The IRS does not require fair-market-value transfer pricing for intracompany goods in ordinary cases, but under 280E the allocation of cost between COGS-eligible (production/transformation) and disallowed (retail overhead) tiers is the central tax calculation — and it is indefensible without a per-unit cost basis at each transfer point.

## How to apply

Document a written internal transfer price policy before the first inter-entity transfer. At minimum, record:

```
Transfer price policy — [entity name] — effective [date]
Cultivation → manufacturing:  $/lb by strain (updated [frequency])
Manufacturing → distribution: $/unit SKU (updated [frequency])
Distribution → retail:        $/unit SKU (updated [frequency])
Basis: [cost-plus x% | comparable-uncontrolled-price | other method]
Approved by: [title] on [date]
```

Calculate the price from a cost-plus build of direct materials, labor, and overhead at each tier. Mark the methodology in the policy and review it quarterly as costs change.

**Do:**
- Set prices using a documented cost-plus methodology tied to actual batch-level costs.
- Update prices when raw material or labor costs shift by more than 10%.
- Archive the transfer-price schedule with each entity's books as supporting documentation.
- Route the final policy to the operator's cannabis CPA before filing any 280E return that depends on it.

**Don't:**
- Move product at zero or arbitrary values "just to get it through the system."
- Ignore transfer pricing when entities share the same beneficial owner — related-party transactions receive more scrutiny, not less.
- Treat a transfer-price adjustment as a bookkeeping footnote; it can flip a profitable entity to a loss after the fact.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- Single-license operators with no inter-entity transfers have no transfer-pricing obligation — though the same cost-build discipline applies to their COGS allocation.
- States that mandate a declared wholesale value at point of licensee transfer (e.g., certain excise-tax regimes) set an external floor; the internal transfer price cannot dip below it.
