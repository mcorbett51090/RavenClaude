# Budtender product knowledge directly drives UPT and gross margin

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Cannabis operations / dispensary retail / workforce
**Applies to:** `cannabis-operations`

---

## Why this exists

The budtender is the most direct lever on basket size, units per transaction (UPT), and category mix in a cannabis dispensary. A budtender who cannot speak fluently to terpene profiles, onset times, consumption formats, or dosage guidance defaults to fulfilling the stated ask — one item, the price the customer named, regardless of what would actually serve them. The result is a basket that captures none of the add-on margin available in accessories, concentrates, or ingestibles. Given that dispensary gross margin is already compressed by the 280E burden, a systematic failure to lift basket size from product knowledge is a material P&L issue, not a soft sales problem.

## How to apply

Build product-knowledge training into the weekly cadence, not just onboarding:

**Weekly product knowledge briefing (15 min, every new product added to menu):**
```
Product: ____
Category: [flower | concentrate | edible | topical | accessory]
Terpene profile (top 3): ____ / ____ / ____
Reported effects (operator's framing, not a medical claim): ____
Onset / duration: ____
Recommended pairing (complementary SKU): ____
Price point vs category average: ____
Margin rank in category: [top quartile | mid | bottom quartile]
```

**Tie UPT to the training program:**
- Track budtender-level UPT monthly; surface it in the floor team's 1:1s.
- When a budtender's UPT is below the store average for ≥2 consecutive weeks, trigger a product-knowledge audit, not a sales script.

**Do:**
- Center training on customer outcomes (effects, experience) rather than selling points — customers trust guidance framed in their interest.
- Rotate high-margin accessories into the briefing regardless of newness — accessories are the highest-UPT add-on and the most underutilized.
- Measure UPT per budtender, per shift, and per category — disaggregating the metric surfaces which skus and which staff need attention.

**Don't:**
- Treat product training as a one-time onboarding event; menu turnover in cannabis is high and monthly additions require ongoing training.
- Make medical claims — budtenders are not licensed healthcare providers; frame everything in terms of reported customer experience.
- Incentivize budtenders purely on transaction count; count-focused incentives push through-put at the expense of basket depth.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- High-traffic, low-dwell environments (express lanes, pre-order pickup) have limited budtender interaction time; optimize those lanes for speed and reserve full-interaction time for consultation-track customers.
- States with strict limits on what employees may say about products (some restrict terpene/effects language specifically) require the training content to be reviewed against the applicable regulations.
