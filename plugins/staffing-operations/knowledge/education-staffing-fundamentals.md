# Education / school-based staffing fundamentals

> **Last reviewed:** 2026-06-04. Working reference for the `education-staffing-specialist`. Covers the roles, the demand structure (IDEA), the academic-calendar seasonality, teletherapy, and the district-budget context (ESSER cliff). **Refresh when:** a new Soliant *State of Special Education Staffing* report, a new LPI teacher-shortage factsheet, or a CMS school-Medicaid change lands. ## 1. The roles (what districts contract)

**Related services — the highest-margin school placements** (IDEA-classified, must be delivered per the IEP):
- **SLP** (+ SLPAs, CFY fellows), **OT / COTA**, **PT**. BLS projects **SLP +15%, OT +14% (2024–34)** — primary-source, defensible. Shortages are structural: the late-1970s **PL 94-142 cohort** is retiring while the training pipeline lags.

**Instructional / classroom:**
- **Special-education teachers** — the #1 reported shortage (45 states); ~**411,549** positions nationally unfilled or filled by under-certified staff (~1 in 8); **74%** of elementary/middle schools struggled to fill a SpEd vacancy with a fully-certified teacher (LPI 2025). NCES: schools filled **79%** of an average **6** vacancies before classes resumed.
- **Paraprofessionals / aides** — high-volume, high-turnover, often bundled into managed-service contracts.
- **Substitute teachers** — the largest contracted category by headcount, dominated by **ESS** and **Kelly Education**; sub fill rates fell below pre-COVID (~70% national fall fill vs. ~80% 2018–19 baseline), driven by ~$18/hr median pay with no benefits. A scale game Soliant does **not** compete in.

**Behavioral / mental health / nursing:**
- **School psychologists** — acute: ratio **~1,071:1 actual vs. 500:1 recommended** (NASP); NASP estimates **~63,000 more** needed.
- **BCBA / RBT** — fastest-growing behavioral demand; **BCBA postings +58–59% in 2024**; driven by autism prevalence now **~1 in 31 children (CDC)** vs. 1 in 150 two decades ago.
- **School nurses** — the healthcare overlap; medically-fragile students + Medicaid-billable services drive demand.

## 2. Demand is non-discretionary (IDEA)

IDEA mandates related services per the IEP **regardless of budget or staffing**. The demand base is structural and growing: students served under IDEA rose from **6.4M (2012–13) to ~7.9M (~15.9% of enrollment, 2023–24)**, +3.8% (~302K) in 2024 alone (NCES; Disability Scoop). This is why a fill miss in school-based work is a *liability* event, not just a revenue miss (see [`credentialing-and-compliance.md`](credentialing-and-compliance.md) §3).

## 3. The academic-calendar hiring cycle (the operating system)

Districts run a **July 1 fiscal year**:
- Budgets draft **Jan–Feb**, finalize/approve in **spring**; contract-renewal deadlines **Mar–Apr**.
- Roles post **late winter** → bulk-fill **late spring/early summer** for an **Aug/Sept start** → a second **late-summer/fall** wave for last-minute gaps. High-demand roles (SpEd, STEM) post earliest.

**Operating consequence (§3 #5):** measure fill rate and time-to-fill **against this cycle, not calendar quarters**. A Q4 fill rate that looks terrible is often a calendar artifact (the hiring happened in Q2–Q3). Front-load the recruiting pipeline in spring/summer. Time-to-fill: on-site SpEd/therapy hires take **3–6 months** (summer) to **6–9 months** (by fall); teletherapy launches in **days**.

## 4. Teletherapy (structural, not a stopgap)

A permanent **hybrid** model: onsite staff cover high-need/crisis/hands-on cases; virtual clinicians cover ongoing IEP services and the hard-to-fill rural roles, launchable in days vs. months. Institutional capital has validated it — **Presence (formerly PresenceLearning)** took majority investment from Spectrum Equity and TPG's Rise Fund and serves 10,000+ schools with 2,000+ providers; **eLuma** ("built for schools, not adapted from healthcare") and **TinyEYE / Huddle Up** are other natives. **The rural-equity gap is the primary teletherapy sales case** — rural/high-need districts can't compete on pay and have the widest vacancies (17% of low-minority vs. 28% of high-minority schools report ≥1 SpEd vacancy, LPI 2025).

## 5. District-budget context — the ESSER cliff

Federal COVID-relief (ESSER III) had to be **obligated by Sept 30, 2024**, spent by Jan 28, 2025 (extensions to **Mar 30, 2026**). ~50% of ESSER III went to labor; in an AASA Aug-2023 survey **53%** of district leaders expected to cut specialist staff. High-poverty districts (>75%) face a **~6%** budget hit vs. ~2% for affluent ones (Brookings).

**The cliff cuts both ways — and the second direction is the consulting angle:** it pressures discretionary budgets, *but* it pushes districts from fixed FTEs toward **variable-cost contract staffing**. LPI frames the period as a "paradoxical labor market" of simultaneous layoffs and shortages. Frame contract staffing as the budget-flexible answer, not an extra cost.

- North America **education-staffing market ~$3.1B (2025)**, ~5.4% CAGR; special-ed substitute segment fastest at ~8.4% CAGR `[ESTIMATE — secondary aggregator]`. The clean **SIA education-staffing figure is paywalled (page 403'd)** — pull it directly for any client-facing number.
