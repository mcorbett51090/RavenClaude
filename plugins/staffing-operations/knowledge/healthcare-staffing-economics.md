# Healthcare staffing economics — bill / pay / spread, burden, and the segments

> **Last reviewed:** 2026-06-04. The working reference for the `healthcare-staffing-specialist`. Covers the bill/pay/burden decomposition, the burden stack, the four segments (travel nurse / locum / allied / per-diem-gig), and the rate cycle. **Refresh when:** SIA/NATHO publishes new bill-rate data or a new annual segment sizing.

**The decomposition rule (§3 #3):** margin = **bill − pay − burden**. Never declare a margin problem a *pricing* problem before itemizing burden. Spread compression hides in the burden line far more often than in the headline markup.

---

## 1. The burden stack (itemize before diagnosing)

For a placed worker, "burden" is everything between the pay rate and true cost:

| Burden line | Notes / where it bites |
|---|---|
| Employer payroll taxes | FICA, FUTA, SUTA — SUTA varies by state and experience rating |
| Workers' compensation | Rate varies by role risk class and state |
| Professional / general liability + **malpractice** | **Dominant in locum tenens**; tail coverage adds cost |
| Health & ancillary benefits | Heavier on perm/long-assignment models |
| **Housing / lodging / per-diem stipends** | **Travel-specific**; the non-taxable stipend is both a margin lever and a compliance landmine (must meet IRS "tax home" rules) |
| Travel reimbursement | Mileage / flights / licensure reimbursement |
| Credentialing & onboarding cost | Background, primary-source verification, compliance docs — real $ and real time |
| Bench / idle time | Unbilled time between assignments; redeployment rate is the lever |

A "22% gross margin" with no burden itemization can't be acted on. When margin moves, walk the stack and name the line.

## 2. The four segments

### Travel nursing
- Highest-volume, most rate-cyclical. **Aggregate bill rate ~$133/hr (2022) → ~$106–107 (2023) → ~$90 (2024) → ~flat $90.5 (2025)** per SIA/NATHO `[unverified — sources differ $89.78 vs $90.54; reconcile before client use]`.
- Revenue contracted **~40% (2023)** and **~37% (2024)** off the COVID peak; bottoming into 2026 (~+1%). SIA 2025 segment size **~$14.2B**.
- Pay = taxable wage + non-taxable stipends; the stipend structure is a margin lever *and* a compliance exposure (worker must maintain a qualifying tax home). Flag stipend design for the client's tax counsel — do not advise on it directly.

### Locum tenens (the growth segment)
- Physicians + advanced-practice providers (NP/PA). **~$9.6B (2025), ~+4–6%/yr.** ~**57,000 physicians** (~8% of eligible) worked locums in 2024, ~**90 days** each; locums touch up to ~1 in 3 US patients (CHG *State of Locum Tenens 2025*).
- **Malpractice + tail coverage is the dominant burden line.** Scale players: CHG (CompHealth/Weatherby/RNnetwork, ~31% locum share), Jackson, AMN.
- Driver: physician shortages (HRSA projects steep deficits through 2037) and each unfilled physician vacancy meaning ~$2.6M lost revenue, pushing utilization ~25% above plan.

### Allied health
- Imaging/radiology, radiation oncology, respiratory, medical lab, therapy. **~$9.8B (2025)**, steadier than travel nursing. BLS projects SLP +15%, OT +14% (2024–34). **Soliant has historically been a top-3 allied firm** — a strength to defend for a Soliant-shaped client.

### Per-diem / gig
- Local (within ~250 mi). Being reshaped by W-2/1099 marketplaces — **ShiftKey, CareRev, Clipboard Health** — now **>20% of US temp-staffing revenue**. **~$4.5B (2025)** per-diem nursing (SIA). Rising regulatory friction: NY in 2025 reclassified gig nursing platforms as staffing agencies, prompting several to exit the state. A model orthogonal to the traditional agency.

## 3. The rate cycle (carry this; operators will test it)

The 2021–22 "crisis pay" spike was an anomaly, not a baseline. Bill rates fell **~33% from 2022 to 2024**; 2025 was the **third straight contraction year** before stabilizing. An operator who staffed up in 2021 may still anchor to peak rates — anchor *them* to the SIA/NATHO series with the date. Quoting a peak-era rate as current is the fastest way to lose credibility (§3 #9).

Facility-side context (NSI National Health Care Retention Report, via Becker's `[unverified — primary 403'd]`): RN turnover 22.5% (2022) → 16.4% (2024) → 17.6% (2025); RN vacancy ~8.6% (2025); each 1-pt RN-turnover change ≈ ±$295K/yr for the average hospital. Travel-nurse cost ~$189,758/yr vs. employed RN ~$123,676/yr — the ~$66K gap is the hospital's agency-vs-internal tension that drives float-pool and direct-sourcing investment (a structural headwind for agencies).
