# ESSER Cliff Is a Structural Demand Shift, Not a Budget Cycle

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Staffing operations
**Applies to:** `staffing-operations`

---

## Why this exists

The end of Elementary and Secondary School Emergency Relief (ESSER) federal funding — the bulk of which expired in September 2024 — is not a routine budget cycle. Districts that used ESSER funds to expand school-based therapy and specialist positions are now cutting positions, closing programs, and reducing contract-staffing budgets. A consultant who treats ESSER-funded demand as a stable baseline is projecting growth into a structural decline. Any education-staffing engagement that doesn't explicitly model the post-ESSER district posture is using the wrong demand baseline.

## How to apply

Assess ESSER exposure for every education-staffing client engagement:

```
ESSER Demand Adjustment — District / Client Segment
────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Districts served:  ________________
ESSER funding expiry:  September 2024 (the final extension deadline) [verify per district]

Assessment:
  1. What % of the firm's education contracts were placed with districts that
     explicitly used ESSER funds for staffing?                        ___%

  2. Of ESSER-funded positions, what % have been:
     [ ] Converted to general-fund permanent positions
     [ ] Maintained on contract with non-ESSER budget
     [ ] Reduced / eliminated
     [ ] Unknown (requires district outreach)

  3. Post-ESSER demand baseline for this firm (conservative estimate):
     ESSER-funded positions × (1 - elimination rate) = ___  remaining positions

  4. Staffing firm exposure:
     Estimated revenue at risk from ESSER position eliminations:  $______

Recommended response:
  [ ] Diversify to districts with strong general-fund SPED budgets
  [ ] Expand teletherapy capacity (lower per-service cost for budget-constrained districts)
  [ ] Model FY2025–26 targets using post-ESSER demand, not FY2023–24 actuals
```

**Do:**
- Model FY2025–26 and FY2026–27 volume targets using a post-ESSER demand baseline, not pre-cliff historical growth rates.
- Identify which district relationships are ESSER-dependent vs. general-fund-supported and prioritize retention of general-fund districts.
- Treat teletherapy as a demand-side response — districts facing budget pressure often shift to teletherapy to reduce per-service cost while maintaining IDEA compliance.

**Don't:**
- Use FY2022–23 or FY2023–24 education-staffing volume as a forward-looking baseline without an explicit ESSER adjustment.
- Treat ESSER cliff as a one-time event; the multi-year budget adjustment as districts rebuild general-fund capacity creates a 2–4 year demand trough in some segments.
- Conflate ESSER-driven demand decline with a recruiter or operational performance problem — they look similar in fill-rate and volume data but have entirely different fixes.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Districts in states with strong state-level SPED funding mandates (California's AB 3632 framework, New York's mandate structure) may have partially offset ESSER expiry with state funds. Confirm at the district level before applying a blanket ESSER discount.
