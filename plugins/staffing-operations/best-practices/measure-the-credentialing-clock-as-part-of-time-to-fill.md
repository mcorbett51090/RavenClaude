# Measure the credentialing clock as part of time-to-fill

**Status:** Pattern. **Constitution:** §3 #7, §3 #8.

## Use when
Time-to-start lags time-to-offer, fall-off concentrates between accept and start, or you're benchmarking time-to-fill in healthcare or school-based work.

## The rule
Time-to-fill runs to **start**, not to offer-accept. The credentialing/clearance interval (licensure, background, Joint Commission documentation, district clearance) is part of the clock, not a back-office afterthought.

## Why
A **9-day submit-to-offer that then sits 21 days in credentialing is a 30-day fill.** A scorecard that stops at offer-accept hides a third of the clock and mis-attributes the delay to sales when it lives in compliance — so leadership optimizes the wrong function. The accept→start interval is also where most **fall-off** happens: a candidate who accepts but can't clear is a failed placement and a re-source cost. In healthcare, end-to-end credentialing commonly runs 90–120 days; in school-based work, fingerprint/background clearance and district onboarding are a real, measurable delay.

## How to apply
- Track the credentialing/clearance interval as its **own KPI** and fold it into reported time-to-fill.
- Map the gates and find the **critical path** — compress the longest sequential chain, parallelize what can run concurrently, front-load document collection at submittal (not accept).
- Instrument **document-completion %** as the hard start-gate; it's where fall-off originates.
- Speed up by **process** (automate, parallelize, pre-credential the bench), never by skipping a compliance step — the gates exist for a reason (Joint Commission, IDEA/Medicaid). See [`../knowledge/credentialing-and-compliance.md`](../knowledge/credentialing-and-compliance.md).
