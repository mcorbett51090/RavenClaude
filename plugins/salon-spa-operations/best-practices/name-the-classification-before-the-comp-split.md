# Name the classification before the comp split

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Compensation / staffing
**Applies to:** `salon-spa-operations`

---

## Why this exists

The single most consequential — and most misunderstood — decision in a salon is whether a stylist is an *employee* (commission, salary-plus-commission) or an *independent contractor* (booth/chair renter). Owners often pick the label for convenience ("renting is less hassle") without realizing that worker classification is a **legal test**, not a preference. Getting it wrong exposes the owner to back-taxes, penalties, and reclassification — long after the comp split felt settled.

## How to apply

- **Frame the operational trade** here: commission buys control of brand/schedule/pricing/client-book at the cost of employer obligations; rental buys low overhead at the cost of control; hybrid splits it.
- **Never represent a model as "legal," "safe," or "compliant."** That verdict turns on control, tools, and schedule and is jurisdiction-dependent.
- **Always flag the classification check** and route it to `people-operations-hr`; route payroll/tax/sales-tax mechanics to `accounting-bookkeeping`.

**Do:** end every comp recommendation with the classification flag.
**Don't:** let an owner treat "I'll just rent the chairs" as a way to sidestep employment obligations.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

None on the flag itself — even a clear-cut case still gets the explicit "confirm classification with people-operations-hr" note, because the test is jurisdiction-specific and the agent is not the legal authority.
