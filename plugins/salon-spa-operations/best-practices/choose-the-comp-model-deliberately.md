# Choose the comp model deliberately

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Provider economics / classification
**Applies to:** `salon-spa-operations`

> Operations and financial decision-support, **not** legal, tax, or employment-classification advice. The employee-vs-1099 determination routes to a licensed professional. Benchmarks are `[verify-at-use]`. No client PII.

---

## Why this exists

The compensation model — commission tiers, hourly-plus-commission, booth rent, or a hybrid — defines the entire relationship between shop and provider: who carries the risk, who keeps the upside, who controls the chair, and whether the provider is an **employee or an independent contractor**. Picking it on a rule of thumb collapses either the shop's margin or the provider's income, and mislabeling a controlled chair as "booth rent" is a real classification exposure.

## How to apply

- **Model on the real book:** run the provider's actual service revenue, product cost, and hours through each model — the "better" model depends on how full and high-priced the book is.
- **Weigh the trade:** booth rent swaps the shop's upside for predictable rent and swaps the provider's safety net for full upside and full risk; commission keeps the shop in the growth and on the hook for the slow week.
- **Follow control:** who sets the schedule, buys the product, and sets the price signals the classification — a chair the shop controls is employee-leaning regardless of what the agreement is called.
- **Flag, don't decide:** model the economics; route the employee-vs-1099 determination, and any lease/tax question, to a licensed professional. `[verify-at-use]`.

**Do:** model each model on the real book; flag the classification call.
**Don't:** pick a model by rule of thumb; call a controlled chair "booth rent."

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Nothing here is legal advice — where classification, lease, tax, or payroll law is in play, the licensed professional's determination governs and this rule defers to it.
