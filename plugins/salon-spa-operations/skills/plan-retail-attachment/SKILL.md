# Plan Retail Attachment

Service revenue is labor-bound; retail is the highest-margin line and the cheapest retention hook. A client using your product comes back for more.

## The sequence

1. **Set a retail-to-service ratio target.** Retail revenue ÷ service revenue — a concrete number to manage to (compare to the dated benchmark in the reference map; re-verify before quoting).
2. **Curate the shelf + back bar.** Stock the SKUs your stylists actually use in the service, so the recommend is the product the client just saw working — not a random shelf item.
3. **Coach the at-the-chair recommend.** The recommend lands when the stylist prescribes what they used ("this is the bond-builder I just put in — it's why your ends feel like that"), not as a hard close at checkout.
4. **Make the front desk close the loop.** The product the stylist named should be ready at checkout; the front desk attaches it, not re-sells it.
5. **Measure attachment by stylist.** Surface the ratio per stylist and coach — like rebooking, it's a trainable behavior.

## What "done" looks like
- A named retail-to-service ratio target, tracked.
- The shelf matches the back bar (you can recommend what you use).
- The recommend is part of the service; the front desk closes it at checkout.

## Anti-patterns
- A shelf of SKUs nobody uses in service (nothing to authentically recommend).
- The recommend as a checkout hard-sell instead of an in-service prescription.
- No per-stylist attachment visibility.

Retail margin and the pricing of product sit with `service-menu-and-pricing-strategist`; the COGS/sales-tax accounting is an `accounting-bookkeeping` seam.
