# Choose Commission vs Booth/Chair Rental

The compensation model defines the business. It's a control/risk trade — not just a split — and it carries a legal classification line you must flag.

## The two models (and the hybrid)

| | Commission (employee) | Booth/chair rental (independent) |
|---|---|---|
| **You control** | Brand, schedule, pricing, products, client book | Little — renter sets their own |
| **You carry** | Payroll taxes, benefits, employer obligations | A space + utilities; near-zero per-stylist overhead |
| **Revenue** | Share of every service + retail | Fixed rent regardless of their bookings |
| **Risk** | Variable cost scales with revenue | Empty chair still owes you rent (to them, the risk) |

**Hybrid** — rental-plus-service-fee, or commission-with-product-charge, or a "salon suite" model — splits the difference.

## The sequence

1. **Name what you're optimizing for** — control of the brand/experience and client relationship, or low overhead and predictable income.
2. **Traverse the compensation-model tree** in the knowledge bank before deciding.
3. **State the trade explicitly** — what you gain and give up under each.
4. **Flag the classification test.** Employee-vs-contractor turns on control, tools, and schedule — a legal test, jurisdiction-dependent. **Never call a model "legal" or "safe."**

## Mandatory escalation
- The **legal worker-classification verdict** → `people-operations-hr`.
- The **payroll/tax mechanics and sales-tax** → `accounting-bookkeeping`.

## Anti-patterns
- Picking rental to "avoid management" — it's different management (a property + tenants), not none.
- Stating a model is compliant without the classification check.
- Ignoring who owns the client book — it drives rebooking and retention.
