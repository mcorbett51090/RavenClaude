# Events have a donor conversion goal, not just a revenue goal

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Nonprofit events and fundraising strategy
**Applies to:** `nonprofit-fundraising`

---

## Why this exists

Events are expensive relative to their net revenue: a gala that raises $200,000 gross often nets $80,000–$100,000 after costs — a cost-to-raise-a-dollar of $1.00–$1.25, the least efficient channel in most shops. The economic case for events does not rest primarily on net event revenue; it rests on the subsequent relationships the event creates. A gala attended by 50 new prospects who become donors in the following 12 months is a far better investment than a gala attended entirely by loyal existing donors with no new relationships formed. Setting a donor-conversion goal — how many event attendees became first-time donors within 12 months — forces the development team to think about the event as a cultivation vehicle, not just a revenue vehicle.

## How to apply

Define, before the event, a donor-conversion goal alongside the net-revenue goal. Build post-event follow-up into the event budget as a required line item.

```
Event metrics framework:
  Primary metric (efficiency): net revenue / total event spend = cost-to-raise-a-dollar
  Secondary metric (pipeline): first-time donor conversion rate
    = (attendees who gave within 12 months and had no prior gift) / total non-donor attendees
    Target: 10–20% conversion within 12 months [ESTIMATE — varies by event type]

Post-event follow-up required (line item in event budget):
  — Thank-you calls to all donors and prospects within 48 hours of event
  — Personalized follow-up letters within 1 week
  — Cultivation ask within 90 days for identified major-gift prospects
  — 12-month tracking in CRM of all non-donor attendees' giving status

Annual evaluation: if donor-conversion rate is below 10% and cost-to-raise > $1.25, evaluate whether the event belongs in the channel mix.
```

**Do:**
- Set a first-time donor conversion rate goal for every major fundraising event.
- Build post-event cultivation touchpoints into the event plan before the event occurs.
- Track event-sourced donors separately in the CRM so cohort analysis is possible.

**Don't:**
- Evaluate events purely on gross or net revenue without measuring subsequent donor conversion.
- Cut post-event follow-up costs as a way to improve event net revenue — the follow-up is where the pipeline forms.
- Repeat an event year after year without reviewing the donor-conversion rate alongside the revenue number.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Purely community-engagement events (a volunteer day, a friend-raiser) have a different conversion timeline and may have a softer relationship-building goal rather than a 12-month giving conversion target. Government-funded programs that host events for service recipients, not donors, are not in scope for this rule.

Codifies the event-as-cultivation discipline standard in development-office practice.
