# Board members are door-openers, not askers by default

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Nonprofit major gifts and governance
**Applies to:** `nonprofit-fundraising`

---

## Why this exists

Development directors frequently report that their biggest major-gifts barrier is that board members "won't ask." This framing misses the real problem: most board members lack the training, the case-for-support fluency, and the relationship context to make a peer-to-peer ask comfortably — and pushing them to ask before they are ready generates awkward conversations that damage donor relationships. The solution is to distinguish the roles board members are effective at — introducing, hosting, co-signing, opening doors — from the trained staff or professional volunteer role of making the ask. An introduction from a trusted board member who says "I want you to meet our ED" is worth more than a fumbled ask.

## How to apply

Clarify, in writing, the specific role each board member plays in the major-gifts cultivation cycle for each identified major-gift prospect. Do not default to "ask" as the board member role.

```
Board member role menu (offer each board member the option that fits their relationship):
  1. Identify: "Do you know anyone in your network who might care about this mission?"
  2. Introduce: "Would you be willing to introduce me to [name] — I'll do the rest."
  3. Steward: "Would you be willing to sign this thank-you letter to [name]?"
  4. Host: "Would you be willing to host a small cultivation dinner at your office?"
  5. Co-sign the ask letter: "I'll write it — would you add a personal line and sign alongside me?"
  6. Make the ask (only when: board member has strong personal relationship AND has attended training)

Cultivation meeting prep for board-member involvement:
  — Brief the board member on the prospect's interests and ask history before any meeting
  — The ED or DD attends; the board member introduces; the ED/DD stewards and asks
  — After the meeting, the board member sends a personal follow-up note (the DD drafts it)
```

**Do:**
- Assign board members specific, comfortable roles in the major-gifts cycle rather than a generic "give and get" responsibility.
- Provide one-page briefing sheets before any cultivation meeting that includes a board member.
- Train willing board members in asking before assigning them to ask — never cold.

**Don't:**
- Tell board members they are "responsible for fundraising" without specifying what they are actually expected to do.
- Allow a board member to improvise an ask conversation without a briefing and a prep call.
- Exclude board members from the cultivation cycle because they "won't ask" — there are other valuable roles.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Some board members with strong personal relationships to specific major-gift prospects are natural askers and request the ask role. In those cases, the major-gifts-strategist should support them with a briefing, a gift ask range, and a prepared follow-up — not step back entirely. Capital campaign volunteer committees often operate differently; a peer-to-peer ask from a campaign chair is standard practice in that context.
