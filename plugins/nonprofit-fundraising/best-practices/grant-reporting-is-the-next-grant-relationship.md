# Grant reporting is the next grant relationship

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Nonprofit grants
**Applies to:** `nonprofit-fundraising`

---

## Why this exists

Most development teams treat grant reports as a compliance obligation — something to be filed on time to avoid a problem. This framing leaves the most valuable renewal lever untouched. A report that demonstrates specific outcome data, shows how the funder's priorities were advanced, and surfaces one or two unexpected wins functions as the first move in the next grant cultivation cycle. Funders read reports to decide whether to renew, increase, and recommend the organization to peer funders. A boilerplate report filled with activity counts and budget summaries does none of this; a report written as a narrative of results, with the funder's language and priorities reflected back, does.

## How to apply

Write every grant report as if it is also the opening of the next proposal. Include: (1) a specific outcome tied to the funder's stated priority language, (2) at least one unexpected finding or adaptive response, (3) a one-paragraph "what's next" that frames the case for continued investment.

```
Grant report structure (results-forward format):
  Section 1: Results summary (2–3 sentences, funder-language priority echoed)
    — Lead with the outcome, not the activity: "X children gained Y skill" not "we held Z workshops"
  Section 2: How we adapted (1 paragraph)
    — Shows the funder they invested in a learning org, not just a program
  Section 3: Financial summary (budget vs. actuals with narrative for variances > 10%)
  Section 4: What's next (1 paragraph)
    — Connects the current work to the next-phase need; plants the seed for renewal
    — Mirrors the funder's next RFP priorities if known

Before submitting: verify the report's framing against the original grant letter's stated objectives.
If the objectives changed mid-grant, acknowledge that explicitly — funders respect candor over silence.
```

**Do:**
- Schedule the report write as a cultivation conversation, not a compliance task.
- Review the funder's most recent communications or RFPs before writing, to match current priority language.
- Include a brief relationship-check line: "We'd welcome 20 minutes to discuss how this work connects to your 2027 priorities."

**Don't:**
- Recycle the proposal narrative as the report narrative — the proposal is what you said you would do; the report is what you did and learned.
- File a bare-minimum report for a funder you intend to renew — it signals you view the relationship as transactional.
- Bury the results in Section 4 after pages of activity description.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Some funders have mandatory report templates with fixed sections and word counts; in those cases, use the remaining flexibility (the narrative sections) to apply this rule. Government grants with strict compliance-format requirements are less amenable to narrative framing, but the "what's next" paragraph still fits in most narrative sections.
