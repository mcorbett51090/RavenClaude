# The discount rate is a strategy, not an accident

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Enrollment management / financial aid & net revenue
**Applies to:** `higher-education-administration`

> Advisory operations rule. Discount-rate norms and aid rules are volatile — `[verify-at-use]`; individual packaging belongs to the aid office. No student PII.

---

## Why this exists

The tuition discount rate is the largest single lever on both who enrolls and how much net revenue a class produces — and it is the one most likely to rise silently. Approve a slightly richer package here, close an aid gap there, and the institution's discount rate climbs a point a year with no one ever having decided to trade net revenue for headcount. A bigger class bought with a higher discount rate can produce *less* net tuition revenue than a smaller, better-priced one. If the rate isn't modeled, it isn't managed.

## How to apply

- Model **net tuition revenue** (gross tuition − institutional aid), not gross headcount or gross tuition, at every discount scenario.
- State the **break-even yield** before committing to a discount change.
- Treat the discount rate as a governed number with an owner and a target — reviewed as a strategy, not summed up after the fact.
- Watch year-over-year discount-rate creep as a leading budget indicator (`[verify-at-use]` against the institution's methodology).

**Do:** model net revenue at each scenario; set the rate deliberately with an owner.
**Don't:** let the rate emerge one package at a time with no net-revenue model.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

A one-time strategic discount investment (entering a new market, a deliberate mission-driven access commitment) can rationally raise the rate — *if* it's a decision on the record with a net-revenue model, not drift.
