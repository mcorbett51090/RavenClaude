# The discount rate has a diminishing return

**Status:** Pattern.

**Rule:** As the institutional discount rate rises, yield rises but net revenue per student falls. The
optimum is where the marginal aid dollar stops adding net enrolled revenue. Past that point, more
discount buys volume that loses money.

## Why

Financial aid is a leveraging tool, not generosity or a blunt price cut. Each additional point of
discount yields some incremental students, but it also reduces what every aided student pays. There is
a point where the next dollar of aid recruits students whose net contribution is below the cost to
serve them — growing the class while shrinking the budget. Institutions that chase headcount past this
point post record enrollments and falling net tuition revenue simultaneously.

## What it looks like in practice

- Net tuition revenue is modeled across discount scenarios to locate the diminishing-returns inflection.
- Additional aid is sized against **net** tuition revenue, not gross, and segmented (price- vs.
  fit-sensitive) so it isn't wasted on students who'd enroll anyway.
- Non-aid yield levers (admit timing, engagement, melt prevention) are exhausted before raising the rate.

## Anti-pattern

Raising discount to "hit the enrollment number" without modeling net revenue — filling seats at a loss.
The hook flags aid/discount decisions stated with no net-revenue basis.
