# Retention beats recruitment on ROI

**Status:** Absolute rule.

**Rule:** Recruiting a new student costs far more than retaining an enrolled one, and a retained
student carries multiple years of net revenue. Model the retention-vs-recruitment tradeoff
explicitly before pouring budget into the top of the funnel.

## Why

The cost to recruit and yield a new student — marketing, admissions staff, and leveraging aid — is
large and buys a single year-one enrollment that may melt or withdraw. Retaining an already-enrolled
student costs a fraction of that and secures the remaining years of their net tuition revenue.
Spending on the top of a leaking funnel refills a bucket that keeps draining; closing the leak
compounds.

## What it looks like in practice

- Enrollment budget allocation compares cost-per-new-enrolled-student against cost-per-retained-student
  and the multi-year net revenue each carries.
- When retention is weak, the first dollar goes to retention before recruitment, because it protects
  revenue already won.
- Recruitment gets the marginal dollar only once retention is strong and the funnel is the binding
  constraint.

## Anti-pattern

Increasing the recruitment-marketing budget while year-1→year-2 attrition stays high — paying full
acquisition cost for students who leave before they pay back. Pair every recruitment-spend proposal
with the current retention rate.
