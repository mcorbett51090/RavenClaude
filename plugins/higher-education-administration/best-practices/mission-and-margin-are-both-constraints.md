# Mission and margin are both constraints

**Status:** Absolute rule.

**Rule:** Institutional decisions are bound by mission **and** margin together. Margin alone reduces a
university to a business that happens to teach; mission alone funds indefinite losses until there is no
institution left. Name both, explicitly, in every consequential decision.

## Why

Higher education is a mission-driven enterprise with a hard budget constraint. A program, a price, or a
service that is great for the mission but structurally unaffordable will eventually be cut by financial
reality — usually abruptly and badly. One that is profitable but corrosive to the mission erodes the
thing that makes the institution worth funding. The discipline is to hold both as real constraints and
make the tradeoff *deliberately*, rather than letting one silently dominate.

## What it looks like in practice

- Program and pricing decisions state the margin **and** the mission case, and resolve the tension on
  purpose (e.g., "keep this unprofitable program because it's accreditation-critical, funded from
  cross-subsidy").
- "Mission" is never used as a shield against ever modeling the cost; "margin" is never used to cut
  something load-bearing for the mission without naming what's lost.
- Cross-subsidies between programs are made visible so they're a choice, not an accident.

## Anti-pattern

Either extreme: a spreadsheet-only cut of a mission-critical program, or a mission-only defense of a
chronic money-loser with no plan. Both are failures to hold both constraints at once.
