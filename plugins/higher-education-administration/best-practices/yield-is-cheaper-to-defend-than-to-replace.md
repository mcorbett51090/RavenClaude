# Yield is cheaper to defend than to replace

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Enrollment management / yield & melt
**Applies to:** `higher-education-administration`

> Advisory operations rule. Yield/melt benchmarks are volatile — `[verify-at-use]`. FERPA-aware: cohorts only, no student PII.

---

## Why this exists

Every admit you convert cost the full funnel — the inquiry buy, the recruitment, the application read, the admit decision — before it ever became a deposit. When that deposit melts before census, you don't just lose a student; you lose everything you spent to earn them, and you have to spend it *again* on a brand-new inquiry to replace the seat. A melt-season touch that saves a deposit is almost always cheaper per net student than the next inquiry purchase. Defense beats replacement on unit economics.

## How to apply

- Read the funnel by stage and separate a **yield gap** (admits not depositing) from a **melt gap** (deposits not enrolling) — they have different fixes.
- Fund a **melt-season plan** (communication, support, aid-question resolution) before adding top-of-funnel spend.
- Compare the marginal cost of a *defended* deposit against a *replacement* inquiry before reallocating budget.
- Segment melt by cause (aid, transfer, fit, logistics) and fix the process behind the cluster.

**Do:** defend yield through census; fund the melt-season touch first.
**Don't:** answer a yield/melt gap by reflexively buying more inquiries.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

If the top of the funnel is genuinely too thin to hit the class (not a yield problem — a volume problem), inquiry investment is the right lever. Diagnose the stage first.
