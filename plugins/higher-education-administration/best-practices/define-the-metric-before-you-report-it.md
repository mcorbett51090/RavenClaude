# Define the metric before you report it

**Status:** Absolute rule.

**Rule:** Before any institutional number is reported, its definition is settled: the census date, who
and what counts (headcount vs. FTE, cohort inclusion rules), and the official source of truth. Two
offices reporting different numbers is almost always a definitions problem, not a data problem.

## Why

"Enrollment," "retention," and "graduation rate" are not self-evident — each depends on a date, a
population, and inclusion rules. When those aren't fixed institution-wide, every office computes its
own defensible version, the numbers disagree, and decision-makers stop trusting the data entirely.
Worse, mandated reports (IPEDS, accreditation) require *specific* definitions; a locally-invented one
fails comparability and can misreport. The definition is part of the metric, not a footnote.

## What it looks like in practice

- Canonical definitions (census date, headcount vs. FTE, cohort rules, source of truth) are published
  and reused everywhere.
- Every dashboard metric carries its definition, cohort framing, and official source inline.
- Mandated-reporting cohorts are constructed to the reporting body's exact definitions and documented.

## Anti-pattern

Two cabinet members arriving at a meeting with different enrollment numbers, both "correct," because
nobody pinned the census date or the counting rule. Settle the definition first; argue about the
strategy second.
