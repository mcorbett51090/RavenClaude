# Aid leverages enrollment — spend it deliberately

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Enrollment management / aid leveraging
**Applies to:** `higher-education-administration`

> Advisory operations rule. Aid-leverage response curves are institution- and segment-specific — `[verify-at-use]`; individual packaging belongs to the aid office. No student PII.

---

## Why this exists

Institutional aid is the single largest lever on both *who* enrolls and *net* revenue, and the same dollar can do very different amounts of work depending on where it lands. A dollar placed on a student who was going to enroll anyway is pure discount — foregone revenue for no enrollment gain. The same dollar placed on a responsive admit segment, at the margin where it flips a decision, is leverage. Aid spent as a default package or a reflexive gap-fill quietly maximizes discount rate and minimizes yield-per-dollar — the opposite of the goal.

## How to apply

- Segment admits by **likely yield response to aid**; find where the response curve is steepest (`[verify-at-use]` — institution-specific).
- Ask of every discount decision: does this dollar **change an enrollment decision at the margin**? If not, it's discount, not leverage.
- Model **net tuition revenue** at the resulting discount rate — a leverage move that erodes net revenue isn't a win.
- Route individual packaging to the aid office; this is strategy, not a determination on any student.

**Do:** place aid where it flips a decision; measure yield-per-discount-dollar.
**Don't:** default-package or fill gaps reflexively; aid segments that would enroll anyway.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Need-based aid tied to access mission or regulatory/consent-decree commitments is not a pure leverage calculation — meet the obligation first, then leverage the discretionary aid.
