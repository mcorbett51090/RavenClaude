# Accreditation evidence is continuous, not an event

**Status:** Pattern.

**Rule:** Accreditation evidence is a system maintained year-round — each standard mapped to the
artifacts that demonstrate it, with owners and a refresh cadence — not a self-study assembled in a
panic before the review.

## Why

A review is a snapshot of work the institution should already be doing. When evidence is gathered only
in the run-up, three things happen: gaps surface too late to fix, the self-study consumes enormous
last-minute effort, and the assessment that should drive improvement becomes a paperwork exercise. A
continuous evidence map turns the review into assembly of existing artifacts and — more importantly —
keeps the underlying quality work actually happening between reviews.

## What it looks like in practice

- A living standards-to-artifact map with named owners and refresh cadence.
- Gaps (standards with thin, stale, or missing evidence) audited well before the review window.
- The self-study is compiled from maintained evidence, not reconstructed from scratch.

## Anti-pattern

The pre-review scramble: reconstructing years of assessment from memory and email. The hook flags
student-data handling without a FERPA note; this rule adds that accreditation evidence assembled only
at review time is a process failure, not just a workload spike.
