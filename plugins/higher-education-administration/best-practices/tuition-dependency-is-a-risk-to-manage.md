# Tuition dependency is a risk to manage

**Status:** Pattern.

**Rule:** The share of operating revenue that comes from net tuition is a structural risk metric. A
high tuition-dependency ratio means a small enrollment or discount miss flows straight to the bottom
line. Measure it, stress-test it, and manage it — don't discover it in a bad year.

## Why

A tuition-dependent institution has little buffer: when an entering class comes in 5% short or the
discount rate creeps up, there is no large endowment draw or appropriation to absorb it, so the miss
hits operations directly. Dependency isn't inherently a failure — many fine institutions are
tuition-dependent — but an *unmodeled* dependency is, because the cliff is invisible until the
institution is standing on it. Knowing the ratio and the scenarios converts a latent crisis into a
managed risk.

## What it looks like in practice

- The tuition-dependency ratio (net tuition ÷ total operating revenue) is a tracked board-level metric.
- The budget is stress-tested against enrollment −5%/−10%, discount +3pts, and melt spikes.
- The mitigations (retention, non-aid yield, program portfolio, cost structure) are tied to the stress
  results, in governance language.

## Anti-pattern

Running a tuition-dependent budget on a single optimistic enrollment forecast with no downside
scenario — then treating the shortfall as a surprise. The hook flags aid/discount decisions with no
net-revenue basis; this rule extends that to the whole revenue base.
