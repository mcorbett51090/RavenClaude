## The admissions funnel, defined

| Stage | Definition | Conversion to watch |
|---|---|---|
| Inquiry | expressed interest | inquiry → applicant |
| Applicant | submitted application | applicant → admit |
| Admit | offered admission | admit → deposit |
| Deposit | committed (enrollment deposit) | deposit → enroll |
| Enroll | registered for term | — |
| Melt | deposited but didn't matriculate | a loss stage, not a footnote |

## Net tuition revenue — the only revenue that counts

```
net_tuition_revenue = gross_tuition − institutional_aid (discount)
discount_rate       = institutional_aid ÷ gross_tuition
```

Sticker price funds nothing. A larger class at a higher discount can lower net revenue — the headline
applicants/admits numbers routinely move opposite to the number that pays salaries.

## The two jobs of financial aid

| Aid type | Job | Optimize for |
|---|---|---|
| Need-based | access / mission | affordability for eligible students |
| Merit / leveraging | yield | enrolled net revenue per aid dollar |

A single blunt discount does both jobs badly — over-paying fit-sensitive students who would enroll
anyway, under-serving need-sensitive ones.

## The discount-rate diminishing-returns curve

As discount rises, yield rises but net revenue per student falls. The optimum is where the **marginal
aid dollar stops adding net enrolled revenue**. Past that point, more discount buys volume that loses
money. Model net tuition revenue across discount scenarios to find the inflection.

## Yield levers, non-aid first

Exhaust before reaching for more discount:

1. Admit timing and decision speed
2. Personalized engagement (faculty/student contact, visits)
3. Melt-prevention outreach and onboarding
4. Segment-fit messaging (price- vs. fit-sensitive)

Then, only if needed, size additional aid against **net** tuition revenue.
