# The Engagement Letter Scopes the Work and the Fee

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** US public-accounting / CPA-firm operations
**Applies to:** accounting-firm-cpa

## Why this exists

In public accounting, the engagement letter is the contract. It defines what the firm will do,
what the client will provide, what the deliverable is, and what it costs. Without a signed
engagement letter, any expansion of scope is a verbal agreement — unenforceable, untracked,
and a source of fee disputes, malpractice exposure, and client relationship damage.

The most common path to scope creep and realization problems at CPA firms is work that was
verbally agreed to but not documented: "while you're at it, can you also do...?" Work performed
outside the engagement letter without a written addendum is work that the firm may not be able
to collect for — and work for which it accepted professional responsibility without explicit
client acknowledgment.

## How to apply

**Do:**
- Obtain a signed engagement letter before beginning any work, for every engagement —
  no exceptions for "we've worked together for years" or "it's just a quick thing."
- Include in every engagement letter: scope of services, deliverables, fee (fixed amount or
  rate with estimate), client responsibilities, term, and termination rights.
- Write scope inclusions and exclusions explicitly. "Preparation of Form 1040" is vague —
  list the schedules, the state returns, and what is not included (prior-year amended,
  audit representation, planning).
- When the client asks for work outside the scope, stop and issue a written addendum
  before performing the work. An email confirmation from the client is a minimum;
  a formal addendum is preferred.
- For CAS engagements, define overage triggers (transaction volume thresholds, additional
  entities) in the engagement letter so the mechanism for additional billing is pre-agreed.
- Review engagement letter terms annually for ongoing engagements. Reprice and re-scope if
  the work has grown beyond the original terms.

**Don't:**
- Begin attest fieldwork, return preparation, or CAS services without a signed letter.
- Treat a prior-year engagement letter as valid for the current year without refreshing it
  (scope, fee, and terms may have changed).
- Agree verbally to additional scope without issuing an addendum — "I'll just do it this
  once" becomes the new baseline expectation.
- Set fees without defining exactly what is and is not included. An engagement letter with
  a fee and no scope is not useful when a dispute arises.
- Issue a report (tax return, financial statements, attest opinion) under a scope that was
  materially different from the engagement letter without documenting the change.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- **Emergency / deadline situations:** if a deadline is imminent and a client needs a return
  filed or a report issued before a formal engagement letter can be exchanged, the firm may
  proceed with a written email (or other documented communication) that captures the key
  terms — scope, fee, and authorization — with a formal letter to follow. A retroactive
  engagement letter with the original engagement date is preferable to no letter at all.
  This is an exception to timing, not to the requirement.
- **Routine annual engagements with unchanged scope:** some firms use an annual renewal letter
  that reaffirms prior-year terms by reference. This is acceptable if it explicitly confirms
  that scope and fee are unchanged and the client signs.

## Provenance

Standard professional practice in US public accounting; reinforced by AICPA professional
standards (AU-C 210 for audits, SSARS for reviews/compilations). The fee-scope linkage is
a firm-management best practice, not a codified professional standard, but it is universal
in well-run firms.
