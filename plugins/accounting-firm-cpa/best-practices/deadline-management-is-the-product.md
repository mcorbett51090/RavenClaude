# Deadline Management Is the Product

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** US public-accounting / CPA-firm operations
**Applies to:** accounting-firm-cpa

## Why this exists

In public accounting, the deadline is not a project milestone — it is the deliverable. A tax
return filed one day late (without a valid extension) generates penalties and interest for the
client, and potentially a malpractice claim for the firm. A financial statement issued after
a debt-covenant deadline may cause a technical default. A PBC list that arrives the day before
fieldwork starts means the auditor cannot perform the audit. Missing a deadline is not a quality
problem — it is the product failure.

The operational implication: deadline management must be designed into every workflow, not
treated as a by-product of good intentions. This means filing deadlines are tracked in a system
(not a person's memory), extensions are filed proactively (not reactively), capacity plans
include buffer for exceptions, and client document-chase programs have defined escalation paths
before the deadline — not after it is missed.

## How to apply

**Do:**
- Build and maintain a master deadline calendar for all filing and reporting deadlines
  by engagement type (federal and state), updated annually at the start of each calendar year.
- Flag weekends and holidays that shift filing deadlines — verify the current-year calendar
  from the IRS and state agencies at the start of each season `[verify-at-use]`.
- File extensions proactively for returns where key documents are not in hand 1–2 weeks
  before the original deadline. Do not wait until the deadline date to decide.
- Build deadline tracking into the workflow management tool (Karbon, Canopy, etc.), not
  just into staff members' memories. A deadline that lives only in one person's head is a
  single point of failure.
- For CAS engagements: define close-calendar SLA dates in the engagement letter (e.g.,
  "financial statements delivered by business day 10"). Monitor adherence monthly; flag
  consistent delays as either a staffing problem (firm side) or a client-responsibility
  failure (client side).
- For audits: issue the PBC list at least 3 weeks before fieldwork start. A PBC list issued
  at the start of fieldwork cannot be ready in time to perform the audit.
- Conduct a mid-season review (approximately February 1 for individual tax season) of
  completion percentages vs. target. If behind, trigger levers: overtime, outsourcing,
  extension decisions.

**Don't:**
- Treat extensions as the deadline management strategy. Extensions are a tool for genuinely
  complex or document-incomplete situations; they are not a substitute for a realistic capacity
  plan.
- Use a spreadsheet as the deadline management system if the firm processes more than 50
  returns. A missed row is a missed deadline.
- Rely on client-driven urgency to set preparation priority. Clients who are loudest in April
  may have the simplest returns; complex returns with quiet clients hit the deadline wall silently.
- Agree to accelerated deadlines for attest engagements (e.g., "we need the audit report in
  30 days") without assessing whether the timeline is achievable at the planned staffing level.
  An unreachable deadline plus a signed engagement letter is a malpractice setup.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- **Superseding deadlines:** when a regulatory or lender deadline supersedes the standard tax
  filing deadline (e.g., a bank requiring audited financials by a specific date earlier than the
  standard audit timeline), that deadline takes precedence — but the same design principles
  apply: track it, build buffer, escalate early if at risk.
- **Disaster / force-majeure extensions:** the IRS and state agencies may grant deadline
  extensions in declared disaster areas. Monitor agency announcements; update the deadline
  calendar when official relief is announced `[verify-at-use]`.

## Provenance

Standard practice in US public accounting; the penalty-and-interest consequences of late filing
are defined by IRC and state statutes. The operational prescriptions (proactive extensions, PBC
list timing, mid-season review) are practitioner-derived best practices, not codified standards.
