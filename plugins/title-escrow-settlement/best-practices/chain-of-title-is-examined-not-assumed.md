# Chain of title is examined, not assumed

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Title / examination
**Applies to:** `title-escrow-settlement`

> Advisory operations rule, not legal or title-underwriting advice. Marketability standards and underwriter guidelines are `[verify-at-use]`. No PII.

---

## Why this exists

A title policy insures against defects in the chain of title. If the examiner *assumes* a link instead of tracing it — trusting a prior policy, waving off a name variance, ignoring an open lien "probably paid" — the defect passes silently into an insured policy and becomes a claim. The whole value of examination is that it does not assume; it verifies.

## How to apply

- **Trace every conveyance** from the search period back to a reliable root of title. Confirm each deed conveyed what the next assumed.
- **Reconcile names, marital status, and entity authority** across links — a variance is a defect until resolved, not a coincidence to overlook.
- **Account for every lien and encumbrance explicitly** — satisfied, released, subordinated, insured over, or excepted. Nothing is silently dropped.
- **A prior policy is a lead, not a substitute for examination.** It tells you what a prior examiner saw, not what is true now.
- **Escalate an unclear defect to the underwriter** with your read — never insure past your delegated authority.

**Do:** close every gap or except it knowingly; document the basis for each requirement/exception.
**Don't:** rely on a prior policy; treat a name mismatch as self-evidently the same party; assume an old mortgage was paid without a release.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Search period and root-of-title depth are set by jurisdiction and underwriter standard (`[verify-at-use]`) — the rule is to examine *the required chain thoroughly*, not to trace infinitely. Certain matters are legitimately taken as standard exceptions rather than cured; that is a deliberate decision, not an assumption.
