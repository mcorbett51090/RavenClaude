## Problem

A VP of Sales took the board's 40% growth target, divided it by headcount, and handed every rep an identical quota — a clean top-down number with no capacity model behind it. The catch: a third of the team were ramping reps who'd been carrying full quota since week one, the territories were wildly uneven (some reps inherited a book of named accounts, others got greenfield), and the comp plan had an uncapped accelerator with no clawback. The number was un-makeable from the heads they actually had, and the comp design quietly rewarded all the wrong behavior to compensate.

## Constraints context

- No bottoms-up capacity model existed: ramped reps, ramping reps, and their expected productivity were never reconciled against the board ask — the quota was a wish, not a plan.
- The uncapped accelerator with no clawback incentivized end-of-quarter deal-dumping and bad-fit closes that churned within two quarters — the comp math rewarded behavior the business didn't want.
- Ramping reps on full quota missed by construction, tanked their comp, and several quit — turnover that made the capacity hole deeper each quarter.

## Attempts

- Tried: pushing harder on the same top-down number and adding a SPIF. Failed — a SPIF on an un-makeable quota just concentrated the deal-dumping at quarter-end; the structural capacity gap was untouched.
- Tried: raising the quota again the next year to "make up" for the miss. Failed predictably — a bigger number stapled onto the same (now smaller, post-attrition) headcount missed by more.
- Tried: building the capacity model bottoms-up — (fully-ramped reps + ramping reps × ramp fraction) × expected productivity — reconciling it against the board ask, ramp-adjusting quota for new reps, balancing territories, and capping the accelerator with a clawback on early churn. This worked.

## Resolution

The capacity model showed the board ask exceeded real capacity by roughly two fully-ramped reps' worth — the gap was a hiring/ramp decision, named explicitly, not a number to inflate. Ramp-adjusted quotas stopped new reps missing by construction and cut the attrition that had been deepening the hole. Capping the accelerator and adding a clawback killed the end-of-quarter dumping and the churn-on-arrival closes. The next year's quota, set from capacity and reconciled to the board, landed within tolerance — and the comp plan rewarded the behavior the business actually wanted.

## Lesson

Quota is built bottoms-up from ramped-rep capacity, then reconciled against the board number — never handed down and divided by heads. When capacity and the board ask don't meet, the gap is a staffing/ramp/productivity decision, not a bigger number on the same people. And every comp/quota/territory design is a behavior change before it's math: name what it accidentally rewards (sandbagging, cherry-picking, end-of-quarter dumping, churn-on-arrival closes) before it ships.
