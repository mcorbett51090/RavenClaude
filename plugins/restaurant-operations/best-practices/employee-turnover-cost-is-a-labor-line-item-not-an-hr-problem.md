# Employee Turnover Cost Is a Labor Line Item, Not an HR Problem

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Labor economics
**Applies to:** `restaurant-operations`

---

## Why this exists

Restaurant employee turnover runs at 70–75% annually for hourly staff [unverified — training knowledge] and is typically treated as an HR metric. It is a direct labor cost. Each departure and replacement carries recruiting, onboarding, training, and productivity-ramp costs that do not appear on the labor line but reduce effective labor efficiency and increase training-manager time — which does appear on the labor line. A restaurant replacing 30 positions per year at a $1,500–$3,000 replacement cost per hourly employee [unverified — training knowledge] is spending $45,000–$90,000 in invisible labor-adjacent cost. An operator who can reduce annualized turnover from 100% to 60% recovers a measurable dollar amount — not just a morale improvement.

## How to apply

Quantify turnover cost before recommending retention investments:

```
Turnover cost model (annualized):
  Total hourly positions:                        ______
  Annual turnover rate:                          ______%
  Annual separations:                            positions × turnover % = ______

  Cost per separation (estimate [unverified]):
    Recruiting / posting:                        $______
    Manager interview time (hours × rate):       $______
    Onboarding paperwork / uniforms:             $______
    Training labor (trainer hours × rate):       $______
    Productivity ramp loss (first 30 days):      $______
    Subtotal per separation:                     $______

  Total annual turnover cost:                    separations × cost per separation = $______

  Retention ROI test:
    If a $2/hr raise for top performers reduces turnover by 20%:
      Separations avoided:       ______
      Turnover cost savings:     $______
      Wage cost increase:        top-performer headcount × $2 × annual hours = $______
      Net:                       savings − wage cost = $______
```

**Do:**
- Run the turnover cost model annually and include it in the four-wall review — it contextualizes any retention investment.
- Segment turnover by tenure (< 90 days is the most expensive cohort; reducing 90-day attrition has the highest ROI).
- Track the exit interview data for systematic causes (scheduling, management, pay) — the data is free and actionable.

**Don't:**
- Recommend wage increases as the only retention lever without diagnosing whether scheduling, management quality, or recognition are the actual drivers.
- Dismiss turnover as "industry normal" — an operator at 60% turnover in a 90% turnover market has a measurable competitive cost advantage.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Single-location operators with 5–10 employees where the owner is the primary trainer have a different cost structure — the training cost is the owner's time, which is an opportunity cost but not a cash line. The principle still applies; the model simplifies.
