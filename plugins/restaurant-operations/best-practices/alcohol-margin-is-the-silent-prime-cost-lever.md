# Alcohol Margin Is the Silent Prime Cost Lever

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Beverage economics / prime cost
**Applies to:** `restaurant-operations`

---

## Why this exists

In full-service and casual restaurants with a bar program, alcohol (beer, wine, liquor) typically runs at 18–25% product cost versus 28–35% for food [unverified — training knowledge]. Operators who read prime cost as food + labor, and treat beverage as a single blended line, miss the single highest-margin category on the P&L. Beverage mix management — growing the attachment rate of cocktails and wine to food covers, reducing beverage waste and over-pour, and optimizing the beverage product mix — can move prime cost 1–3 points without touching a single food recipe. Restaurants that treat bar economics as separate from the food P&L leave margin on the table.

## How to apply

Build a standalone beverage P&L alongside the food cost analysis:

```
Beverage cost analysis (per period):
  Total beverage sales ($):                    ______
  Beverage cost of goods ($):                  ______
  Beverage cost % (BCOGS / sales):             ______%  (target: 18–25% [unverified])

  Beverage attachment rate:
    Covers with at least one beverage:         ______%  (target: depends on segment and license)
    Average beverage spend per cover:          $______

  Waste / over-pour indicator:
    Actual BCOGS vs. theoretical beverage cost: ______%  gap
    Target gap: <2% (>3% = over-pour or theft signal)

  Mix optimization:
    Rank beverage items by contribution margin (not cost %)
    Cocktail CM vs. beer CM vs. wine CM — move the mix toward high-CM items
```

Lever sequence:
1. Increase attachment rate (training, menu design, server prompting).
2. Tighten pour controls and waste documentation (jiggers, measured pours, waste log).
3. Engineer the cocktail list toward high-CM items.
4. Review wine pricing — bottle markups are often underused relative to by-the-glass.

**Do:**
- Track beverage cost % separately from food cost % in the weekly P&L — blending them hides the highest-margin line on the menu.
- Conduct a quarterly pour audit (theoretical vs. actual beverage cost by item) to surface over-pour or waste.
- Train servers and bartenders on beverage upsell as a revenue-not-intrusion skill — attachment rate is a training outcome.

**Don't:**
- Cut beverage cost % to below 18% through cheap-ingredient substitution — below that level, guest quality perception drops and volume follows.
- Ignore beverage waste in the comp/void/waste control system — beverage comps and over-pours are often the least-tracked cash leak.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Beer-and-wine-only licenses, dry counties, or concepts without meaningful alcohol sales (fast-casual, QSR) have no beverage program to optimize; in those cases, non-alcoholic beverage economics apply but the margin lever is much smaller. For beverage-forward concepts (cocktail bars, wine bars) where beverage IS the P&L, the analysis inverts — food is the attachment sale.
