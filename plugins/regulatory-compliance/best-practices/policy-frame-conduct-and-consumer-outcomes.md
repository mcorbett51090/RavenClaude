# Frame conduct and consumer-outcome obligations as outcomes, not box-ticking clauses

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Policy authoring — conduct / consumer duty
**Applies to:** `regulatory-compliance`

---

## Why this exists

Conduct and consumer-protection regimes `[verify-at-build — e.g. UK Consumer Duty, fair-treatment / market-conduct rules; the specific regime varies by jurisdiction]` are written as **outcomes the firm must deliver**, not as procedures the firm must perform — and policy that translates them into a checklist of clauses misses the point a conduct examiner is testing. "We disclosed the fee" is a box ticked; "the customer understood the fee and it represented fair value" is the outcome the regime actually requires. The recurring failure is drafting conduct policy in the same prescriptive voice as an operational procedure, so it reads as compliant but produces no evidence that the *outcome* was achieved. Conduct obligations also tend to be principle-level and jurisdiction-stable at the top (treat customers fairly) while the *evidence expectations* diverge sharply by regime — which is exactly the layer this rule asks the writer to get right.

## How to apply

Draft to the required outcome, then name the evidence that the outcome was delivered — not just that the step was performed:

```
Obligation        the conduct/consumer rule, cited to the regime  [verify-at-build — regime varies]
Required OUTCOME   state it as a customer outcome ("the customer understood X", "the product offered fair value")
Operational steps  what the firm does to produce that outcome (this is procedure, kept separate)
Outcome evidence   what proves the OUTCOME (not just the step) — testing results, complaint trends,
                   fair-value assessments, target-market reviews, customer-understanding checks
Governance         who owns the outcome at board/committee level + the MI that reports on it
```

Conduct evidence is outcome-shaped: complaint-root-cause trends, fair-value/target-market assessments, customer-understanding testing — not a register of disclosures sent.

**Do:**
- State the required *outcome* in customer terms, then map operational steps and the evidence the outcome was achieved.
- Tie conduct obligations to board/committee ownership and the management information that reports on outcomes.
- Keep the principle (treat customers fairly) stable across jurisdictions; adapt the evidence expectations per regime.

**Don't:**
- Translate a conduct obligation into a disclosure checklist and call it covered — that evidences the step, not the outcome.
- Assume one jurisdiction's consumer-duty evidence bar applies in another (house opinion #12).
- Treat "no complaints" as evidence of good outcomes — absence of complaints is not presence of fair value.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- **Pure prudential/solvency obligations** (capital, reserving) are not conduct outcomes — don't force an outcomes frame onto a numeric requirement; those map to the reporting and controls disciplines.
- **B2B / wholesale-only firms** may fall outside a given consumer-duty regime's scope `[verify-at-build — scope tests are jurisdiction-specific]` — scope the applicability before drafting; not every firm owes consumer-duty outcomes.
- **Legal-opinion gate** — whether a specific conduct obligation legally applies to the firm routes to counsel; the outcome-framing and evidence design continue once scope is confirmed.
