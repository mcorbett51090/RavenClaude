# Customer success frameworks — the anchor methodologies a PSM operates under

> **Last reviewed:** 2026-05-21. Research-distilled reference. Sources: TSIA publications, Gainsight publications, Reichheld / Bain (NPS lineage), Dixon et al. (CES lineage), Bessemer / KeyBanc / High Alpha benchmark reports, peer-reviewed academic critique (Baehre et al. 2021 JAMS). Refresh when: (a) TSIA's DARE framework either gets traction or stalls, (b) a new SaaS benchmark report changes the NRR/GRR consensus materially, (c) Bain or Gainsight publishes a substantively new methodology, or (d) academic literature on NPS predictive validity moves further.

This file is the **conceptual anchor** for everything else in the plugin. The metrics glossary names the numbers; the segment-fundamentals file names the buyers; this file names the frameworks the PSM operates from when she answers "what's the play here." It is *deliberately not vendor-neutral* — Gainsight is the dominant practitioner voice, TSIA is the dominant industry-research voice, and Bain owns the loyalty-measurement lineage. The honest position: pair the lenses; don't let any one of them be the only voice.

## 1. TSIA's LAER (and the proposed successor, DARE)

**LAER** — Land, Adopt, Expand, Renew — was introduced as TSIA's customer-engagement model in *Technology-as-a-Service Playbook* (Thomas Lah & J.B. Wood, TSIA Press, 2016) and has been the dominant industry-wide engagement framework for technology services ever since.

- **Land** — initial sale / contract close.
- **Adopt** — the customer reaches productive use; this is where CSM lives.
- **Expand** — additional product, additional seats, additional use cases (PSM + AE collaboration).
- **Renew** — contract re-signing; the lagging indicator everything else serves.

**TSIA's structural prescription**: CSM, Renewals, and Expansion are *distinct disciplines* and should usually be staffed (and compensated) separately. The CSM owns Adopt; a Renewals Manager owns Renew; an Account Manager or AE owns Expand. Conflating roles confuses the comp model and the customer relationship.

> Sources: TSIA, "Re-Thinking Your Customer Engagement Model with LAER" (https://www.tsia.com/blog/re-thinking-your-customer-engagement-model-with-laer); Lah & Wood, *Technology-as-a-Service Playbook* (TSIA Press, 2016), ISBN 978-0986046230.

**DARE — Design, Activate, Realize, Evolve** — is TSIA's publicly-proposed successor framework for the AI era. **Status as of 2026-05:** TSIA's framing, not yet industry consensus. The shift TSIA describes is from a sales-funnel-shaped customer journey (Land → Adopt → Expand → Renew) to a value-engineering-shaped one (Design the outcome → Activate the customer → Realize value → Evolve the relationship). Treat DARE as emerging vendor framing; **don't anchor a partner-facing presentation on it yet**.

> Source: TSIA, "From LAER to DARE: Why the AI Era Demands a New Customer Engagement Model" (https://www.tsia.com/blog/laer-to-dare-ai-new-customer-engagement-model).

**EdTech overlay:** TSIA's research base is enterprise tech. LAER transfers reasonably well to higher-ed and corporate L&D but less cleanly to K-12 — the "Adopt" phase in K-12 is gated by teacher professional development (PD), curriculum integration, and district IT coordination, none of which look like enterprise-IT adoption cycles. Don't promise a 90-day K-12 adoption motion modeled on enterprise LAER timelines.

## 2. Gainsight's "Customer Success Elements"

Gainsight's operating methodology is the **Customer Success Elements** framework — a prescriptive set of practice areas (Adoption Management, Renewal Management, Expansion Management, Advocate Engagement, plus others) combining people, process, and platform. It is the most widely-deployed CS operating model in 2026 by reach, in part because Gainsight is the market-leading CS platform vendor.

> Source: Gainsight, "Gainsight Elements: The Science of Customer Success" (https://www.gainsight.com/blog/gainsight-elements-science-customer-success/).

**Anchor publications worth knowing about:**

- Mehta, N., Steinman, D., Murphy, L. — *Customer Success: How Innovative Companies Are Reducing Churn and Growing Recurring Revenue* (Wiley, 2016). The book that introduced the modern customer-success vocabulary to a broad audience.
- Mehta, N., Pickens, A. — *The Customer Success Economy: Why Every Aspect of Your Business Model Needs a Paradigm Shift* (Wiley, April 2020). The thesis that CS isn't a function inside a SaaS company; the entire business model is downstream of CS.

**Gainsight's annual Customer Success Index** — the 2025 edition is titled "Outcomes Over Everything" — is the most-cited industry-survey artifact even though Gainsight is a vendor. Useful as practitioner signal; **not independent research**. The discipline is to pair Gainsight's framing with TSIA's industry-association research and Bain's loyalty-research lineage so the PSM isn't reading one vendor's worldview as gospel.

> Source: Gainsight, "Customer Success Index 2025: Outcomes Over Everything" (https://www.gainsight.com/resource/customer-success-index-2025/).

**Pitfall (specific to Gainsight-cited claims):** Gainsight publishes internal-customer benchmarks like "Elements-maturity correlates with +7-13% gross retention and +14-33% net retention." These are Gainsight's benchmarking of Gainsight customers — a useful directional signal, but not the causal evidence the field would accept. Cite them as such.

## 3. Value Realization (TSIA), Net Promoter 3.0 / Earned Growth (Bain)

The two dominant outcome-measurement lenses in 2026 are:

### Value Realization (TSIA)

TSIA defines **Value Realization** as the *continual* value the customer derives from adopting a supplier's solution — measured in quantified business outcomes (revenue lift, productivity, risk reduction). TSIA treats it as the design center of modern XaaS offer engineering: outcomes are designed up front, not discovered post-sale.

A rigorous **Value Realization Plan (VRP)** in current TSIA practice has these load-bearing pieces:

1. Named business outcomes with measurable KPI targets.
2. Baseline measurement *before* deployment.
3. Defined value categories tied to compelling offers (productivity / revenue / risk / cost / experience).
4. Consumption + adoption analytics that show whether the customer is on track.
5. Periodic Executive Business Reviews (EBRs) that re-baseline.

> Sources: TSIA, "The XaaS Value Stream and SaaS Systems Thinking" (https://www.tsia.com/blog/the-xaas-value-stream); TSIA, "Outcome Selling and Renewal Value Management" (https://www.tsia.com/blog/outcome-selling-and-renewal-value-management); TSIA, *State of Technology Services 2025* (https://www.tsia.com/ebooks/state-of-tech-services-2025).

### Net Promoter 3.0 / Earned Growth Rate (Bain)

Reichheld's original "The One Number You Need to Grow" (HBR, Dec 2003) introduced NPS. Reichheld, Darnell & Burns subsequently published **"Net Promoter 3.0"** (HBR, Nov-Dec 2021) and the accompanying book *Winning on Purpose* (Harvard Business Review Press, Dec 2021). The substantive update: **Earned Growth Rate** — an accounting-audited financial metric (revenue from existing customers + their referrals, divided by total revenue) that complements the survey-based NPS score with a metric the CFO can validate.

> Sources: Reichheld, "The One Number You Need to Grow" (https://hbr.org/2003/12/the-one-number-you-need-to-grow); Reichheld, Darnell & Burns, "Net Promoter 3.0" (https://hbr.org/2021/11/net-promoter-3-0); Bain, "Net Promoter 3.0" (https://www.bain.com/insights/net-promoter-3-0/).

**Why the two lenses don't conflict:** Value Realization is the *forward* discipline (what outcomes are we engineering, are we on track). Net Promoter 3.0 / Earned Growth is the *backward* validation (did the customer's loyalty and referral behavior actually produce revenue). A mature CS function uses both.

**EdTech overlay:** Value Realization is the framework that most needs translation for EdTech. Outcomes are pedagogical (learning gains, course completion, time-to-credential, instructor satisfaction, equity metrics) and frequently non-monetary — not the productivity / revenue-lift framing TSIA assumes for enterprise tech. For corporate L&D, outcomes look more SaaS-like (skill acquisition, certification, internal mobility). For K-12, outcomes are constrained by what districts can legally and ethically measure on minors and what's defensible under ESSA Tier evidence levels (Tier I-IV).

## 4. Loyalty / experience metrics — CES, CSAT, NPS

The triangulation in current practice (2026):

| Metric | Best for | Origin |
|---|---|---|
| **CES** (Customer Effort Score) | *Transactional* service-recovery moments | Dixon, Freeman & Toman, "Stop Trying to Delight Your Customers," HBR Jul-Aug 2010 |
| **CSAT** (Customer Satisfaction) | *Episodic* satisfaction with a specific interaction | Pre-dates NPS; no single origin |
| **NPS** / Earned Growth | *Relational* loyalty / expansion likelihood | Reichheld 2003; updated 2021 |

CES introduced a contrarian thesis: in service-recovery contexts, *reducing customer effort* predicts loyalty better than *delighting customers* does. The original research was a CEB study of 75,000+ customers; Dixon, Freeman, and Toman published the playbook in *The Effortless Experience* (Portfolio/Penguin, 2013).

> Sources: Dixon et al., "Stop Trying to Delight Your Customers" (https://hbr.org/2010/07/stop-trying-to-delight-your-customers); Dixon, Toman, DeLisi, *The Effortless Experience* (2013).

**Academic critique of NPS — Baehre, O'Dwyer, O'Malley & Lee (2021)** published in *Journal of the Academy of Marketing Science*. Using five years of sportswear data, they found that the *brand-health* variant of NPS (surveying potential customers, not just existing) predicts sales growth, but the original customer-only NPS does not robustly predict growth. Methodological concerns: arbitrary cut points (why is 7 "passive"?), the large passive group is discarded, single-question fragility. The peer-reviewed consensus is skeptical of NPS as a predictive metric, especially cross-segment or cross-region.

> Source: Baehre, O'Dwyer, O'Malley & Lee (2021), *Journal of the Academy of Marketing Science* (https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11747-021-00790-2).

**The defensible practitioner position**: NPS is fine *as a trend indicator on a consistent population* — track its direction across surveys, segment-by-segment, and pair it with a transactional metric (CES) for friction diagnosis. Don't compare NPS across segments or countries; cultural and category variance swamps the signal.

**EdTech overlay:** NPS is structurally weak in K-12 because "would you recommend?" assumes peer-to-peer purchasing power that teachers and admins often don't have. CSAT after PD sessions and CES on workflow friction (rostering, content authoring, gradebook integration) travel better. Higher-ed and corporate L&D can use NPS more conventionally, but segment by persona (faculty vs. admin vs. student / employee vs. manager).

## 5. Health-score methodology

The 2025-2026 consensus is **hybrid scoring** — rule-based composites layered with ML-predictive churn signals — not a winner-takes-all shift to pure ML. Composite scores typically span four dimensions:

1. **Product usage** (breadth, depth, frequency)
2. **Sentiment** (CSAT / CES / NPS / support-ticket tone)
3. **Financial** (payment status, expansion signal, contract length)
4. **Relationship** (sponsor strength, named-champion tenure, exec engagement)

Vendor-published numbers like "multi-component scores are ~34% more accurate than single-dimension" are widely cited but trace to Gainsight blog content rather than peer-reviewed research. **Treat as plausible heuristic, not finding.**

> Sources: TSIA, "Customer Health Scoring: The Shift Toward Predictive Models" (https://www.tsia.com/blog/customer-health-scoring-predictive-models); ChurnZero, "Customer Health Scores in the Age of AI" (https://churnzero.com/blog/customer-health-scores-in-the-age-of-ai/).

**The non-negotiable design rules** (cross-cutting house opinion, also captured in [`partner-health-score-drift.md`](partner-health-score-drift.md)):

- **Decay matters more than weighting.** A signal from 6 months ago is not a signal today. Every component has a half-life. Most teams over-tune weights and under-design decay.
- **Show your work.** A composite score that says "yellow" without naming the 2-3 components that dropped is useless to the PSM and unconvincing to the partner.
- **Calibrate quarterly against outcomes.** Correlation of (final score) × (actual renewal outcome) is the only honest test. Below ~0.5, the score is broken.
- **Renewal-risk-score separate from health-score.** Health is "are they in a good state generally"; renewal risk is "will this specific contract close." A green-health customer can have high renewal risk if the champion just left; a yellow customer can have low renewal risk if it's mid-term on a multi-year. Maintain both; surface disagreements as the action signal.

**EdTech overlay:** Standard SaaS health-score decay breaks in EdTech because of the **academic calendar**. A flat July K-12 customer is not unhealthy; a flat October customer is. Without calendar-aware decay, every K-12 partner looks like a churn risk every summer. See [`partner-health-score-drift.md`](partner-health-score-drift.md) for the full recalibration playbook.

## 6. Onboarding and time-to-value

The **"aha moment"** construct — the point at which a user first experiences the core value of the product — is folk-canonical in PLG circles. Origin commonly credited to the Facebook growth team ("7 friends in 10 days") and to Sean Ellis's writing. **Field caveat:** specific numbers like "60-70% of churn happens in the first 90 days" or "users hitting aha within 3 days are 90% more likely to be active" recur in vendor blogs but trace to single-source claims, not validated benchmarks. Cite the construct; don't cite the specific multipliers.

**Time-to-Value (TTV) and Time-to-First-Value (TTFV)** are the operational proxies:

- **TTFV** — time from purchase to the *first* meaningful value moment. Vendor-side instrumented (first key in-app action) vs. partner-side defined (the partner's success criterion). The two often disagree; the *partner's* definition is the one that drives renewal.
- **TTV** — time to *full* anticipated value, often months into the relationship.

**Pitfall:** activation as a proxy for value — logging in is not value. Define the outcome event with the partner, not internally.

> Sources: Vitally, "What is Time to Value (TTV)?" (https://www.vitally.io/post/what-is-time-to-value-ttv); Totango, "Time to Value (TTV)" (https://www.totango.com/glossary/time-to-value-ttv).

**EdTech overlay:** Define **persona-specific TTFV**. For a teacher: first formative-assessment dashboard insight (perhaps 6 weeks in). For a student: a single productive class period. For a district admin (the buyer): the first usage report. Standard SaaS "Day 0 = signup" cohorting will mislead — cohorts in EdTech should be anchored to the **start of the in-service / PD week** or the first day of the term, not the contract signature date.

## 7. NRR / GRR benchmarks — what's industry consensus in 2026

**Public SaaS median NRR ~110%**, best-in-class >120% (High Alpha / OpenView 2024 Benchmarks Report). **Private SaaS** (KeyBanc/Sapphire 2024 survey, n=104 companies, median $26M ARR): NRR ~101%, top quartile 108-110%; **GRR median ~87%**, top quartile >92%. **By ACV segment** (per High Alpha aggregation, single-source — directional only): Enterprise (>$100K ACV) ~118%, Mid-Market ($25-100K) ~108%, SMB (<$25K) ~97%.

> Sources: 2024 SaaS Benchmarks Report, High Alpha (https://www.highalpha.com/saas-benchmarks/2024); KeyBanc/Sapphire 2024 (via SaaSletter analysis https://www.saasletter.com/p/2024-keybanc-sapphire-saas-benchmarks); CFI, "GRR vs NRR" (https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/valuation/grr-vs-nrr-saas-retention-metrics/).

**EdTech divergence — flagged honestly:** There is no clean public EdTech-segment NRR/GRR benchmark dataset as of 2026-05. EdSurge, LearnPlatform, Tyton Partners, and HolonIQ publish EdTech market data, but not segment-specific retention medians comparable to the SaaS benchmark reports. The structural reasons EdTech NRR likely runs lower than horizontal SaaS:

1. **Annual renewals dominate** — multi-year is the exception, not the norm in K-12.
2. **Expansion within a single district is capped by enrollment.** NRR ceilings are lower unless cross-selling modules or expanding into adjacent buildings / departments.
3. **Summer creates a usage trough** that breaks generic engagement-driven health scoring.
4. **Annual budget appropriation** (especially K-12) makes the renewal a re-purchase decision, not an automatic continuation.

The PSM-side discipline: treat horizontal SaaS as the *floor*, adjust for these dynamics, and don't promise board-level NRR projections based on horizontal SaaS benchmarks alone.

**CAC payback period** has overtaken LTV/CAC as the primary efficiency metric in 2024-2026 — cash matters more in a higher-rate environment. Bessemer's tiered guidance is the most-cited rule: **<18 months "good," <12 months "better," <6 months "best."** EdTech CAC payback runs longer than horizontal SaaS because of procurement / board-approval cycles, lower K-12 ACVs, and seasonal sales windows; adjust working assumptions upward 6-12 months.

> Source: Bessemer Venture Partners, "Cloud Computing Metrics" (https://www.bvp.com/atlas/cloud-computing-metrics).

## 8. The honest position — pair the lenses

No single framework owns the field as of 2026:

- **Gainsight** owns the operating playbook (Elements, Customer Success Index, Scorecards methodology). Heavy market presence; not neutral.
- **TSIA** owns the outcome-engineering lens (LAER, DARE, Value Realization, services-industry research). Member-research-funded, less product-coupled.
- **Bain / Reichheld** owns the loyalty-measurement lineage (NPS, Earned Growth Rate, *Winning on Purpose*). Long-tenured, financially-validated.
- **Dixon / CEB (now Gartner)** owns the effort-friction lens (CES, *The Effortless Experience*). Service-recovery-anchored.
- **Academic literature** (Baehre, Keiningham, Cooil, Dawes) is the critical voice — skeptical of NPS as a single number; supportive of triangulated measurement.

A mature PSM treats these as a toolkit, not as competing religions. The framework picked for a given conversation should match the audience: TSIA-Value-Realization with a CFO, Gainsight-Elements with a CS Ops leader, NPS-Earned-Growth with a board, CES with a product-friction-debugging session.
