## 1. The annual budget cycle by segment

### K-12 (the dominant case for this plugin)

- **Fiscal year:** July 1 – June 30 (most US states) [verified — California Department of Education](https://www.cde.ca.gov/fg/fi/ir/budgetstatus.asp)
- **Budget-build window:** typically **January – March** (practitioner consensus per AASA "School Budgets 101"; primary AASA source confirms "spring" but not the exact Jan-Mar window)
- **Board approval window:** late April – June (boards typically meet monthly)
- **PSM start-renewal clock:** **120-180 days before contract end, not 90.** The 90-day SaaS-industry default misses the K-12 budget cycle.

> **Why 120-180 days for K-12?** For a June-expiring contract, a 90-day clock starts in March — *after* the January-March budget-build is essentially closed. By the time the PSM opens the conversation, the line item is already either in or out of the proposed budget. Practitioner synthesis from the budget-cycle primary sources; not a single cited rule.

### Higher education

- **Fiscal year:** July 1 – June 30 most public institutions; private institutions vary
- **Distinction:** higher-ed institutions often *capitalize software* (multi-year amortization) which makes multi-year contracts more administratively friendly than in K-12
- **PSM start-renewal clock:** **120 days minimum.** Provost / CFO turnover risk + accreditor cycles + faculty senate involvement on academic-affecting changes all extend the timeline.

### Corporate L&D

- **Fiscal year:** highly variable (Jan-Dec, Apr-Mar, Jul-Jun, Oct-Sep all common in US enterprise)
- **Decision motion:** CHRO approves; CLO / L&D director builds and presents; budget envelope often shifts at last forecast
- **PSM start-renewal clock:** **90 days standard.** But: confirm fiscal-year-end per account at engagement start — corporate fiscal-year variance is the main thing that breaks generic SaaS renewal timing.

> Sources: [AASA School Budgets 101](https://www.aasa.org/docs/default-source/resources/reports/school-budgets-101.pdf), [K12 Prospects](https://www.k12prospects.com/understanding-school-budgets-when-and-how-schools-buy/), [Synthesia L&D budget primer](https://www.synthesia.io/post/ld-budget), [Tambellini Group higher-ed renewal](https://www.thetambellinigroup.com/strategies-for-successful-software-renewals-in-higher-education/)

## 2. Multi-year discount math — and why K-12 is the exception

### The SaaS-industry rule

- **Typical SaaS multi-year discount:** 10-20% total for 2-3 year deals
- **Per-additional-year increment:** ~5% (common rule of thumb)

> Sources: [The SaaS CFO multi-year discount guide](https://www.thesaascfo.com/multi-year-saas-discounts/), [SaaStr "How Big a Discount"](https://www.saastr.com/how-big-a-discount-should-i-give-for-multi-year-deals/)

### Why K-12 is the exception

**Annual appropriation principle:** US public school districts generally cannot legally bind funds beyond the current fiscal year. Multi-year EdTech contracts in K-12 are typically written as "subject to annual appropriation" — they are **3-year price holds, not 3-year commitments.** The customer can walk at any fiscal-year boundary.

**Practical implication:** A "3-year contract" in K-12 means "we promised the price for 3 years; the district promised nothing." Higher-ed and corporate L&D *can* genuinely commit multi-year — K-12 cannot.

> Sources: [NationGraph K-12 procurement primer](https://www.nationgraph.com/post/education-technology-companies-k12-procurement) (practitioner) + the annual-appropriation principle is a well-established public-finance norm. State-by-state legal verification before quoting a 3-year K-12 commitment in writing.

### Annual price escalator clauses

- **CPI-linked escalator** is the most common index in SaaS contracts; clauses typically structured as **CPI + 3-5%**
- **The 2024-2025 reality:** SaaS prices outpaced CPI substantially — [CFO Dive citing Gartner](https://www.cfodive.com/news/stubbornly-high-saas-prices-outpace-cpi-inflation/699683/): IT budgets grew 2.8% while SaaS vendors hiked 9-25%
- **Sources:** [NPI on CPI in IT contracts](https://www.npifinancial.com/blog/what-you-need-to-know-about-it-contract-renewals-and-the-consumer-price-index-cpi), [SaaStr 2025 price-surge breakdown](https://www.saastr.com/the-great-price-surge-of-2025-a-comprehensive-breakdown-of-pricing-increases-and-the-issues-they-have-created-for-all-of-us/)

### Honest NPV framing

A 3-year deal at 15% total discount saves the customer ~5% NPV vs. annual — but locks them into a 3-year window that may see leadership turnover, product strategy change, or competitor displacement.

**Specifically in K-12:** **23% of the 500 largest US districts had superintendent turnover in 2024-25** [K-12 Dive](https://www.k12dive.com/news/the-average-length-of-current-superintendent-tenures-54-years/815908/). The lock-in cuts both ways — and the new superintendent may not honor the prior administration's commitments anyway, given the annual-appropriation principle.

## 3. Price-increase conversations — the highest-risk renewal lever

### The headline data point

**71% of survey respondents cited price increases as the #1 reason for loss of customers** — [Recurly Customer Churn Research, verified 2026-05-21](https://recurly.com/research/churn-rate-benchmarks/). Caveat: the methodology page (sample size, demographics) is on a separate Recurly resource; treat the 71% as load-bearing but verify the underlying study before citing in a vendor's own quote.

### The Gainsight / ChurnZero / TSIA practitioner playbook

- **Lead with value delivered, not the price line.** The "value-justifies-the-increase" framing outperforms generic announcements.
- **"We held price for existing customers"** is most credible when paired with a visible price increase for *new* customers — otherwise it reads as gaslighting.
- **Personalized, segmented price-increase comms** outperform generic announcements (Gainsight cites ~60% better response rate — single-vendor figure, methodology not visible; treat as directional).

> Sources: [ChurnZero renewal playbook PDF](https://info.churnzero.net/hubfs/Best%20Practices/Playbook%20Library%20-%20Renewal%20-%20High-Touch%20Customers.pdf), [Gainsight Customer Success](https://www.gainsight.com/customer-success/), [TSIA State of Customer Growth and Renewal 2025](https://www.tsia.com/blog/state-of-customer-growth-and-renewal-2025)

### EdTech-specific price-increase considerations

**K-12 specifics:**
- Mid-year contract increases are **generally not possible** because funds were appropriated at a specific amount for the fiscal year (AASA confirms the annual-appropriation principle)
- A price increase landing inside an existing fiscal year means the partner has to absorb it or breach — neither is a renewal-friendly position
- **PSM defense:** any price increase must be communicated **before** the partner's budget-build window (so before January for July-1 fiscal years)

**Higher-ed specifics:**
- Multi-year capital planning gives the institution more flexibility to absorb a one-time bump
- Faculty senate scrutiny on price increases for academic-affecting tools is real

**Corporate L&D specifics:**
- CHRO budget envelopes are pre-set quarterly; a renewal hitting outside that envelope gets cut, not absorbed
- Confirm budget-cycle timing before quoting any increase

## 4. The K-12 board-approval pattern

### The mechanics

- **Most district boards meet monthly**
- **Contracts above the state/local threshold require board approval.** Thresholds vary: commonly $5K-$50K informal; **California: $114,800 effective January 1, 2025** [CDE primary](https://www.cde.ca.gov/fg/ac/co/bidthreshold2025.asp); Massachusetts: $50K for quotes, $100K for formal bid
- **School boards typically use a "consent agenda"** — multiple items approved with a single motion — unless a board member pulls the item for discussion

> Sources: [Diligent school-board agenda template](https://www.diligent.com/resources/blog/school-board-agenda-template-and-sample), [F3 Law bidding guide](https://www.f3law.com/insights/guide-to-bidding-and-contracting-for-school-districts-and-community-college-districts/), [ERIC open-public-meetings guide](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED521600.pdf)

### What the PSM needs to give the champion before the board meeting

A renewal that goes to a board needs a **1-page artifact in the district's language**:

- **Per-student cost** (always — never just total contract value)
- **FTE time saved** OR equivalent productivity metric
- **Outcome data** (proficiency growth, attendance, certification — segment-appropriate)
- **Reference letters** from peer districts
- **Risk language** (FERPA / data-protection rider confirmation, sub-processor list, SOC 2 / HIPAA where applicable)

### The "pulled from consent" warning sign

If a board member has a question, the renewal item gets pulled from consent → becomes a discussion item → the vote is no longer routine. **Warning signs that the item is at risk of being pulled:**

- Named decision-maker hasn't been to a QBR in two quarters
- CFO line-item question goes unanswered through the budget cycle
- Champion can't articulate ROI in district's language
- A previous incident (data exposure, comms misstep, parent complaint) is unresolved

## 5. Renewal QBR vs. regular QBR

The renewal QBR runs **90-120 days before contract end** and shifts focus:

| Dimension | Regular QBR | Renewal QBR |
|---|---|---|
| Time horizon | Last quarter + next quarter | Last year + next year |
| Content lead | Usage, adoption, near-term objectives | Outcome story + the case for next year |
| Decision-maker presence | Operational stakeholders | **Named decision-maker MUST be confirmed alive in role** |
| Multi-year framing | n/a | Pitch only when appropriate (see §2 K-12 caveat) |
| Action items | Operational | Pre-board / pre-budget-build artifacts |

**The single highest-value pre-renewal QBR action: confirm the named decision-maker hasn't changed.** Realm CS research [via Realm](https://www.withrealm.com/blog/what-is-a-quarterly-busines-review): "Churn jumps to 25% when the decision-maker leaves versus just 8% when they stay." Given K-12's 23% superintendent turnover rate, **confirming decision-maker continuity at every QBR (not just the renewal QBR) is non-negotiable.**

## 6. Expansion during renewal

### The discipline

- **Bottom-quartile adoption → no expansion ask** (the data-platform house opinion #10 from edtech-partner-success CLAUDE.md)
- **Expansion fires only when the partner has earned value, not when the quarter needs revenue**

### TSIA finding worth knowing

TSIA finds that **selling expansions off the renewal cycle has a positive impact on renewal rates** — meaning expansion *attached to* renewal must be handled carefully. The renewal conversation is for renewal; expansion conversations land cleaner when they're separately staged.

> Source: [TSIA State of Customer Growth and Renewal 2025](https://www.tsia.com/blog/state-of-customer-growth-and-renewal-2025)

### Land-and-expand patterns in K-12 EdTech (highest-to-lowest velocity)

1. **New schools within an existing district** — already on the contract, already through procurement. Expansion is a usage / seat add.
2. **New modules to existing users** — usually requires a budget amendment but no new board approval if under threshold.
3. **New user roles** (admin, parent, paraprofessional tiers) — slowest, often requires new training + curriculum-side buy-in.

**Confidence: practitioner synthesis** — no single primary source ranks these for K-12 specifically.

## 7. Competitive procurement at renewal

### Incumbent RFP win rates

**60-90% in non-federal competitive procurements** — [Capture Planning](https://captureplanning.com/articles/winning-a-procurement-that-is-wired.cfm), [Procurement Cube](https://procurementcube.org/selling-to-procurement-win-less-than-4-2-of-rfps-or-more-than-50-your-choice/).

**Implication:** Even if the district issues an RFP at renewal, the incumbent is still favored. But the **price ceiling drops** because competitors will bid lower to win in.

### When does competitive procurement get triggered?

- **K-12:** State procurement rules + threshold-triggered competitive bidding (e.g., California's $114,800 threshold). Above threshold = formal bid mandatory.
- **Higher-ed:** RFPs at renewal are less common — incumbents in ERP and LMS often run 7-10 years between RFPs
- **Corporate:** rarely RFP at renewal; usually direct negotiation

### Defensive plays when an RFP is opened at K-12 renewal

1. **Get on the RFP-writing team** (or at least on the requirements call)
2. **Make the spec match the differentiated capability** — the district often wants something specific
3. **Supply reference districts before the RFP drops** — the case-study layer
4. **Price-protect by offering renewal + a longer-term price hold** in the proposal

## 8. State data-privacy laws that re-trigger at renewal

The Big Three K-12 student-privacy regimes all require contract work at renewal:

| State | What re-triggers at renewal |
|---|---|
| **NY Ed Law §2-d** | Data-protection rider must be re-attached. On contract expiration without renewal, vendor must assist data export then securely delete protected information. [NY OGS contract guidance](https://ogs.ny.gov/system/files/documents/2022/05/2022-15-how-your-contract-can-meet-school-vendor-responsibilities-for-protecting-student-information.pdf) |
| **IL SOPPA** | Districts must post each vendor contract publicly **within 10 days of signing** + update the published list twice per year. A renewal that changes any data-handling term requires re-posting. [CPS SOPPA page](https://www.cps.edu/about/policies/student-online-personal-protection-act/) |
| **CA SOPIPA** | Applies whether or not a contract is in place; requires deletion/transfer of PII within 60 days of termination. [CA SB 1177](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=201320140SB1177) |

### State-approved vendor lists — the silent killer

Many states (Massachusetts's Approved Bid Vendors program is the documented example) restrict districts to buying from **approved cooperative contracts or state-approved lists**. If an incumbent **drops off the list between contracts**, the district often *cannot* renew without going back through cooperative procurement.

> Sources: [MA pre-qualified vendor list](https://www.doe.mass.edu/sfss/partnerships/prequalified-vendors-list.xlsx), [OMNIA Partners cooperative purchasing](https://www.omniapartners.com/publicsector/who-we-serve/k-12-education-procurement-services)

**PSM action:** check vendor's state-approved-list status quarterly for engagements in CA, NY, IL, MA, TX, FL. Drop-off is a renewal-killer.

## 9. Where PSMs are typically under-prepared

### The four common gaps

1. **Procurement officer perspective** — procurement cares about **process compliance** (threshold rules followed, data-privacy rider attached, vendor on approved list). NOT about pedagogy or product features. PSMs who only briefed the curriculum director are unprepared for the procurement gate.

2. **CFO perspective** — the CFO reads the **line item delta**, not the QBR. A renewal exceeding last year's line by >5% without a budget amendment is a CFO-blocker. The CFO doesn't care about adoption depth — they care about budget variance.

3. **Comparable contracts** — peer districts pay roughly comparable rates for comparable software. **Public-sector contracts are FOIA-able** in K-12 and discoverable through state contract registries. **Few PSMs use these.** Walking into a renewal blind on comps is negotiating in the dark.

4. **Mid-year check-in** — TSIA's research finds the strongest indicator of renewal is **consistent value confirmation through the year, not the renewal QBR itself.** If renewal is the first real conversation in months, it's already at-risk.

## The closing synthesis — the most common PSM mistake at renewal time in EdTech

**Running a generic SaaS renewal motion (90-day clock, value-recap QBR, multi-year discount pitch, CPI-linked increase) and discovering too late that the curriculum director who loves the product was never the buyer.**

In K-12, the appropriation cycle is closed by April for a July 1 fiscal year, the board votes on a consent agenda where a single skeptical question turns a routine approval into a hold, and the CFO reads the line-item delta — not the outcome story.

In higher-ed, the named decision-maker may have moved on between QBRs because of provost turnover or a restructure.

In corporate L&D, the CHRO's budget envelope shifted at the last forecast and the line item got cut without ever showing up as a "concern" the PSM could have addressed.

**The fix:**
1. Start sooner — **120-180 days in K-12**, not 90
2. **Confirm the named decision-maker every quarter**, not just at the renewal QBR (especially given K-12's 23% superintendent turnover)
3. **Give the champion a one-page ROI memo in the buyer's language** — per-student cost, FTE time saved, board-readable outcomes
4. **Treat the mid-year check-in — not the renewal QBR — as the place renewal is actually won**
