# K-12 PD (professional development) norms and constraints

## 1. The K-12 PD landscape (high-level)

K-12 PD has three drivers, and they overlap unevenly per state and district:

1. **State teacher-licensure renewal requirements** — most states require N hours of PD every M years for a teacher to maintain their teaching license. Hours vary widely.
2. **District-level PD allocation** — each district has a set number of in-service days, half-days, after-school PD blocks, and asynchronous PD hours per year. These are negotiated with teacher unions in unionized states.
3. **Federal funding requirements** — Title I, Title II-A, and ESSA carve out PD spending; some PD must be aligned with specific federal frameworks (e.g., evidence-based interventions per ESSA).

For vendor training to "count" toward any of these, it usually needs to:
- Align with the district's PD framework (learning objectives, evidence-base, hours)
- Be delivered through approved channels (in-service day, district-approved training, etc.)
- Document attendance (sign-in sheets, learning-objective alignment, post-assessment in some cases)

## 2. State-by-state PD hour requirements (anchor states)

These are starting points; districts often add on top of state minimums.

| State | Teacher PD hours required | Renewal cycle | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| California | 150 hours | 5 years (Clear Credential) | Highly structured; specific PD-credit categories |
| New York | 100 hours | 5 years (CTLE — Continuing Teacher and Leader Education) | Strict — only specific PD providers count |
| Florida | 120 inservice points | 5 years | Inservice point = ~1 hour; district controls allocation |
| Texas | 150 CPE hours | 5 years (Continuing Professional Education) | Subject-specific requirements for some teachers |
| Illinois | 120 hours | 5 years (PEL — Professional Educator License renewal) | Approved providers list |
| Massachusetts | 150 PDPs | 5 years (Professional Development Points) | Tied to license renewal |
| Other states | varies | typically 5 years | Confirm per state Dept. of Education |

**Implication for vendor training:** the relevant question isn't "will the district pay us to train" — it's "will the district recognize this training for PD-hour credit?" That determines whether teachers actually attend.

## 3. District PD allocation patterns

Most US K-12 districts allocate teacher PD time as a mix of:

| Modality | Typical allocation per teacher per year | When it happens |
|---|---|---|
| **In-service days** | 5-10 full days | Usually scattered across the school year (pre-school-start week + 2-4 distributed days); state-mandated minimums vary |
| **Half-day PD** | 4-8 half-days | Often early-release days where students go home early and teachers do PD |
| **After-school PD** | Variable; often union-negotiated cap | Specific contractual windows; very limited |
| **Asynchronous PD** | Variable; growing in 2020s | Done on teacher's own time with documented learning-objective alignment |
| **Summer PD** | Variable; often paid (stipend) | Teacher opt-in; can be week-long institutes or shorter workshops |

**Variance is high.** A district in TX may allocate 7 in-service days; the neighboring district may have 5. The vendor needs to ask, not assume.

## 4. In-service day timing patterns

In-service days cluster predictably in the school year:

- **Pre-school-start week** — most common in-service window (usually 1-3 days in late August)
- **Pre-Thanksgiving** — 1 day in some districts
- **January PD day** — common around MLK Day or shortly after winter break
- **February-March** — 1-2 distributed days
- **End-of-year** — 1-2 days for wrap-up + next-year planning

**Implication for vendor training:** plan vendor training to align with in-service-day patterns. Don't expect a district to schedule a vendor session in mid-October — teachers don't have PD time then. (Per [`k12-psm-operating-cadence.md`](k12-psm-operating-cadence.md), in-service days fall in the SETTLING phase but they're pre-scheduled and the vendor can't insert them.)

## 5. PD modality preferences (the post-COVID shift)

Pre-COVID, K-12 PD was overwhelmingly **synchronous + in-person**. The pattern shifted dramatically 2020-2024:

| Modality | Pre-COVID share (rough) | Post-COVID share (rough; 2026) | Trajectory |
|---|---|---|---|
| Live-in-person | ~75% | ~50% | Declining slowly but still primary for hands-on / critical content |
| Live-virtual (synchronous) | ~10% | ~25% | Significant adoption; convenient for distributed champions |
| Hybrid | ~5% | ~10% | Growing for cohort-based long-form training |
| Asynchronous (self-paced) | ~10% | ~15% | Significant adoption; preferred for reference + refresher content |

**Implication for vendor training:** offer multiple modalities. Live-in-person remains the gold standard for high-stakes content (train-the-trainer Session 2 facilitation craft); async-with-followup is now the dominant pattern for sustained-enablement.

## 6. The PD-credit framework alignment question

When a district WILL grant PD credit for vendor training, the typical alignment requirements:

- **Learning objectives in writing** — what teachers will be able to do after training
- **Evidence-base** — for content claims, what's the research backing
- **Hours documented** — sign-in sheets + start/end times
- **Post-assessment** — sometimes required (especially for state-approved providers)
- **Provider credentialing** — some states (NY especially) require the vendor or trainer to be a state-approved PD provider

**Practical recommendation:** at engagement start, ask the partner's PD coordinator (different role from the curriculum-director-champion) what's required for PD credit. If credit isn't required, skip the framework alignment — it's overhead without value. If credit IS required, build the framework alignment into the training design from the start, not retrofit later.

## 7. The teacher-union overlay (when it applies)

In unionized states (CA, NY, IL, MA, NJ, PA, OH, WA, MI, MN, others), teacher PD is often union-bargained:

- **Cap on after-school PD** — teachers cannot be required to do PD past contractual hours without compensation
- **Stipend requirements** — summer / after-hours PD often requires stipends per the union contract
- **PD opt-in vs opt-out defaults** — varies by contract
- **Approved-provider language** — some union contracts restrict PD to approved providers

**Implication:** in unionized districts, the PSM should NOT directly negotiate PD time with teachers. Route through the partner's PD coordinator + HR. Asking a teacher to "spend 30 minutes after school next week to learn the new feature" can be a contract violation.

## 8. The federal-funding-tied PD overlay

If the district is funding vendor training with Title I, Title II-A, or ESSA-allocated funds, additional requirements apply:

- **Evidence-based intervention** — content must align with the ESSA evidence tiers
- **Equity focus** — Title I-funded PD often must demonstrably address equity gaps
- **Compliance reporting** — district must document how PD funds were spent against goals

This rarely affects vendor training directly (vendor doesn't usually know the funding source), but if the partner is using Title-funded budget for the contract, the partner's compliance team will be involved.

## 9. The asynchronous-PD documentation challenge

Asynchronous vendor training is harder to "count" for PD credit than synchronous:

- **Attendance is harder to prove** — sign-in sheets become activity logs
- **Engagement is harder to validate** — completing the video isn't the same as understanding it
- **Post-assessment is more important** — usually required for async PD to count

**Pattern that works:** async modules + a synchronous followup discussion where attendance is documented. The async-with-followup pattern.

## 10. Higher-ed and corporate L&D PD (compact reference)

For when the partner segment isn't K-12:

### Higher-ed
- Faculty PD is opt-in, not required for licensure (except for teacher-prep faculty in some states)
- Academic-affairs office is the gatekeeper, not a PD coordinator
- "Training" framing lands poorly — frame as "academic-technology integration" or "pedagogical development"
- Asynchronous-with-followup is dominant
- Stipends rare; PD is part of faculty load

### Corporate L&D
- L&D function is dedicated and well-resourced
- LMS-of-record is the integration target (Cornerstone, Workday Learning, Degreed, etc.)
- Cohort-based + certification-driven
- Mobile-first asynchronous + monthly cohort sessions
- Stipends rare; L&D budget is the funding mechanism

## 11. Anti-patterns this file flags

- **Assuming PD time exists when it doesn't.** Asking a teacher to do training in mid-October without PD time allocated is a non-starter.
- **Skipping district PD-coordinator engagement.** The curriculum-director-champion may say "yes, teachers will be trained" without coordinating with PD office; the actual scheduling falls apart.
- **PD-credit alignment retrofitted late.** If PD credit matters, build the alignment in at design time. Adding learning objectives + evidence base after the curriculum is built is rework.
- **In unionized districts: direct teacher engagement on PD scheduling.** Route through HR / PD coordinator, not teachers directly.
- **One-modality offering in 2026.** Districts expect multi-modality options post-COVID. A vendor offering only live-in-person reads as out of date.
- **Async-only for high-stakes content.** Train-the-trainer Session 2 (facilitation craft) doesn't land async; the mock-training feedback loop needs synchronous interaction.
