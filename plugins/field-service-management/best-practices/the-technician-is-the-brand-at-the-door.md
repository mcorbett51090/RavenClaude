# The technician is the brand at the door

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Technician management and customer relationship
**Applies to:** `field-service-management`

---

## Why this exists

In a field-service business, the company's brand promise is delivered not by the marketing
department, the sales team, or the dispatch office — it is delivered by the technician who walks
through the customer's door. That technician is the sum of everything the business has committed
to: the SLA, the technical competence, the communication, the professionalism. Customer
satisfaction scores, contract renewal rates, and upsell opportunities are all won or lost
at the point of service.

This means technician productivity, training, and coaching are not just operational efficiency
levers — they are customer-relationship investments. A business that optimizes for technician
cost per hour while neglecting technical training and customer-communication skills is destroying
the asset that drives retention.

## How to apply

- **Invest in technical training as a first-time-fix lever.** Every skill gap that contributes
  to a first-time-fix miss is also a customer experience failure. Training ROI in field service
  is measured in first-time-fix improvement, callback reduction, and contract renewals — not
  just in certification cost.
- **Teach customer communication as a core skill.** A technically perfect repair delivered with
  poor communication (no explanation of what was found, no scope-setting on what was excluded,
  no follow-up on open items) leaves the customer uncertain and dissatisfied. Technician training
  must include: arrival/status communication, scope confirmation at job start, findings
  explanation at job completion, and escalation communication when a second visit is needed.
- **Use the technician scorecard to coach, not just to rate.** The scorecard (utilization,
  first-time-fix, MTTR, callbacks, customer satisfaction) exists to identify where a technician
  needs support, not just to rank performance. A technician who understands their own metrics
  and has a clear improvement path outperforms a technician who only sees a quarterly rating.

**Do:**

- Include customer-communication and professionalism in technician evaluation alongside technical
  metrics.
- Provide targeted technical training based on first-time-fix root-cause analysis — not just
  annual generic refreshers.
- Give technicians the tools to communicate well in the field: job histories, service notes,
  customer contact information, and a script for scope changes and escalations.

**Don't:**

- Treat technician training as a cost item to minimize rather than an investment in customer
  retention.
- Coach technicians on metrics they don't control (parts-delay failures, skill-mismatch dispatches).
- Build a scorecard that rates without developing — feedback without a path forward is
  demoralizing, not motivating.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

In highly commoditized, low-margin service categories with very low customer loyalty (e.g., one-
time warranty service on low-cost appliances), the customer relationship may not justify
investment above a minimum professional standard. This is a deliberate margin-vs-relationship
tradeoff, not a default posture.
