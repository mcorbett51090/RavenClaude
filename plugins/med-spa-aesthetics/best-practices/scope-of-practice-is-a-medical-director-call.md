# Scope of practice is a medical-director call, not an ops choice

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Compliance structure
**Applies to:** `med-spa-aesthetics`

> Operations rule about **routing**, not a legal determination. Every scope/supervision specific is state-specific and `[verify-at-use]`. No patient PHI/PII. Flag, never decide.

---

## Why this exists

In aesthetics, the scheduling pressure to keep injector-hours full creates a constant temptation to treat "who may perform this" as an operations convenience. It is not — scope of practice, delegation, supervision level, and good-faith-exam requirements are **state-specific legal/medical determinations**. A confident wrong answer here is the most dangerous output in the domain. The rule is: map the structure, flag the specific, route the determination.

## How to apply

- Separate **structure** (there is a delegating physician, a supervision level, a consent capture) from **determination** (what the rule *is*). Map the first; never state the second.
- Flag every specific as state-specific and `[verify-at-use]`.
- Route the determination to the **medical director and a licensed attorney/regulator**.
- Vet marketing copy for medical/efficacy claims and scope overreach; route before it ships.

**Do:** map the compliance structure; flag and route every rule.
**Don't:** answer "can this person inject in our state?" with a rule; treat scope as a scheduling convenience.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Describing the **elements** of a consent or adverse-event protocol (as structure to have in place) is operational and allowed — the *legal sufficiency* of the language is still a professional's call.
