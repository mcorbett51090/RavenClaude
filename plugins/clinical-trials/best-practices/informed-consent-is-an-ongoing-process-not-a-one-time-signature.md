# Informed Consent Is an Ongoing Process, Not a One-Time Signature

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Clinical operations / participant rights
**Applies to:** `clinical-trials`

---

## Why this exists

Regulatory agencies and IRBs treat informed consent as a continuing obligation, not a baseline event. When a protocol is amended in a way that affects participant risk or burden, re-consent is required. When new safety information emerges mid-trial, participants must be informed. A trial in which consent records are missing, outdated, or improperly obtained — even if participants were never actually harmed — is a critical protocol deviation that can invalidate data from affected participants, trigger a clinical hold, and create both regulatory and legal exposure for the sponsor.

## How to apply

Treat informed consent as a living operational workflow with three distinct phases:

```
Consent workflow phases:
1. BASELINE CONSENT
   - Use the most recently IRB-approved version
   - Obtain before any study procedures including screening
   - Document: date, time, witness if required, participant signature, staff signature
   - Store: original in ISF; copy to participant; copy in site files per local law

2. RE-CONSENT TRIGGERS (check at each amendment and safety update)
   - Protocol amendment that changes: procedures, visit burden, risk profile, endpoints
   - New safety information that materially changes the benefit-risk assessment
   - Participant moves to a new age category (pediatric → adult)
   - Change in sponsor or investigator in certain jurisdictions

3. ONGOING CONSENT DOCUMENTATION
   - Consent log current at all times: version in use, participants consented to each version
   - Expired consent version flagged and replaced immediately at IRB approval
   - Lost-to-follow-up participants: document consent status before attempting re-contact
```

**Do:**
- Maintain a consent version log at the site level — which participants are consented to which version.
- Treat the consent date vs. first-procedure date comparison as a routine data-review check; any first-procedure-before-consent is a critical deviation.
- Train coordinators to distinguish "consent" from "assent" in pediatric populations.

**Don't:**
- Allow a participant to proceed to eligibility screening before the consent is signed and dated.
- Use a superseded consent form after a new version has received IRB approval, even if the amendment seems minor.
- File re-consent as optional when an amendment adds a new invasive procedure or extends participant exposure.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Emergency/waiver-of-consent situations in life-threatening condition studies (21 CFR 50.24) are the narrow regulatory exception — these require prospective IRB approval of the waiver and have their own strict documentation requirements.
