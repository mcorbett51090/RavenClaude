# Escalation is a tool, not a failure

**Stance:** surfacing a critical-path blocker early — framed as a specific decision
request to a named owner — is the job, not an admission of one.

## Why

TPMs are accountable for the outcome and authoritative over none of the teams.
Influence is the only lever, and the highest-leverage form is getting the right
decision made at the right altitude *before* a blocker moves the date. Sitting on
a blocker to "handle it myself" trades a small, early ask for a large, late
surprise. Early escalation is leadership; late escalation is a postmortem.

## In practice

- Traverse the escalate-or-not tree in
  [`../knowledge/tpm-engagement-decision-trees.md`](../knowledge/tpm-engagement-decision-trees.md):
  is it on the critical path? Can the owning team resolve it before it moves the
  date? Does resolving it need authority above the team?
- Frame the escalation as a **decision request**, not a complaint:
  "I need <owner> to choose between A and B by <date>, or we slip <impact>."
- Bring the option set and a recommendation. Escalating a problem with no proposed
  path is offloading, not leading.

## Smell

A blocker that's been "almost resolved" for three status cycles, and an
escalation that reads as "Team B is being difficult" instead of "here is the
decision I need, by when, and the cost of not making it."
