# Service Failure Response Is a Sales Event

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Freight-forwarding sales
**Applies to:** `freight-forwarding-sales`

---

## Why this exists

A delayed shipment, a customs hold, or a cargo damage event handled fast and transparently retains the account better than a flawless quiet quarter. The account manager who is the first call when something goes wrong — who calls the customer before the customer calls them, who has the root cause and the recovery plan before the meeting, and who closes the loop with a written summary — has differentiated the forwarder from every competitor who just hopes the problem fades. Service failures that are handled well generate more loyalty than incidents that never happen. Service failures that are handled poorly generate churn.

## How to apply

Treat every service failure as a four-step sales event:

```
Service Failure Response Protocol
────────────────────────────────────
Step 1 — Acknowledge (within 2 hours of knowing)
  Contact:  Proactive outreach to the customer before they contact you.
  Format:   Phone call (not email first) — email as a follow-up record.
  Message:  "[Shipment X] has [description of issue]. I'm on it and will have an update
             by [time]. Here's what I know so far: [facts only, no speculation]."

Step 2 — Root cause + recovery plan (within 24 hours)
  Root cause identified?  [ ] Yes  [ ] Pending investigation (give a timeline)
  Recovery options:
    Option A: ________________  ETA: ___  Cost impact: ___
    Option B: ________________  ETA: ___  Cost impact: ___
  Recommendation:  ________________  Rationale:  ________________
  Present options to the customer — don't unilaterally choose.

Step 3 — Execution + proactive updates
  Update the customer at each milestone: "cleared customs at X"; "en route, ETA Y."
  Do not wait for the customer to ask for an update — send updates before they ask.

Step 4 — Close the loop (written summary within 48 hours of resolution)
  Root cause:              ________________
  Resolution:              ________________
  Corrective action taken: ________________  (what prevents recurrence?)
  Goodwill gesture (if warranted):  ________________

Follow-on:
  Add a QBR agenda item: "Service failures this quarter + our response + resolution." (§ QBR rule)
```

**Do:**
- Call before emailing on a significant failure — a phone call signals urgency and care; an email at the same severity signals bureaucracy.
- Present the corrective action as a process improvement, not just an apology — customers want to know it won't happen again.
- Log the failure and the response in the CRM — a history of well-handled failures is a retention asset in the next re-bid.

**Don't:**
- Wait for the customer to call you — they will, and by then the conversation is reactive, not partnership-oriented.
- Offer a blanket "this won't happen again" without a specific corrective action — it sounds like an empty promise.
- Use the service failure response to sell new services in the same communication — help first, sell later.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Minor exceptions (a 1-day transit delay on a non-time-critical shipment that self-resolved without customer impact) may not warrant the full four-step protocol. Use judgment on proportionality; the customer should still be proactively informed, even briefly.
