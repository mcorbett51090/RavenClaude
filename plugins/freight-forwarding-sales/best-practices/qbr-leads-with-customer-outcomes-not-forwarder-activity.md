# QBR Leads with Customer Outcomes, Not Forwarder Activity

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Freight-forwarding sales
**Applies to:** `freight-forwarding-sales`

---

## Why this exists

A QBR that opens with "here's how many shipments we handled for you this quarter" is a vendor report. A QBR that opens with "here's what your supply chain cost and how we moved those numbers" is a partner conversation. The first keeps the relationship transactional; the second builds the strategic trust that makes the customer reluctant to re-source. The single most reliable predictor of whether a QBR produces a price increase or triggers a competitive re-bid is which conversation it is.

## How to apply

Structure every QBR in three sections, in this order:

```
QBR Structure
───────────────
Section 1 — Your Supply Chain Performance (customer outcomes, 40% of the meeting)
  Metrics to present:
    On-time delivery rate (%):         ___ vs. target ___  vs. prior quarter ___
    Claims rate (% of shipments):       ___ vs. target ___
    Average transit time (by lane):    ___ vs. SLA ___
    Cost per unit/kg shipped:          $___  vs. prior period $___  (savings: $_____)
    Exception volume (late / damaged / customs-held): ___

Section 2 — Honest Assessment + Issues Resolved (10% of meeting)
  Acknowledge service failures and what was done:
    Issue:  ________________  Resolution:  ________________  Root cause fixed: Y/N
  Do not skip this section even if it is uncomfortable — a QBR that whitewashes problems
  signals that the forwarder is not paying attention.

Section 3 — Next Quarter + Joint Actions (50% of meeting)
  Customer's upcoming supply chain changes:  ________________
  Lanes or volumes changing:  ________________
  Upsell / new service opportunities identified:  ________________
  Joint action items:
    | Action | Owner | Date |
    |--------|-------|------|

Avoid: opening with shipment count, tonnage handled, or any other activity metric that
tells the customer what the forwarder did, not what the customer achieved.
```

**Do:**
- Request the customer's own supply chain metrics (their inventory turns, landed cost, stockout rate) before the QBR — the conversation is richer when you know their business objectives.
- Send a QBR prep email 5 days before the meeting asking for agenda input — customers who contribute to the agenda own the conversation.
- Leave the meeting with at least three documented joint action items with owners and dates.

**Don't:**
- Open with a volume/activity table without translating it into customer outcomes — "we moved 450 TEUs" is not an outcome; "your on-time rate was 96.2% vs. an industry average of 91%" is.
- Skip Section 2 (honest assessment) in a quarter with service issues — the customer already knows about the problems; the QBR's job is to demonstrate that the forwarder does too and has fixed them.
- Let the QBR become a one-way presentation; it should be 40% presentation and 60% dialogue.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

New accounts with fewer than three months of shipment history do not have enough data for a full QBR metrics review; substitute a "service delivery check-in + forward planning" meeting using the Section 3 structure. A full QBR is appropriate once there is at least one quarter of shared history.
