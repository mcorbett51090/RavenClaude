# Collect competitor intelligence systematically — not just when you lose a deal

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Sales strategy / pipeline
**Applies to:** `freight-forwarding-sales`

---

## Why this exists

Win/loss analysis in freight forwarding is almost always reactive: after a deal is lost to a competitor, the seller asks why. That is too late. A forwarder who understands, in advance, how each major competitor prices, which lanes they are strong on, where they underinvest in service, and what their win narrative is, can build better tender responses, sharper objection handles, and a more targeted win story. Competitor intelligence gathered only in the heat of a lost deal is biased, incomplete, and drives defensive rather than offensive positioning.

## How to apply

**The four sources of forward-looking competitor intel (all ethical, all public or customer-shared):**

1. **Win/loss debrief discipline** — ask every customer who does not choose you, at every stage: "Can you tell me where we fell short?" Log the answer in the CRM by competitor name. Over 10–20 deal outcomes, patterns emerge.
2. **Tender debrief rights** — in formal tenders, many procurement processes offer a debrief to unsuccessful bidders. Always request it. The information on pricing vs competitors and where your service narrative missed is gold for the next bid.
3. **Front-line field intelligence** — sellers who call on shippers hear what their competitors are doing: who has cut rates, who has had a service failure, who has lost a key person, which carrier partnership has changed. Log every piece of third-party intel in a structured format, tagged by competitor.
4. **Public sources** — carrier press releases (which forwarders are appointed preferred partners), trade press (market share, acquisitions, network changes), and LinkedIn (key hires, departures, new capability announcements) provide structural intelligence about competitor positioning.

**Competitor intelligence card (per major competitor in your territory):**

```
Competitor: [NAME]
Strengths: [e.g. Strong Asia–US Pacific lane; large ocean carrier block-space]
Weaknesses: [e.g. Thin Europe origin network; limited tech/visibility offering]
Typical win narrative: [e.g. Low-cost transactional pricing; high volume-discount offers]
Typical pricing posture vs us: [e.g. 8–12% below us on base; thinner on surcharges]
Common objection they raise against us: [e.g. "Smaller than the top-3 forwarders"]
Our counter-narrative: [e.g. "Dedicated account team vs the big forwarder's call centre model"]
Recent market signal: [DATE — source — intel]
```

**Do:**
- Update competitor cards at least quarterly; label any entry with a date and source.
- Share competitor intelligence across the sales team in a regular "state of the market" forum — one seller's intelligence makes everyone sharper.
- Separate observed fact (a customer told you the competitor quoted USD X) from inference (you think the competitor probably won on price) — label them differently.

**Don't:**
- Disparage competitors in customer conversations — share your positive differentiation, not their weaknesses.
- Treat a single data point (one lost deal) as a reliable picture of a competitor's strategy.
- Rely on generic "they're cheaper" or "they have more resources" — that is not intelligence, it is an excuse.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

In very niche specialist lanes (project cargo, heavy-lift, perishables) where the competitive set is small and already well-known, a full card for every competitor may be overkill. A shared "lane competitive map" maintained at team level is sufficient. The principle — be systematic, not reactive — holds regardless of format.
