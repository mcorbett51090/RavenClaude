# Discount Is the Last Lever, Not the First

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Freight-forwarding sales
**Applies to:** `freight-forwarding-sales`

---

## Why this exists

A price reduction without a give-get trains the customer to request discounts every time. Once a forwarder discounts to win or retain business without asking for something in return — a volume commitment, a longer contract term, a mode shift, additional scope — the price becomes a ceiling that is only ever revised downward. Freight is a relationship business; a professional who leads with value (lane expertise, transit reliability, claims handling, visibility tools) and holds price until it is the last remaining issue is perceived as a partner. One who offers discounts pre-emptively is perceived as a commodity.

## How to apply

Before offering any price reduction, work through the value-first sequence:

```
Price Concession Framework
────────────────────────────
Step 1 — Quantify the value gap
  Customer's stated target price:  $______
  Your all-in quote:                $______
  Gap:                              $______ (___%)

Step 2 — Lead with value before touching price
  What reliability / transit / visibility proof can you provide?
  Can you demonstrate a cost saving elsewhere in their supply chain?
  Is there a service failure that this customer experienced with a prior forwarder?
  Talking points: ________________

Step 3 — If price is still the objection, establish the give-get
  Discount of $___/container  ←→  Volume commitment of ___+ containers/quarter
  Discount of ____%            ←→  Contract term of ___ months
  Rate reduction on lane X     ←→  Add lanes Y and Z to scope
  Document the give-get in writing before the next call

Step 4 — If no give-get is accepted, hold price or walk
  A discount without a give-get erodes margin and establishes a precedent.
  Better outcome: keep price, lose the rate-shopper, and fill capacity with margin-positive freight.
```

**Do:**
- Present the value argument in writing (a brief summary of transit performance, claims rate, or cost-savings analysis) before mentioning price flexibility.
- When a give-get is agreed, write it into the rate confirmation or the rate agreement — a verbal "we'll give you more volume" is not a commitment.
- Track every discount and its give-get in the CRM — a pattern of discounts without give-gets is a sales discipline problem, not a pricing problem.

**Don't:**
- Offer "I'll check what I can do on the rate" without first presenting the value case — it signals that price is the primary lever before the customer has even pushed.
- Match a competitor's rate without understanding whether the competitor's service scope and reliability are equivalent — apples-to-apples comparisons require equivalent service levels.
- Accept volume commitments that aren't legally binding or verifiable; a commitment that can't be measured isn't a give-get.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Spot quotes on one-off, purely transactional shipments from price-sensitive shippers with no relationship potential are a different context. Competing on price for a single-shipment spot quote with no upsell path is a volume decision, not a price-discipline failure — provided the margin floor is respected.
