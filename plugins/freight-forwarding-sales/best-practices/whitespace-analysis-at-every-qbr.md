# Run a whitespace analysis at every QBR — map what the customer moves vs what you handle

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Key account management / growth
**Applies to:** `freight-forwarding-sales`

---

## Why this exists

A QBR that reviews only the lanes the forwarder already handles leaves money and strategic opportunity on the table at the most important commercial conversation of the quarter. The customer's full logistics spend — modes, lanes, trade corridors, and domestic moves — is the universe. The forwarder's current wallet share is a fraction of that universe. The whitespace is the delta. Every QBR that passes without surfacing the whitespace is a QBR that left a growth conversation to the competition. Identifying the whitespace does not require espionage; it requires preparation and the right three questions in the meeting.

## How to apply

**Before the QBR — build the whitespace map:**

1. Pull the volumes you currently handle: lanes, modes, commodity groups, frequency.
2. Estimate (or confirm with the customer) their total logistics footprint: all trade lanes, all modes, all regions, all volumes — not just what you move.
3. Identify the gaps: which lanes are not with you? Which modes? Which new markets or suppliers?
4. Hypothesise the reason: competitor lock, price, capability gap, incumbent inertia?

**Whitespace map format (for the account plan):**

| Lane / mode / service | Estimated annual spend | Currently with us? | Competitor / reason | Growth play | Timing |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| [CN–US FCL — primary volumes] | [USD X] | YES — 100% | — | Deepen + protect | Annual renewal |
| [VN–EU FCL — new supplier base] | [USD X estimated] | NO | Competitor Y — incumbent from previous RFQ | Lane + ops pitch at QBR | Q3 RFQ cycle |
| [Air — high-value samples] | [USD X estimated] | NO | Customer self-managing via integrator | Airfreight capability demo | Next QBR |
| [US domestic distribution] | [USD X] | NO | Customer's in-house carrier base | 3PL partnership angle | Long term |

**Three questions to ask in the QBR that surface whitespace naturally:**
1. "We handle [X TEU/month] on [LANE] — are there other lanes or new supplier corridors you're opening in the next 12 months that we're not yet involved in?"
2. "We know you're managing [air / road / domestic] separately — is that working well, or is there a consolidation play we should look at?"
3. "If you could change one thing about your logistics in the next year, what would it be?" — the answer almost always contains a lane or mode they're not happy with.

**Do:**
- Build the whitespace map in the account plan before every QBR, not during.
- Frame whitespace conversations as "helping the customer think forward," not as a sales pitch — the best whitespace conversations feel like problem-solving.
- Record the customer's answers in the CRM immediately after the QBR; they become the basis of the next opportunity.

**Don't:**
- Spring the whitespace conversation without preparation — if you don't know their total footprint, the questions will feel generic.
- Overwhelm the QBR with growth pitches; the first half of the agenda is the customer's outcomes, not your proposals. One whitespace conversation per QBR is enough.
- Conflate whitespace with upsell pressure — the customer will close down if the conversation feels commercial-first.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

For fully transactional accounts (no regular QBR cadence, purely inbound spot-quote model), whitespace analysis is conducted less formally — via periodic check-in calls, not a structured QBR worksheet. The principle is the same; only the cadence and formality differ.
