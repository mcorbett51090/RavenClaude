# Multi-thread every key account before the single contact leaves

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Key account management / retention
**Applies to:** `freight-forwarding-sales`

---

## Why this exists

In freight forwarding, the primary commercial relationship is almost always built around one person — the Head of Logistics, the Supply Chain Director, or the Procurement Manager who runs the RFQ. When that contact leaves, moves sideways, or is replaced in a restructure, a single-threaded account becomes a cold-start for the competitor who already knows the replacement. The churn rate on logistics contracts within 12 months of a key contact departure is a well-documented industry pattern. Multi-threading is the structural fix, not a nice-to-have.

## How to apply

Build at least two named contacts at decision-making level **before** you need them.

**Contact coverage target per key account:**

| Contact type | Who | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Primary (operational) | The logistics / supply chain manager who runs day-to-day | Relationship is already there |
| Secondary (commercial) | Procurement lead or VP Supply Chain | Holds the contract and the renewal |
| Sponsor (executive) | CFO / COO / CEO (at SME) or regional director | Escalation path; sees the strategic value |

**Tactics to build the second contact:**
1. Request a QBR with both the operational and procurement contact — frame it as aligning on objectives for the year.
2. Include the sponsor in a service-recovery debrief after a significant exception — they were already informed; now they know you personally.
3. Use LinkedIn to connect with the wider logistics/procurement team at the account; comment on their content before you need a favour.
4. On site visits, ask to walk the warehouse with the operations director, not just the logistics coordinator.

**In the account plan:**
```
Multi-thread status:
  Primary contact: [Name, Title, relationship depth 1–5]
  Secondary contact: [Name, Title, relationship depth 1–5]
  Executive sponsor: [Name, Title, relationship depth 1–5]
  Action to deepen weakest contact: [Action, Owner, Date]
```

**Do:**
- Set a calendar reminder to actively develop the second contact every quarter.
- Log every contact at the account — not just the primary — in the CRM.
- Propose a joint business planning session that naturally requires multiple stakeholders to attend.

**Don't:**
- Wait until the primary contact announces their departure before you try to build the secondary.
- Treat every stakeholder above the logistics manager as "too senior to contact" — executive relationship-building is a seller skill, not an escalation.
- Let the "multi-threaded" box sit checked after the initial introduction; the relationship must be maintained, not just initiated.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

It does not apply to pure transactional spot accounts where there is no ongoing relationship to protect. For accounts below your key-account threshold (annual freight value below your defined floor), a single qualified contact is acceptable — route them to a standard account-management cadence instead.
