# Three-tier and licensing are a professional call

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Distribution compliance
**Applies to:** `craft-beverage-operations`

> Operations rule about **routing**, not a legal determination. Every three-tier / licensing / excise specific is jurisdiction-specific and `[verify-at-use]`. No PII. Flag, never decide.

---

## Why this exists

The three-tier system, distributor franchise law, TTB and state licensing, direct-ship permits, and excise tax are where craft producers get locked in or caught out — and they are **jurisdiction-specific legal/tax determinations**, not operations choices. A confident wrong answer here can mean an unenforceable contract, a permit violation, or a tax exposure. The rule is: model the economics, map the structure, flag the specific, route the determination.

## How to apply

- Model the **economics** of the choice (self-distribute vs distributor, add a state, a new channel) in real numbers and constraints.
- Describe the **structure** (there's a federal TTB layer, a state license, a direct-ship permit, an excise obligation) without asserting the rule.
- Flag every specific as jurisdiction-specific and `[verify-at-use]`.
- Route the determination to a **licensed attorney/accountant and the regulator** — especially franchise-law lock-in before signing a distributor.

**Do:** model economics; map structure; flag and route every rule.
**Don't:** answer "is self-distribution allowed / what's the excise / can we ship there?" with a rule; sign a distributor without reading franchise law.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Describing the *general shape* of the three-tier system as background is fine; the moment it becomes "here's the rule for your state," it's a determination — flag and route.
