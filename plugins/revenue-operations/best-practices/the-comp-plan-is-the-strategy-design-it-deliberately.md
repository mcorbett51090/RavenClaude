# The comp plan is the strategy — design it deliberately

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Sales compensation
**Applies to:** `revenue-operations`

---

## Why this exists

The comp plan is the most powerful strategic communication tool a GTM leader has. Reps optimize for
what they are measured and paid for. If the strategy says "land enterprise accounts," but the comp
plan pays the same rate on an SMB deal and an enterprise deal, reps will rationally chase SMB volume
— shorter cycle, lower complexity, same commission rate. The strategy and the comp plan are
misaligned, and the strategy will lose.

The problem compounds because comp plan effects are often perverse and unintended. Quarterly
accelerators above 100% create an incentive to pull deals forward into the current quarter —
including by offering discounts or making promises the company cannot keep. Uncapped plans with
no decelerator can drive reps to sandbag deals across quarters to smooth earnings. A SPIF with
no end date stops driving incremental behavior within two quarters. Each of these is a design
failure that could have been predicted before the plan was signed.

## How to apply

Before designing any comp mechanic:

1. **Name the intended behavior.** What specific behavior is this mechanic designed to drive?
   (Not "close more deals" — too generic. "Close net-new enterprise logos in the healthcare
   vertical in Q3.")
2. **Name the adjacent perverse behavior.** What behavior might a rational, self-interested rep
   adopt in response to this mechanic that the company does not want? (e.g., "Rep pulls a deal
   forward into Q3 by offering an unauthorized discount.")
3. **Accept the behavioral consequence or redesign.** If the perverse behavior is acceptable
   (or manageable with a guardrail), proceed. If not, redesign the mechanic.
4. **Count the mechanics.** A plan with more than 4-5 distinct earning components no longer
   drives any single behavior reliably — reps cannot optimize for it.

**Do:**

- Write the behavioral-strategy mapping before opening the spreadsheet with commission rates.
- Source every quota, rate, and OTE figure with a methodology and a date — or label it as an
  estimate and commit to calibrating it.
- Test the plan against a rep behavioral simulation: at P10 / P50 / P90 attainment, what does
  a rational rep do in month 11 of the year?
- Review the plan annually; change it at a defined change window, not mid-period.

**Don't:**

- Design a comp plan in isolation from the strategy — copy a competitor's plan or "what we did
  last year."
- Add more mechanics to fix every observed problem — complexity destroys signal.
- Set OTE before setting quota (quota drives OTE via the quota multiple, not the reverse).
- Publish a plan without a behavioral-consequence table — even a short one.
- Introduce a mid-period plan change without legal and HR review; it damages rep trust.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

In a pre-product-market-fit company where the sales motion is still being discovered, a simple
comp plan (straight commission, no accelerators, no complex mechanics) is appropriate precisely
because the strategy is not yet defined. Optimizing the comp mechanics for an undefined strategy
produces complexity without behavioral benefit. Simplicity is a virtue at this stage; design for
legibility and trust, not for behavioral nuance.
