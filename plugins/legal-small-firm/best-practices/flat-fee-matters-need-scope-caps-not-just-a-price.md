# Flat-Fee Matters Need Scope Caps, Not Just a Price

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Small-firm legal practice
**Applies to:** `legal-small-firm`

---

## Why this exists

A flat-fee engagement without explicit scope limits is an open-ended hourly in disguise. When the client's demand expands — more revisions, more counterparty calls, a contested close that was supposed to be routine — the attorney absorbs every extra hour while the client holds the original price as a ceiling. The flat fee becomes a write-off machine. Scope caps are what make flat fees sustainable.

## How to apply

Every flat-fee engagement letter must define three elements before the fee is quoted:

```
Flat-Fee Scope Definition
──────────────────────────
Included:
  - [specific deliverables and tasks covered]
  - [number of drafts / revision rounds]
  - [bounded counterparty interactions, e.g., "up to two rounds of negotiation"]

Excluded (examples):
  - Contested provisions requiring more than two rounds of negotiation
  - Additional parties added after engagement begins
  - Litigation arising from the transaction

Re-scope trigger:
  "If [named condition] occurs, the fee will be adjusted to $[range] or converted
   to hourly at $___/hr; attorney will notify client before proceeding."

Out-of-scope hourly rate:  $___/hr
```

**Do:**
- Quote the flat fee only after scoping the matter; do not reverse-engineer the scope to match a market-price number.
- Use the re-scope trigger clause as the primary defense against scope creep — it keeps the attorney from working free and the client from being surprised.
- Document every out-of-scope expansion and its authorization before performing the work.

**Don't:**
- Use vague scope language ("drafting the agreement") without specifying deliverables and revision rounds.
- Skip the out-of-scope hourly rate — without it, the re-scope clause has no practical enforcement mechanism.
- Price the flat fee at a level that only works if nothing goes wrong; flat fees must include a complexity margin.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Truly commoditized form matters (a standard single-issue LLC formation with no complications) may not need a formal re-scope trigger — but still need an explicit included/excluded list. Contingency matters are not governed by this rule.
