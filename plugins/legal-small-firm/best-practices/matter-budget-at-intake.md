# Set a Matter Budget at Intake, Not After the Work Starts

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Small-firm legal practice
**Applies to:** `legal-small-firm`

---

## Why this exists

An engagement agreement that specifies only the hourly rate and the scope leaves the attorney flying blind on fee exposure and the client with no ceiling expectation. The write-offs that follow are almost always traced back to the absence of a budget conversation at intake: the client assumed the matter would cost $5 k; the attorney billed $18 k; neither had a document to point to. A matter budget at intake is also the attorney's risk management tool — it forces scope decomposition before work begins and surfaces assumptions that would otherwise become disputes.

## How to apply

Every new matter gets a budget memo (internal) and a fee-estimate disclosure (client-facing) before work begins:

```
Matter budget memo (internal)
─────────────────────────────
Matter:               [name / number]
Fee structure:        [hourly | flat | contingency | hybrid]
Phase breakdown:
  Phase 1 — [description]         Est. hrs: ___ @ $___/hr = $___
  Phase 2 — [description]         Est. hrs: ___ @ $___/hr = $___
  ...
Total estimated fee:              $[low] – $[high]
Assumptions / scope limits:       [list key assumptions]
Trigger for re-scope:             [what changes require a new budget conversation]
Retainer / advance fee:           $___
```

For flat-fee matters, the "budget" is the quoted flat plus documented assumptions about what is and isn't included.

**Do:**
- Give a range (low / high), not a single number — a single number becomes a ceiling in the client's mind.
- Document the trigger condition for a mid-matter re-scope conversation (e.g., "if discovery expands to more than three depositions, we will revise the estimate").
- Use the budget as the baseline for write-down analysis — a write-down below budget signals a scope or execution problem; one above signals the estimate was wrong.

**Don't:**
- Start work before the fee structure and initial budget are documented and signed.
- Treat the budget as a guarantee. It is an estimate with documented assumptions, not a cap unless the engagement letter says so explicitly.
- Skip the budget conversation on "small" matters — the write-offs on under-$5 k matters are disproportionately common in solo practices because they're treated as not worth the conversation.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Pure contingency matters have no billable estimate; the economic disclosure is the contingency percentage and the scope of recovery. The intake document still captures scope and the trigger for withdrawal.
