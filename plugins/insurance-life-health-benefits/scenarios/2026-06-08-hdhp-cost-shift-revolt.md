## Problem

To hold the budget, an employer replaced its PPO with the lowest-premium HDHP and made no employer HSA contribution. Premiums dropped and leadership called it a win — then employees with chronic conditions and young families hit the full deductible out of pocket and revolted. The package had also quietly dropped long-term disability, and a few employees discovered the new plan's network excluded their specialists.

## Constraints context

- A hard budget cap drove the premium-only optimization; nobody had modeled member out-of-pocket cost across a realistic claims distribution.
- A mixed workforce: some healthy single employees (fine on an HDHP) and some high-utilizers (badly exposed).
- The HDHP was sold as a "consumer-driven benefit" with zero employer HSA seed money.

## Attempts

- Tried: defend the move on premium savings alone. Failed — the savings were real for the employer but came straight out of members' pockets; total cost of coverage went up for the people who used care.
- Tried: add a modest employer HSA contribution to the existing HDHP. Helped the cost-shift but didn't fix network fit or the high-utilizers' exposure.
- Tried: offer a two-plan choice — a funded HDHP+HSA (with real employer seed money) for those who want it, alongside a PPO option — restore LTD, and verify network adequacy for the actual employee/specialist mix. This worked.

## Resolution

The dual-option design let healthy employees take the funded HDHP and high-utilizers stay on a PPO, the employer HSA contribution turned the HDHP from a cost-shift into a genuine benefit, restoring LTD closed the income-protection gap, and a network check caught the specialist exclusions before renewal. Member satisfaction recovered without blowing the budget, because the savings were redistributed rather than extracted.

## Lesson

Optimize the member's total cost of coverage, not the headline premium — an HDHP with no employer HSA contribution is a cost-shift, not a benefit. Design the package as a system (don't silently drop disability income), check network adequacy before plan-design elegance, and give a mixed workforce a choice.
