# Food Service Quality Drives Both Satisfaction Scores and Census

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Resident experience / operations
**Applies to:** `senior-care-operations`

---

## Why this exists

In every resident and family satisfaction survey, dining consistently ranks among the top three drivers of overall satisfaction — and dissatisfaction. For a senior living community, dining is not amenity or hospitality: it is a fundamental component of the care experience. Residents who are dissatisfied with food quality, variety, temperature, service, or dignity of the dining experience are at higher risk of avoidable move-out, negative online reviews, and complaints to surveyors. Conversely, communities known for excellent dining quality earn word-of-mouth referrals, better satisfaction scores, and a genuine competitive differentiation in a market where physical amenities are often comparable. Dining quality is both a quality-of-life obligation and a competitive and census management asset.

## How to apply

Manage food service through resident feedback, operational metrics, and a named dining quality owner.

```
Dining quality management framework:
1. Resident feedback (structured, not just comment box):
   - Resident Council meeting: dining discussion at every meeting (monthly minimum)
   - Quarterly dining satisfaction survey: standardized questions on taste, variety, temperature,
     portion size, staff interactions, special diet accommodation
   - Threshold: any satisfaction item below 75% positive → 30-day improvement plan
     [unverified — training knowledge]

2. Operational metrics (track monthly):
   - Menu cycle compliance: are the planned menus being executed as written?
   - Food waste: % of prepared food not consumed (proxy for palatability and portion sizing)
   - Special diet accuracy: incidents of wrong diet tray delivery (tracked as a safety event)
   - Dining room attendance: % of residents eating in the dining room vs. room trays
     (a declining dining room attendance rate often precedes dissatisfaction escalation)

3. Nutritional monitoring:
   - Monthly weight checks for all residents
   - Weight loss >5% in 30 days or >10% in 6 months: dietitian referral, care plan review,
     and physician notification (as required by state and community policy)
   - % of residents with a nutritional risk flag and an active dietary intervention

4. Special dietary needs compliance:
   - Therapeutic diets (texture modification, diabetic, low-sodium, allergen restriction):
     documented in care plan and confirmed at each meal service
   - A wrong diet tray delivery is a safety incident — treated with the same seriousness
     as a medication error in the documentation and review process

5. Survey readiness:
   - Food service and dining operations are a top survey focus area
   - Documentation: menus, weight monitoring logs, therapeutic diet records, resident
     satisfaction survey results
```

**Do:**
- Make the Executive Director a visible presence in the dining room at least 3x per week — resident and family satisfaction with leadership visibility is directly connected to dining satisfaction.
- Respond to every dining complaint from Resident Council or a family within 24 hours with a specific action, not just "we'll look into it."
- Include the food service director in the clinical and quality review meeting — food service quality is not a hospitality department silo.

**Don't:**
- Reduce food service staffing or ingredient quality as a cost-cutting measure without modeling the satisfaction and census impact — it is one of the highest-risk cost-cut categories.
- Allow a therapeutic diet error to be managed informally without an incident report and a care plan review — it is a safety event.
- Use Resident Council dining feedback as the only quality input; residents in cognitive decline may not articulate concerns verbally — observe dining room behavior and staff reports.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Memory care dining has additional considerations — residents with dementia may require adapted dining environments (smaller dining groups, visual cues, finger foods) that require a protocol beyond the standard framework. Apply the general principles with dementia-specific adaptations.
