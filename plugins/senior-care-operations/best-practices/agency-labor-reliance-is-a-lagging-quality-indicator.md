# Agency Labor Reliance Is a Lagging Quality Indicator

**Status:** Primary diagnostic
**Domain:** Staffing / labor management
**Applies to:** `senior-care-operations`

---

## Why this exists

Agency (temporary) caregiver labor costs 2–3x the equivalent employee cost per hour and introduces staff who are unfamiliar with individual residents' routines, preferences, and care needs. [unverified — training knowledge] A community running more than 10–15% of its direct-care hours on agency labor is experiencing both a direct margin problem and a quality risk: unfamiliar staff have higher incident rates, lower resident satisfaction scores, and slower recognition of care-need changes. Agency reliance above 20–25% is a signal that the community's employee retention problem has progressed from a cost issue to an operational and safety issue. [unverified — training knowledge] Yet in many communities, agency usage is tracked only in the payroll report, not in the clinical or quality review.

## How to apply

Track agency labor reliance as a staffing quality metric, not only a cost metric.

```
Agency labor reliance tracking:
Metric: Agency direct-care hours ÷ Total direct-care hours × 100
Frequency: Weekly (not monthly — a monthly lag allows an acute staffing problem to persist)

Thresholds:
  <10% agency: healthy — some agency use is normal for PTO and sick coverage
  10–15%: monitor — investigate if trend is upward for 3+ consecutive weeks
  15–25%: elevated risk — trigger root-cause analysis and staff recruitment action
  >25%: critical — quality review, incident pattern review, and Executive Director escalation

Root cause triage for elevated agency reliance:
  - Turnover-driven: which positions are turning over? CNA, med tech, dining, other?
    → Retention root cause (exit survey data)
  - Scheduling-driven: is the PPD target set correctly for current census and acuity?
    → Re-evaluate if the staffing model is realistic for current acuity mix
  - Recruitment-driven: is there an adequate pool of qualified candidates in the market?
    → Recruiting strategy and compensation review

Quality signal check (when agency > 15%):
  [ ] Review incident/fall log for the same period — incident rate increase is a quality signal
  [ ] Check resident/family complaint log for the period — new complaints correlate with
      unfamiliar staff
  [ ] Survey state-specific staffing minimum requirement compliance (do not rely on agency
      hours to meet minimums — verify)
```

**Do:**
- Report agency reliance in the clinical/quality review meeting alongside the incident and fall data — the connection between unfamiliar staff and adverse events must be visible.
- Set a community-specific agency-reliance budget (dollar and percentage) approved by ownership; exceeding it requires Executive Director sign-off.
- Treat agency reliance reduction as a 90-day goal with a named owner, named positions, and a hiring plan — not a standing concern.

**Don't:**
- Treat agency reliance as a staffing department problem alone; it is a clinical, operational, and financial problem that requires Executive Director ownership.
- Use agency staff to fill positions that are vacant due to a known pay-rate problem without first addressing the pay structure — the underlying cause perpetuates.
- Report agency reliance only in dollar variance to budget; the hours-percentage is the quality and coverage signal.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

New community openings (first 6 months of operation) routinely require higher agency use while permanent staff is hired and trained — set a time-bound ramp plan and a month-over-month reduction target, not a standard threshold.
