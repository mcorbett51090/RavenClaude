# Move-In Readiness Reduces Lead Time and Increases Conversion

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Senior care operations / census development
**Applies to:** `senior-care-operations`

---

## Why this exists

A senior care community that loses a prospective resident between signed deposit and actual move-in is losing one of its highest-probability conversions. Time-to-move-in — the elapsed days between first inquiry and actual move-in — is not just a logistics metric; it is a conversion risk. Every additional day in the process is a day the family can reconsider, find an alternative, or experience a change in the care need that shifts them to a different setting. Communities with high time-to-move-in lose deposits they should not lose. The operational levers are unit readiness (how quickly a vacant unit can be prepared and offered), pre-admission paperwork, and care plan initiation — all of which can be streamlined without compromising clinical quality.

## How to apply

Track and manage time-to-move-in as a census KPI with specific sub-step benchmarks:

```
Move-In Readiness Dashboard — [Community] [Month]
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
Average time-to-move-in (inquiry to move-in):   ___ days  (target: ≤ 14 days)
Average time-to-move-in (deposit to move-in):   ___ days  (target: ≤ 7 days)

Process sub-steps:
  Inquiry to tour:                 ___ days  (target: ≤ 2 days)
  Tour to deposit:                 ___ days  (target: ≤ 5 days)
  Deposit to unit assignment:      ___ days  (target: ≤ 1 day)
  Unit assignment to unit ready:   ___ days  (target: ≤ 3 days)
  Unit ready to move-in:           ___ days  (target: ≤ 2 days)

Deposits lost before move-in (trailing 90d):  ___  (___% of total deposits)
  Primary reason:  [ ] Found alternative  [ ] Changed mind  [ ] Health change  [ ] Financial
```

**Unit readiness protocol:**

| Step | Responsible | Target completion |
|---|---|---|
| Vacancy notice (from current resident / family) | Resident services | Same day as notice received |
| Unit inspection (damage assessment) | Maintenance | Within 1 business day of vacancy |
| Cleaning and refresh initiated | Housekeeping | Within 24 hours of vacancy confirmed |
| Repair / painting (if needed) | Maintenance | Within 3 days |
| Furniture / equipment readiness confirmed | Admissions | Before deposit paperwork |
| Pre-admission nursing assessment scheduled | Nursing | Within 48 hours of deposit |
| Care plan initiated (draft) | Nursing | Before move-in date |

**Do:**
- Maintain a "pipeline ready" unit at all times in a community with >10% vacancy — a unit that can be shown and committed to immediately is a conversion advantage when a family is deciding under time pressure (hospital discharge, crisis placement).
- Assign a single move-in coordinator who owns the checklist from deposit to day-1 and communicates proactively with the family; uncertainty about the move-in process is a conversion risk driver.
- Track deposits lost by reason code — "found alternative" and "changed mind" warrant different responses; the former may indicate a competitive pricing or speed-to-offer problem, the latter may indicate a sales process follow-up gap.
- Set a target for the elapsed time between deposit and move-in and report it monthly alongside census — operators who don't measure it don't manage it.

**Don't:**
- Delay the care plan initiation until after move-in day — a day-1 care plan is both a clinical quality issue and a survey risk; the pre-admission assessment feeds the initial care plan.
- Use a single "average time to move in" figure without the sub-step breakdown — the bottleneck could be in the sales process, the unit preparation, or the clinical intake, and each requires a different fix.
- Accept a long time-to-move-in as normal for memory care or high-acuity admissions — the clinical complexity may justify an extra 2–3 days for assessment, but the sales and unit-prep steps can still be parallelized.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Emergency or crisis placements from hospitals or skilled nursing facilities often have a time-to-move-in of 24–72 hours regardless of process — in these cases the bottleneck is clinical assessment speed, not operational readiness, and the process should be designed to handle emergency admissions as a distinct workflow. CCRC life-care contract admissions involve legal and financial review processes that extend the timeline beyond the operational levers described here.
