# Care Plan Documentation Is the Survey and Liability Record

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Clinical documentation / survey readiness
**Applies to:** `senior-care-operations`

---

## Why this exists

The care plan is the legal and regulatory record of the care commitment the community made to the resident — and it is the primary document a surveyor reviews when investigating a complaint or an adverse event. A care plan that is absent, generic, outdated, or disconnected from the resident's actual care is a survey deficiency waiting to be written. In a civil claim following a fall, pressure injury, medication error, or elopement, the care plan (and its absence or inadequacy) is exhibit one for the plaintiff's attorney. Conversely, a well-documented, individualized, and regularly updated care plan is the community's strongest defense in both a survey and a legal proceeding — it demonstrates that the community knew the resident's needs and planned systematically to meet them.

## How to apply

Build care plan documentation into the care workflow as a clinical standard, not a paperwork afterthought.

```
Care plan documentation requirements:
1. Comprehensive assessment at move-in:
   - ADL status (bathing, dressing, toileting, transferring, continence, eating)
   - Cognitive status (standardized tool: MMSE, BIMS, or state-specific)
   - Fall risk (validated tool — see fall prevention rule)
   - Medication management level
   - Nutritional status and dining needs
   - Psychosocial and behavioral patterns
   - Family/responsible party involvement preferences

2. Care plan development (within 14 days of move-in, or per state regulation):
   - One goal per identified need
   - Specific, measurable interventions (not "assist as needed" — how? when? by whom?)
   - Responsible party for each intervention (e.g., "CNA on evening shift assists with shower
     Tues/Thurs/Sat using shower chair per resident preference")
   - Resident preferences documented and incorporated

3. Care plan update triggers:
   - Any change in condition (new diagnosis, hospitalization, significant weight change)
   - Any fall or incident
   - Any behavioral change or new behavioral concern
   - At each scheduled reassessment (minimum: quarterly or per state regulation)
   - At the resident's request or family request

4. Survey and legal readiness check (monthly):
   - % of residents with care plans updated within the required timeframe
     Target: 100% [unverified — regulatory minimum; verify state requirements]
   - % of care plans with measurable, individualized interventions (not generic language)
   - % of care plans updated after an incident (same day or next business day)
```

**Do:**
- Train care plan reviewers to flag generic language ("monitor for changes," "assist as needed") and require specificity before the plan is finalized.
- Update the care plan after every fall, behavioral incident, or hospitalization before the next shift ends — the update window is a documentation standard, not a goal.
- Keep the care plan in a format that front-line staff actually read — a 20-page document is not a care plan, it is a filing failure.

**Don't:**
- Use a copy-forward function in the EHR/care management system to roll over last quarter's care plan without review — it is both a survey risk and a clinical risk.
- Allow the care plan to lag behind the resident's actual condition — a resident who is now chair-bound with a care plan that says "ambulatory with cue" is a deficiency and a safety hazard.
- Confuse the care plan with the assessment — the assessment documents findings; the care plan documents the response. Both must exist and align.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Independent living communities with limited care service commitments have reduced care plan requirements, but still require documentation of any care services being provided and any special needs accommodations. Confirm state-specific requirements for the specific license type.
