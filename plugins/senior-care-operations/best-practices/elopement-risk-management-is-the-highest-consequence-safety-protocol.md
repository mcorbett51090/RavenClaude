# Elopement Risk Management Is the Highest-Consequence Safety Protocol

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Resident safety / memory care operations
**Applies to:** `senior-care-operations`

---

## Why this exists

An elopement — a cognitively impaired resident leaving the community unsupervised — is an event with potentially catastrophic consequences: injury, exposure, death, criminal investigation, license revocation, and immediate family legal action. It is among the most serious adverse events that can occur in a memory care or assisted-living community. Unlike many quality events that can be managed with incremental operational improvements, an elopement that results in serious harm produces permanent reputational, regulatory, and legal consequences. The prevention investment is unconditional — there is no acceptable trade-off between convenience, cost, or operational preference and elopement risk management.

## How to apply

Implement a layered elopement prevention program that covers access control, resident monitoring, staff training, and response protocol.

```
Elopement prevention layers (all required):
1. Identification and risk screening:
   - Elopement risk assessment at move-in for all residents with any cognitive impairment
   - Validated tool or structured assessment: history of elopement, wandering behavior,
     goal-directed exit-seeking behavior, time-of-day pattern
   - High-risk residents: documented in care plan with specific prevention measures
   - All staff informed of elopement-risk residents at shift start

2. Physical access control:
   - Secured perimeter: all external doors with delayed egress or coded locks
   - Alarm system: door alarms configured to alert within 15 seconds of breach
   - Monthly alarm test: documented and logged
   - Secured common spaces (memory care units): resident access controlled independently
     of AL common areas

3. Resident monitoring:
   - Wander management system (RFID bracelet, GPS pendant, or equivalent) for all
     memory care residents; review technology options against state-specific requirements
   - Hourly or more frequent rounds for high-risk residents documented in the care record
   - Visual checks on exit-seeking behavior documented

4. Staff training (annually, and at hire):
   - Elopement risk identification
   - Appropriate response to exit-seeking behavior (redirection, not physical restraint)
   - Emergency response protocol: search procedures, 911 call timing, family notification

5. Elopement response protocol (written document, posted at all nursing stations):
   - Immediate actions: secure the area, initiate search, designate a search coordinator
   - 911 call threshold: if not located within [community-specific time, typically 15 minutes]
   - Family notification: within [time threshold per state regulation]
   - Documentation: incident report within 24 hours; regulatory report per state requirements
```

**Do:**
- Conduct an unannounced quarterly drill for the elopement response protocol — a drill that only happens when scheduled reveals nothing about real response capability.
- Review the elopement risk assessment and wander management technology at every care plan update — risk level changes with disease progression.
- Report any elopement or near-elopement (resident found attempting to exit) at the next morning meeting as a clinical event, not just a security incident.

**Don't:**
- Rely solely on technology (alarms, pendants) without a trained human response — a door alarm that sounds and is ignored is not a prevention system.
- Use the term "wanderer" as a casual label without connecting it to the formal elopement risk assessment — not every ambulatory resident with dementia is an elopement risk, and mislabeling creates alert fatigue.
- Defer state-required incident reporting of an elopement on the basis of "no harm occurred" — most states require reporting regardless of outcome.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Independent living communities without cognitively impaired residents do not have elopement risk, but should have a protocol for recognizing a new cognitive concern in an existing resident and transitioning them appropriately to a higher care level.
