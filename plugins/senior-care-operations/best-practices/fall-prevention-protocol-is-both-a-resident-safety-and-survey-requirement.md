# Fall Prevention Protocol Is Both a Resident Safety and Survey Requirement

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Clinical quality / survey compliance
**Applies to:** `senior-care-operations`

---

## Why this exists

Falls are the leading cause of injury in older adults and are among the most cited deficiencies in assisted-living and senior care surveys. A community without a documented, individually tailored fall prevention protocol for each resident at elevated fall risk is simultaneously delivering substandard care and holding open a survey deficiency. Falls also trigger incident reporting requirements, potential family complaints, and in cases of serious injury, legal claims. The cost of a single fall-related fracture — in survey exposure, legal liability, family relations, and reputation damage — vastly exceeds the cost of a strong prevention program. Prevention is the operational and ethical obligation.

## How to apply

Build fall prevention as a resident-level protocol with documented assessment, care-plan integration, and a post-fall response process.

```
Fall prevention protocol components:

1. Fall risk assessment (at move-in and at each significant health change):
   - Validated tool: Morse Fall Scale, STRATIFY, or equivalent
   - Score documented in the care plan
   - High-risk residents (score above tool-specific threshold): individualized prevention plan

2. Individualized prevention plan (for each high-risk resident):
   - Environmental: bed height, call bell placement, lighting, floor surface, footwear
   - Clinical: medication review for fall-risk drugs (anticholinergics, benzodiazepines,
     anti-hypertensives, diuretics — coordinate with prescriber for medication management)
   - Activity: scheduled toileting, ambulation assistance, exercise program
   - Device: bed alarm, non-skid socks, assistive device, personal alarm pendant
   - Staff communication: shift handoff includes fall-risk residents

3. Monitoring:
   - Fall log reviewed weekly by the charge nurse or Director of Nursing
   - Post-fall huddle: within 24 hours of any fall — what happened, who was present, what changes are needed
   - Monthly fall rate tracked: falls per 100 resident-days
     Target: ≤3 falls per 100 resident-days [unverified — verify against CMS or state benchmark]

4. Post-fall documentation (required for every fall):
   - Incident report: time, location, witnesses, resident condition at time of fall
   - Medical assessment: vital signs, neurological check, any injury
   - Notification: physician, family/responsible party (timeframe per state regulation)
   - Care-plan update: was the prevention plan adequate? What changes are being made?

5. Survey readiness:
   - Fall logs and post-fall documentation available to surveyors on request
   - Care plans for high-risk residents must show active, individualized prevention measures
   - Any fall resulting in significant injury: review for reportable incident requirements
```

**Do:**
- Train all direct-care staff on fall prevention and the post-fall huddle process — a post-fall huddle that happens but is not documented is invisible to surveyors.
- Include medication management in the fall prevention plan — falls caused by poly-pharmacy are preventable, and the coordination with the prescriber must be documented.
- Review fall patterns by location (bathroom, bedroom, dining room) and time-of-day monthly — clustering reveals environmental or staffing factors.

**Don't:**
- Apply a generic fall prevention note to every high-risk resident's care plan — "implement standard fall precautions" is not an individualized plan and will be cited.
- Skip the post-fall assessment for a fall that did not result in observed injury — a fall without immediate visible injury can have a delayed presentation (subdural hematoma, hip fracture not immediately apparent in ambulatory residents).
- Allow the fall log to fall more than 30 days behind — survey requests for current fall data must be met without a scramble.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Memory care communities have additional fall risk factors related to dementia (wandering, spatial disorientation) and may require population-specific prevention protocols beyond the standard assisted-living framework. Apply the general framework with dementia-specific additions.
