# Plain language serves the citizen — and is required

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Citizen services / accessibility / communications
**Applies to:** `public-sector-govtech`

---

## Why this exists

The Plain Writing Act of 2010 (Pub. L. 111-274) requires federal executive agencies to use plain
language in all covered documents — letters, publications, forms, notices, and instructions sent
to the public issued or substantially revised after October 13, 2010. OMB implementation guidance
(M-11-15) provides the framework. This is not aspirational; it is a statutory requirement with
OMB oversight.

Beyond the legal mandate, plain language is fundamentally an equity issue. A benefits notice written
at a 16th-grade reading level effectively denies access to the populations who most need government
services — people with lower literacy, people reading in their second language, people under stress
who cannot parse bureaucratic prose. Government jargon is not neutral: it systematically advantages
educated professionals and disadvantages people already in vulnerable situations.

The cost of fixing unclear language after publication is also real: confused citizens generate
phone calls, errors on forms, and appeals — all of which consume agency resources that could be
directed at service delivery.

## How to apply

Apply the Federal Plain Language Guidelines (plainlanguage.gov):

1. **Active voice.** "The agency will review your application" — not "Applications will be reviewed
   by the agency." Active voice identifies who is acting.
2. **Short sentences.** Average sentence length ≤ 20 words for public-facing documents. Break
   compound sentences.
3. **Common words.** "Use" not "utilize." "Show" not "demonstrate." "Help" not "facilitate."
   "Must" not "is required to." When a technical term is unavoidable, define it on first use.
4. **Question-and-answer format** for instructions and guidance. "What do I need to submit?" answers
   the citizen's question directly.
5. **Headers and lists.** Break walls of text. Use headers to navigate; use bulleted lists for
   options; use numbered lists for sequences.
6. **"You" and "we."** Address the reader as "you." Use "we" or the agency name for the agency.
   Do not write in the impersonal third person when the audience is the individual citizen.
7. **Readability target.** Aim for Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level 6–8 for most public-facing documents.
   Benefits forms, notices, and instructions should target Grade 6.

**Do:**

- Run a readability score (Flesch-Kincaid) on every public-facing document before publication.
- User-test important documents with real members of the target population before publishing.
- Treat a plain-language review as part of the definition of done for any public-facing content sprint.
- Revise legacy documents opportunistically — the Act applies to substantially revised documents.

**Don't:**

- Use acronyms without spelling them out on first use (even common government acronyms).
- Use passive voice to avoid assigning responsibility ("Mistakes were made").
- Write a single sentence that spans three subordinate clauses.
- Substitute simpler explanations with a glossary link — define in context when possible.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- **Internal agency documents** not directed to the public are outside the Plain Writing Act's
  scope — but good plain-language practice is still beneficial for clarity and reduces misinterpretation.
- **Legal / regulatory text:** statutes and regulations have their own drafting conventions and
  often cannot be simplified without changing meaning. Plain language applies to explanatory documents
  about the law, not to the law itself.
- **Technical documentation for professional audiences:** a VPAT, a security control assessment, or
  a contracting officer technical representation does not need to be written at a 6th-grade level —
  but it still benefits from clear structure, active voice, and defined acronyms.
