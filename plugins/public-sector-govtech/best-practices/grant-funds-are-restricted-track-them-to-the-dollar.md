# Grant funds are restricted — track them to the dollar

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Grants management
**Applies to:** `public-sector-govtech`

---

## Why this exists

Federal grant money is not general operating revenue. Every dollar of a federal award is restricted
by the program statute, the applicable federal regulations, and the specific terms and conditions of
the Notice of Award. Spending grant funds on costs that are unallowable under 2 CFR 200, outside the
approved budget, or outside the period of performance is an audit finding — and potentially a
criminal violation of the False Claims Act or theft of federal funds if the organization certifies
compliance on financial reports while spending improperly.

Single audits (required for organizations expending ≥ $750K in federal awards annually) specifically
test whether grantees maintained proper accounting for federal award expenditures, applied costs only
to allowable categories, and did not commingle federal funds with other money. A finding that federal
funds were commingled or misapplied can trigger repayment demands, suspension of future awards, and
referral to the agency's Office of Inspector General.

The standard is clear: track every grant dollar from drawdown to expenditure to documented program
benefit, with documentation that would satisfy a single-audit auditor seeing it for the first time.

## How to apply

1. **Set up restricted-fund account codes before spending begins.** Federal funds must be accounted
   for separately from other funds (2 CFR 200.302(b)(3)). In most accounting systems: a unique fund
   code, grant number, and object-class coding for every transaction.
2. **Confirm allowability before every expenditure.** The test: is this cost necessary and reasonable
   for the program, allocable to the award, consistent with program regulations, and adequately
   documented? (2 CFR 200.405) If uncertain, ask the program officer before spending.
3. **Document every cost with source evidence.** Invoice + payment record + description of program
   benefit. For personnel: timesheets with grant-activity coding and supervisor certification.
4. **Track budget vs. actual by object class.** Know your budget status at all times. Do not
   inadvertently over-spend a budget category — budget modifications require prior agency approval
   in most cases (2 CFR 200.308).

**Do:**

- Assign dedicated account codes to every federal award before the first drawdown.
- Document costs at the time of expenditure — reconstruction after the fact is an audit risk.
- Request prior approval for any budget modification, scope change, or key personnel change.
- Monitor subrecipients — their compliance failures are your audit findings (2 CFR 200.332).

**Don't:**

- Commingle federal grant funds with unrestricted operating accounts, even temporarily.
- Allocate costs to a grant based on convenience rather than documented program benefit.
- Execute budget modifications without prior agency written approval when required.
- Pay a subrecipient without a subaward agreement that includes 2 CFR 200 flow-down clauses.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- **General purpose grants (formula grants):** some state/local formula grants have broader
  allowability parameters than project grants. The applicable statute and NOFO define the limits —
  but "fewer restrictions" does not mean "no restrictions." The same documentation standards apply.
- **De minimis items under $25:** trivial costs below materiality thresholds still require the
  same documentation discipline, though auditor focus is typically on material transactions.

## Provenance

2 CFR 200 (Uniform Guidance for Federal Financial Assistance), particularly Subpart D (Post-Federal
Award Requirements) and Subpart E (Cost Principles). Single audit requirements: 2 CFR 200 Subpart F
and OMB Compliance Supplement. Criminal provisions: 18 U.S.C. § 666 (theft or embezzlement from
federal programs) and the False Claims Act (31 U.S.C. §§ 3729–3733).
