# Public records are discoverable — write accordingly

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Government records / FOIA
**Applies to:** `public-sector-govtech`

---

## Why this exists

The Freedom of Information Act (5 U.S.C. § 552) and state public-records laws give the public a
legal right to access government agency records. This includes emails, Slack messages (if used for
agency business), meeting notes, contract files, procurement documents, grant records, and
internal policy drafts — subject to enumerated exemptions. Every employee, contractor, and
government official who creates a record in the course of government work should assume that record
could become public.

This is not a counsel of paranoia — it is a counsel of professionalism. Government officials who
write emails as if they are discoverable produce cleaner, more accurate, more defensible records.
They do not use informal channels to avoid documentation (which creates its own legal risk), and
they do not put casual speculation in writing that will be misread out of context. The discipline
produces better government and better records simultaneously.

The corollary: records that *should* be created often are not, because people avoid the paper trail.
This creates a different problem — the absence of documentation is itself a finding in a FOIA
lawsuit or an audit.

## How to apply

1. **Write substantive communications in the primary system of record.** If the decision was made
   in Slack and the email just says "per our conversation," the Slack record may be responsive
   to a FOIA request — and may say something you didn't intend to be the documented record.
2. **Distinguish deliberative process from final decisions.** Pre-decisional deliberations may be
   protected by FOIA Exemption 5 (deliberative process privilege), but the final decision document
   is generally not. Write final decisions clearly in the primary record system.
3. **Do not use personal email or unofficial channels to avoid FOIA.** This is a legal violation
   independent of the FOIA itself (Federal Records Act, Presidential Records Act, state equivalents).
   Courts and IGs treat the use of non-official channels to avoid public-records laws as evidence
   of intent to conceal.
4. **Assume records are retained.** Even "deleted" records are often recoverable from backups,
   litigation holds, or federal records management systems. Write as if retention is permanent.

**Do:**

- Use the agency's or organization's official communication and document management systems.
- Document decisions and their rationale in the primary record — not just in meeting notes that
  may not be retained.
- Label pre-decisional deliberative materials clearly if they are legitimately privileged.
- Cooperate fully with FOIA requests; the obligation to search is broad.

**Don't:**

- Use personal email, consumer Slack, or other unofficial channels for substantive government business.
- Put casual speculation, informal nicknames for people, or off-the-record commentary in a written
  record — it will look different in a production.
- Withhold records claiming Exemption 5 without segregating releasable portions (segregability is required).
- Delete records outside of an authorized records schedule.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- **Purely personal records:** notes that are not used in agency business, do not reflect agency
  policy, and were created and retained solely for personal use are not "agency records" subject
  to FOIA. The line is context-specific and often litigated.
- **Congressional records:** congressional offices are not subject to FOIA (only executive agencies).
- **Classified information:** Exemption 1 protects properly classified national security information,
  but classification cannot be used to shield records that should not have been classified.
