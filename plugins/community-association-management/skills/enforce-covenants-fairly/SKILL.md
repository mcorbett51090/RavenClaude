# Skill: enforce-covenants-fairly

> **Invoked by:** `governance-and-covenant-specialist` (primary). Also consulted by `association-management-lead` for the fine-revenue/budget and operational side.
>
> **When to invoke:** "How do I enforce a covenant without getting sued?"; "an owner won't repaint / remove the trailer / stop the short-term rental"; "how do I deny an architectural request fairly?"; "are we exposed to a selective-enforcement claim?"; any covenant/architectural/rules enforcement question.
>
> **Output:** a fair-and-consistent enforcement ladder (covenant cited → notice → hearing → consistent penalty → record) with the selective-enforcement guardrail, captured in [`../../templates/covenant-enforcement-and-collections-timeline.md`](../../templates/covenant-enforcement-and-collections-timeline.md), and the legal determination flagged to counsel.

> ⚠️ **Whether a specific fine or enforcement action is lawful is a legal determination that varies by US state and the governing documents — this skill is operational guidance, NOT legal advice.** Hearing and notice requirements before a fine, the enforceability of a given covenant, and fair-housing implications differ by state; route the legal question to the association's counsel.

## Procedure

1. **Name the enforcement sub-branch and confirm the source.** Covenant (recorded CC&Rs), architectural guideline, or board-adopted rule — traverse [`../../knowledge/community-association-decision-tree.md`](../../knowledge/community-association-decision-tree.md), then locate the **specific recorded provision** the owner allegedly violated. Vague "you're bothering neighbors" is not enforceable; the recorded provision is. See [`../../knowledge/community-association-patterns-2026.md`](../../knowledge/community-association-patterns-2026.md).
2. **Check consistency FIRST — the guardrail before the action.** Has the association enforced this same provision against other owners? **Selective, arbitrary, or retaliatory enforcement** — acting against one owner and ignoring the identical violation next door — is the fastest route to a lost lawsuit and can trigger fair-housing/discrimination exposure. If enforcement has been inconsistent, fix the policy across the board before pursuing one owner.
3. **Give written notice.** Notice of the violation stating the **provision cited**, the specific condition, and **what cures it** — with a reasonable cure period.
4. **Provide an opportunity to be heard (hearing) before a penalty attaches.** The owner's chance to respond to the board (or a covenants committee) before a fine takes effect — required in many states (verify). The hearing is not a formality; it is what makes the eventual penalty defensible.
5. **Apply a published, consistent penalty schedule.** A board-adopted, evenly-applied fine/penalty schedule — not an ad-hoc amount invented for this owner. Escalate per the schedule (e.g. warning → fine → increasing fines), documenting each step.
6. **Keep a documented record.** Dates, the notices sent (and how), photos/evidence, the hearing and the owner's response, and the decision — so the process is *provable*. The record is the defense.
7. **Run architectural review on the same fairness discipline.** For approving/denying owner modifications: published standards, a defined review process, timely decisions, and consistent application — a denial with no standard cited and no timely response is as exposed as an inconsistent fine.
8. **State the seams and flip conditions.** Route the fine-revenue/budget impact to the management lead, the legal determination to counsel, and name what would change the enforcement call.

## Worked example

> User: "An owner has an RV parked in their driveway against the CC&Rs. I want to fine them. How?"

- **Sub-branch + source:** covenant enforcement; locate the **recorded CC&R provision** on RV/vehicle parking — cite the section, not "it looks bad."
- **Consistency check FIRST:** are there other RVs/boats in driveways the association has *not* acted on? If yes, fining this one owner is **selective enforcement** — the likely losing side of a lawsuit. Fix the policy community-wide (notice all violators consistently) before pursuing one.
- **Notice:** written notice citing the provision, the RV, the cure (remove/relocate by a date).
- **Hearing:** offer the owner a hearing before any fine attaches (required in many states — verify).
- **Penalty:** apply the **published** fine schedule (e.g. warning → $X → escalating), evenly, documented at each step.
- **Record:** dated notices, a photo, the hearing outcome — the file that makes the fine defensible.
- **Seam:** whether the fine amount and the covenant are lawful/enforceable here is **counsel's** determination; the fine revenue's budget treatment routes to the management lead.

## Guardrails

- **Consistency is the whole game** — selective/arbitrary/retaliatory enforcement loses the case you'd otherwise win; check it before acting.
- **No enforcement without a cited recorded provision, notice, and a hearing** — the process, not the outcome, makes a fine defensible.
- The penalty is a **published, evenly-applied schedule**, never an ad-hoc amount for one owner.
- **Document everything** — dates, notices, evidence, the hearing, the decision — the record is the defense.
- Architectural review runs the same discipline: published standards, defined process, timely decisions, consistent application.
- **Not legal advice** — whether a fine/enforcement is lawful, the hearing/notice requirements, and fair-housing implications are **state-specific legal determinations** that route to counsel; volatile claims carry a **retrieval date**.
