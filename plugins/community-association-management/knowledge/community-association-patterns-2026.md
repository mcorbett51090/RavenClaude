## Reserve study — the spine of the budget

A **reserve study** is the plan for funding the repair and replacement of an association's major common-area components. It has two halves:

- **Physical analysis (component inventory):** every reserve component (roofs, roads/asphalt, elevators, pools, siding, fences, mechanicals), its **estimated replacement cost**, and its **remaining useful life (RUL)**.
- **Financial analysis:** the current reserve **balance**, the **fully-funded balance** (the accrued share of every component's cost, given its age), the resulting **percent funded**, and a multi-year **funding plan**.

**Percent funded = reserve balance ÷ fully-funded balance.** It is the honest measure of reserve health — a healthy *balance* means nothing without the fully-funded liability behind it. Rough industry framing treats higher percent funded as lower special-assessment risk; the exact bands are a professional judgment. _(Consensus framing, 2026-07-13; the reserve study itself and its adequacy certification are a **reserve specialist's/engineer's** determination — this team reads and frames the study, it does not certify it.)_

**Reserve studies are typically prepared/updated on a cycle** (a full study periodically, with lighter updates between) — some states and lenders (e.g. secondary-mortgage-market requirements) mandate a study and/or a minimum reserve contribution. _(Requirement varies by state and lender — verify, 2026-07-13.)_

## Funding plans — full, baseline, threshold

| Plan | Goal | Trade-off |
|---|---|---|
| **Full funding** | Reserve balance tracks ~100% of the fully-funded balance over time | Highest dues; lowest special-assessment risk |
| **Baseline (cash-flow) funding** | Keep the reserve balance above **zero** across the projection — never runs dry | Lowest dues; **highest** special-assessment risk when components cluster |
| **Threshold funding** | Fund to a chosen **percent-funded floor** — a middle path | The threshold is a documented policy choice by the board |

The reserve study models these; the board chooses the funding plan as policy. Baseline keeps dues lowest and is exactly the choice that most often produces a surprise special assessment. _(Consensus framing, 2026-07-13.)_

## Special assessment vs association loan — the funding failure modes

When a component fails while the reserve is short, the board faces two ways to cover it:

- **Special assessment** — a one-time charge levied on owners (a lump sum, or over a few installments). Immediate, no interest, but politically painful and a hardship for some owners. Approval/notice requirements vary by state and the governing documents (some cap the amount the board can levy without a member vote — verify).
- **Association loan** — the association borrows (often secured by its right to levy assessments) and repays through assessments over years. Spreads the cost, but adds interest and a lender relationship.

Model both honestly against a **properly funded reserve** to show what adequate funding would have avoided — don't let "keep dues low" hide the coming bill. **The approval thresholds and any member-vote requirement are state- and document-specific — verify, and route the legal question to counsel.**

## Assessment collections — prevention, then a STATE-SPECIFIC ladder

> **Collections/lien law varies materially by US state, the statutes change, and this is operational guidance — NOT legal advice.** The association's assessment lien is what lets it secure and ultimately collect unpaid assessments — a **statutory** process with strict notice, timing, and (in some states) pre-lien and pre-foreclosure requirements that differ by state. A mis-stepped notice can void the lien. Attach the **state + a retrieval date** to every specific claim and route the actual legal question to the association's counsel.

**Prevention (cheapest control):**

- **Autopay / ACH enrolment** and a clear, board-adopted **collection policy** applied consistently to every owner — the single most effective control and itself evidence of consistency.
- **Clear, agreement-stated late fees and interest** applied on schedule (amounts/caps vary by state — verify).

**The collections ladder (typical *sequence*; exact days/notices/methods are state-specific — verify):**

1. **Assessment due → grace → late fee** — first late fee/interest after grace, per the policy and state.
2. **Reminder / demand letter** — a documented reminder, then a formal demand for the balance.
3. **Payment-plan offer** — offer a reasonable payment plan (some states *require* one be offered before further action — verify).
4. **Pre-lien notice** — the notice of intent to lien, with the required contents and delivery method, after the required delay (highly state-specific).
5. **Record the assessment lien** — record the lien against the unit/lot for the unpaid amount, fees, and costs, per the state's contents and timing rules.
6. **Foreclosure (where applicable)** — some states permit foreclosing the assessment lien (judicial or non-judicial), often with thresholds (a minimum amount owed and/or months delinquent) and further notice. **This is the highest-stakes step and squarely a legal matter — counsel-led.**

**The point:** run collections as a disciplined, consistent, state-specific ladder — consistency protects against a selective-enforcement/discrimination claim, and a mis-stepped pre-lien or lien notice can void the lien. Prevention (autopay + a consistent policy) is far cheaper than any step of the process.

## Covenant / architectural / rules enforcement — fair and consistent

The declaration/**CC&Rs**, bylaws, architectural guidelines, and board-adopted **rules** are the enforceable framework. Fair, defensible enforcement runs the same ladder every time:

1. **Cite the specific covenant/rule** the owner allegedly violated — vague "you're bothering neighbors" is not enforceable; the recorded provision is.
2. **Notice** — written notice of the violation, the provision cited, and what cures it.
3. **Opportunity to be heard (hearing)** — the owner's chance to respond before a fine/penalty attaches (required in many states — verify).
4. **Consistent penalty schedule** — a published, evenly applied fine/penalty schedule, not an ad-hoc amount.
5. **Documented record** — dates, notices, photos, the hearing, and the decision, so the process is provable.

**The guardrail:** **selective, arbitrary, or retaliatory enforcement** — fining one owner and ignoring the identical violation next door — is the fastest route to a lost lawsuit and can trigger fair-housing/discrimination exposure. Consistency is the whole game. **Architectural review** (approving/denying owner modifications) runs on the same fairness discipline: published standards, a defined review process, timely decisions, and consistent application. **Whether a specific fine or enforcement is lawful is a legal determination — flag it to counsel.** _(Consensus framing, 2026-07-13; hearing/notice requirements vary by state — verify.)_

## Board governance & fiduciary duty

- **Fiduciary duty:** directors owe duties of **care** (be informed, act diligently) and **loyalty** (act in the association's interest, not self-interest — avoid conflicts and self-dealing).
- **Business-judgment rule:** courts generally defer to a board decision that was made **through a defensible process** — informed, conflict-free, and within the governing documents and applicable law — even if the outcome is poor. It protects **process, not result**; a board with no conflict check and no documentation forfeits the protection. _(General common-law framing; the exact standard and any statutory modification vary by state — not legal advice.)_
- **Open-meeting & records rules:** most states require board meetings to be **open to owners**, properly **noticed**, with a **quorum**, **minutes**, and owner **access to records**. Decisions made outside a proper meeting, or records improperly withheld, are challengeable. Executive session is limited to defined topics (litigation, personnel, delinquent-owner hearings — varies by state).
- **Elections & quorum:** follow the governing documents and state election rules for notice, ballots/proxies, quorum, and counting. **Blown quorum** is the most common failure — plan for proxies, adjournment, and any **reduced-quorum** provision for a reconvened meeting.

## Management company vs self-managed

| Model | Fits when | Watch out for |
|---|---|---|
| **Self-managed** | Small association, engaged volunteer board, simple common areas | Volunteer burnout, knowledge gaps, single-person key-holder risk; the board still owes the full fiduciary duty |
| **Management company (CAM)** | Larger/more complex association, significant common areas, collections/enforcement volume | Cost; the board **cannot delegate the fiduciary duty** — the manager executes, the board decides and signs; manage the contract (scope, term, fees, portfolio-manager caseload) |
| **Hybrid / portfolio vs on-site** | Mid-size; portfolio manager covers several associations, or an on-site manager for a large one | Portfolio-manager attention is spread thin; define response-time and reporting expectations |

The board is the fiduciary in **every** model — a management company is a hired hand that runs the day-to-day, not a transfer of the duty. Vendor management (bids, scope, term) is a board control. _(Consensus framing, 2026-07-13.)_

- **Community-association-management (CAM) platforms:** **AppFolio**, **Vantaca**, **TOPS** and **CINC** (TOPS/CINC are related under common ownership) — accounting, assessments/billing, autopay, violation/architectural tracking, owner portals, communications, and reporting; feature sets and integrations vary by platform. _(Retrieved 2026-07-13; parity changes — verify.)_
- **CAI — Community Associations Institute:** the primary industry body for community associations — education, credentials (e.g. the CMCA/AMS/PCAM manager designations), model practices, and advocacy. A useful reference for standards and guidance, not a legal authority. _(Retrieved 2026-07-13.)_
- **Reserve-study providers / standards:** reserve studies follow a recognized professional methodology (component inventory + financial analysis + funding plan); some are prepared by credentialed reserve specialists/engineers. - **Insurance / master policy:** the association's **master policy** typically covers common-area **property**, **general liability**, **directors-&-officers (D&O)**, and **fidelity/crime** — with the condo **unit-boundary** question (bare-walls vs all-in vs original-specifications) determining where the master policy stops and an owner's HO-6 begins. _(Coverage norms and the unit-boundary convention vary by state and the declaration — verify.)_

## State-law variance (dated — volatile, re-verify before quoting)

- Community associations are governed primarily by **state statute plus the association's own governing documents** (declaration/CC&Rs, bylaws, rules). The statutes **differ materially by state** — California's **Davis-Stirling Common Interest Development Act** is a prominent example, and most states have their own HOA/COA act (many drawing on the Uniform Common Interest Ownership Act, UCIOA, to varying degrees). _(Directional, 2026-07-13.)_
- Everything volatile — assessment-lien and foreclosure requirements, pre-lien and payment-plan mandates, open-meeting and election rules, hearing requirements before fines, reserve-study/funding mandates — **varies by state and changes.** Treat any specific requirement as a snapshot, attach a **state + retrieval date**, and route the legal determination to counsel. This team gives **operational guidance, not legal advice.**
