## The four elements every treatment note must establish

| Element | What it proves | Failure = |
|---|---|---|
| Skilled service | a licensed therapist was required (not aide/HEP) | "maintenance" denial |
| Medical necessity | care was needed for this condition now | necessity denial |
| Progress toward goals | movement against measurable POC goals | "no progress" denial |
| Timed-minute basis | the minutes that justify the billed units | units denial / audit |

A note missing any element turns a delivered service into a denial.

## Plan-of-care requirements

- Measurable, functional goals (not "improve strength" but "ascend 12 stairs with rail, no rest").
- Justified frequency and duration tied to the condition.
- Diagnosis and medical-necessity linkage.
- Correct initial certification and recertification timing — a lapsed cert can invalidate good care.

## The 8-minute rule as a documentation requirement

Before it is a billing calculation, the 8-minute rule is a documentation requirement: the **total
timed minutes must be recorded at the point of care**. Reconstructed minutes are an audit red flag.
The billing analyst converts minutes to units; the note must contain the minutes.

## Skilled vs. unskilled

The recurring denial battleground. Document *why* the service required therapist skill: clinical
decision-making, progression/modification, assessment, safety judgment — not just the exercise
performed. "Patient performed exercises" reads as unskilled; "therapist progressed and modified based
on assessment of X" reads as skilled.

## Audit-readiness sampling

Periodically sample notes against the four elements, the timed-minute basis, and certification timing.
Prioritize the highest-risk gaps: units without minutes, necessity not established, lapsed certs,
skilled rationale absent.

## Defensible-documentation principle

Payers reimburse *evidence* of care, not care. Authored at the point of care, the note is the
evidence; reconstructed later, it is a liability. This is the documentation-defends-the-claim rule in
practice.
