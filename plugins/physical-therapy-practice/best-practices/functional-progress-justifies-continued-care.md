# Functional progress justifies continued care

**Status:** Pattern.

**Rule:** Continued, reimbursable visits are justified by documented functional progress toward a goal
and the remaining gap to that goal. Measured progress against MCID is the strongest evidence of medical
necessity for the visits that remain.

## Why

The recurring payer challenge to an extended episode is "why does this patient still need skilled
therapy?" The most defensible answer is functional: a validated measure showing meaningful change
(past MCID) from baseline, plus a quantified gap still to close toward the functional goal. That
evidence simultaneously demonstrates the care is working (so it should continue) and that the goal
isn't yet met (so it isn't maintenance). A clinic that documents only visits and exercises, without
functional measurement, has a weaker necessity argument exactly when it needs the strongest.

## What it looks like in practice

- Functional measures are captured at intervals and the change is reported against MCID and baseline.
- The plan-of-care goal is functional and measurable, so the remaining gap is quantifiable.
- The continued-care rationale cites the measure, the change, and the remaining gap — not visit count.

## Anti-pattern

Justifying continued care with "patient is improving" and a visit tally, with no functional
measurement behind it — the weakest necessity argument, and the one most likely to lose on review.
Verify program/payer specifics against current policy.
