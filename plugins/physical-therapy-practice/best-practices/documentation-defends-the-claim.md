# Documentation defends the claim

**Status:** Absolute rule.

**Rule:** A delivered service that isn't defensibly documented is a denial waiting to happen and an
audit risk. Documentation is the evidence of skilled, medically necessary care that justifies
reimbursement — authored as care happens, never paperwork bolted on afterward.

## Why

Payers and auditors don't reimburse care; they reimburse *evidence* of care. A note that fails to
establish skilled service, medical necessity, progress toward measurable goals, and the timed-minute
basis for its units cannot defend the claim — regardless of how good the actual treatment was.
Reconstructed-after-the-fact documentation is itself an audit red flag. The clinic that documents
defensibly at the point of care keeps the revenue it earns; the one that documents loosely funds a
denial-and-appeal treadmill.

## What it looks like in practice

- Every treatment note establishes the four elements: skilled service, medical necessity, progress,
  timed-minute basis.
- The plan of care carries measurable goals, justified frequency/duration, and correct
  certification/recertification timing.
- Timed minutes are recorded as care happens, so units are defensible.

## Anti-pattern

Treating documentation as billing paperwork done at end of day from memory. The hook flags billing
decisions stated with no medical-necessity or documentation basis. Verify specifics against current
CMS/payer policy and a compliance professional.
