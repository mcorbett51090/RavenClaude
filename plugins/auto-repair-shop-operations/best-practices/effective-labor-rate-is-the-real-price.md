# The effective labor rate is the real price

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Shop economics / P&L
**Applies to:** `auto-repair-shop-operations`

> Advisory operations/financial rule. Rate norms and GP targets are `[verify-at-use]` against the shop's own numbers. No customer PII.

---

## Why this exists

The posted door rate is a sign on the wall. What the shop actually collects per billed hour — the **effective labor rate (ELR)** — is the door rate minus every source of erosion: discounts, lower warranty rates, comeback (rework) labor billed at zero, and unapplied time that never lands on an RO. A shop reading its profitability off the door rate is flattering itself; two shops with the same posted rate can have wildly different ELRs and therefore different labor gross profit. The gap between the door rate and the ELR is **recoverable margin the shop is already entitled to** — which is why closing that gap comes before any conversation about raising the posted rate.

## How to apply

- Compute ELR = total labor dollars collected ÷ total billed hours; track it as the headline labor number.
- Quantify each erosion source (discounts, warranty, comebacks, unapplied time) and attack the biggest first.
- Read labor GP and parts GP **separately** — never blend them into one number.
- Raise the door rate only after the ELR-to-door-rate gap is recovered, not as a substitute for recovering it.

**Do:** measure ELR; close discount/comeback/unapplied-time leaks first.
**Don't:** report the door rate as the price; blend labor and parts margin; raise the posted rate to paper over erosion.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

A genuine market repositioning (the door rate is simply below the local market for the shop's quality tier) can justify a posted-rate increase on its own — but confirm that against the market (`[verify-at-use]`) rather than using it to avoid fixing internal erosion.
