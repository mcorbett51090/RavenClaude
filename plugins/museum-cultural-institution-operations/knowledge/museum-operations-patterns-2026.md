## Collections lifecycle & ethics

The object's journey and the ethics gate at each step:

| Stage | What happens | The ethics/quality gate |
|---|---|---|
| **Accession** | Formally add to the permanent collection (gift, purchase, bequest) | Provenance established (1970-UNESCO, Nazi-era 1933–45, legal title); fits the collecting scope; capacity to care for it |
| **Cataloging** | Object ID, accession number, medium/dimensions, provenance, rights, location in the CMS | Documented to a standard, in the CMS — not a spreadsheet the museum outgrows |
| **Storage & care** | Environmental controls (temp, RH, light, IPM), housing, handling | Preventive conservation; a stable, monitored environment |
| **Condition reporting** | Documented state at every transfer (loan, move, install) | A report at *every* transfer — the cheap insurance against an avoidable loss |
| **Loans (in/out)** | Object travels with a loan agreement, insurance, courier | Wall-to-wall / nail-to-nail insurance; condition report both ends; facility report |
| **Deaccession** | Ethical removal from the collection | Proceeds fund **collection care / acquisition ONLY** (AAM/AAMD); board approval; documented rationale; policy-compliant |
| **Repatriation / NAGPRA** | Return of Native American / culturally affiliated / looted material | Consult source community; comply with NAGPRA and cultural-patrimony law; consent before display/publish |

**The cardinal rule:** the collection is a **public trust**. Deaccessioning to fund operations, a deficit, or a capital project is the field's brightest ethical line — proceeds are restricted to collection care or new acquisition, full stop.

### Provenance & due diligence red flags

- **1970 UNESCO Convention** — for antiquities/archaeological material, an unbroken ownership/export history back to 1970 is the benchmark; gaps are a looting/illicit-export risk.
- **Nazi-era (1933–1945)** — for European works that changed hands in this window, research for looting/forced-sale (Washington Principles).
- **Cultural patrimony / NAGPRA** — Native American human remains, funerary/sacred objects, and objects of cultural patrimony carry repatriation obligations; source-community consultation is mandatory, not courtesy.
- **The rule:** a red flag **holds** the transaction until researched and cleared — you do not accession, loan, or acquire on hope.

## The exhibition project lifecycle

Permanent, temporary, and traveling exhibitions run as **projects** with a critical path:

1. **Concept & feasibility** — the idea, the audience, the scholarly premise, the fit with mission; a go/no-go with a rough budget.
2. **Checklist & loans** — the object checklist; loan requests to lenders (with facility reports, insurance, and lead times of 12–24 months for major loans).
3. **Interpretation & design** — narrative, labels, graphics, accessibility (large print, tactile, audio, plain language), exhibition design & fabrication.
4. **Budget** — direct costs (loans, shipping, insurance, fabrication, conservation, marketing) against **earned** (admission uplift, catalog/retail, sponsorship, traveling fees) and **contributed** (grants, sponsorship) revenue — costed *with* the operations-lead.
5. **Schedule & install** — critical path back-planned from opening; condition-report every object on arrival; install & lighting; environmental sign-off.
6. **Run & program** — public programs, education tie-ins, evaluation data capture.
7. **De-install & evaluation** — condition-report on de-install; return loans; **evaluate** (attendance, revenue, learning outcomes, visitor studies) and feed it back.

> Temporary and traveling shows are the earned-revenue engine and the audience-refresh engine — but a blockbuster that starves collection care, staff, or mission is earned revenue eating the institution. Weigh it.

## Membership & the earned-vs-contributed revenue mix

### Membership is a retention business

- **Tier for behavior, not vanity** — visit frequency, guest passes, reciprocal-network access (ROAM/NARM-style), member previews, shop/café discounts. The benefits should map to how a member actually uses the museum.
- **First-year renewal is the number that moves the model.** Acquisition without a renewal engine is a leaky bucket. Track acquisition → first-year renewal → multi-year retention → upgrade separately.
- **Membership straddles earned and contributed** — a low-tier membership is largely a transactional benefit exchange (earned-ish); a patron/upper tier shades into philanthropy (contributed). Model where each tier sits.

### The earned-vs-contributed mix

- **Earned** — admissions, membership (transactional tiers), retail/shop, café, **venue rental**, program fees, traveling-exhibition fees, image licensing.
- **Contributed** — individual development (patron circles, major gifts), corporate sponsorship, galas, **grants** (government/foundation), **endowment** draw, planned/legacy gifts.
- **The pattern:** name a **target mix** and **diversify**. A museum ~90% one line — all-earned (fragile to a footfall shock) or all-endowment (fragile to a market shock) — is one crisis from trouble. The mix is a deliberate target with named levers, re-checked annually.

## Admissions & visitor experience

- **Pricing models** — free/suggested-donation (access-first), timed ticketing (flow/capacity), dynamic/tiered (yield), pay-what-you-wish/free days (barrier-lowering). Each is an **access-vs-revenue trade-off** — cost the access in, name the funding source. (Full branch in the decision tree.)
- **Accessibility** — physical (ADA/step-free, seating), sensory (quiet hours, sensory maps), cognitive (plain-language labels), and financial (free days, access memberships). Accessibility is mission, and often a funder/accreditation expectation.
- **Evaluation / visitor studies** — front-end (before design), formative (prototyping), summative (post-opening): attendance, dwell time, learning outcomes, satisfaction. Evaluation is how you know the mission spend worked.
- **Earned-revenue amenities** — shop, café/restaurant, and **venue rental** are real earned lines; manage them as a business without letting the rental calendar crowd out the public mission.

## Digital collections & access

- **Open access** — many major museums release public-domain collection images under **CC0 / CC-BY** to expand reach; the mission case is strong where rights allow. Locking up public-domain works leaves reach on the table.
- **IIIF** (International Image Interoperability Framework) — the standard for serving/zooming/annotating high-res images interoperably (Image API + Presentation API/manifests); pairs with a IIIF viewer (Mirador/Universal Viewer). _(Retrieved 2026-07-09.)_
- **Rights statements** — use standardized rights labels (rightsstatements.org, Creative Commons) so users know what they can do with an image.
- **DAMS** — a digital-asset-management system as the source of truth for media, feeding the online catalog and IIIF.
- **Cultural sensitivity** — traditional-knowledge (TK) labels and source-community consultation gate the publication of culturally sensitive or restricted material — consent before publish.
- **Virtual exhibitions & online catalog** — extend the physical show's reach and serve researchers; a mission-and-audience multiplier, not a replacement for the object.

## Governance & standards

| Framework | What it is | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **AAM Accreditation** | The American Alliance of Museums' peer-reviewed seal of institutional excellence | The field's benchmark; requires the **Core Documents** and an operating audit — treat prep as discipline, not paperwork |
| **AAM Core Documents** | Mission statement, institutional code of ethics, strategic institutional plan, disaster/emergency plan, collections-management policy | The minimum governance substrate; accreditation and good operation both rest on them |
| **AAMD** | Association of Art Museum Directors — professional practice, esp. **deaccession ethics** | Sets the deaccession-proceeds restriction and reporting norms for art museums |
| **ICOM Code of Ethics** | International Council of Museums' ethics code | The global ethics baseline (acquisition, provenance, repatriation, professional conduct) |
| **Board governance / nonprofit status** | 501(c)(3) (US) board of trustees, fiduciary duty, tax-exempt compliance | The legal + governance frame the whole operation sits inside |
