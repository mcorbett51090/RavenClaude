# Fee Proposal Must State Exclusions, Not Just Inclusions

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Architecture/AEC
**Applies to:** `architecture-aec`

---

## Why this exists

A fee proposal that describes only what is included leaves the scope boundary implicit — and every implicit boundary becomes a future dispute. The client who reads "design and construction documents for a 10,000 sf office renovation" does not know whether specialty lighting design, acoustic consulting, commissioning, LEED documentation, or furniture and equipment coordination are included or excluded. When the architect excludes them and the client expected them, the additional-services authorization is resisted because there was nothing in the proposal to warn the client. Explicit exclusions establish the boundary at proposal stage, when the client can negotiate rather than dispute.

## How to apply

Every fee proposal includes an Exclusions section adjacent to the Scope of Services:

```
Fee Proposal — Exclusions from Basic Services
────────────────────────────────────────────────
The following services are NOT included in the Basic Services fee above.
They may be engaged as Additional Services at agreed rates.

  [ ] Specialty design: acoustic, lighting, AV/IT, security, signage/wayfinding
  [ ] Geotechnical investigation and report
  [ ] Environmental assessment (Phase I/II)
  [ ] Commissioning (Cx) and retro-commissioning
  [ ] LEED / green-building certification documentation
  [ ] Furniture, fixtures, and equipment (FF&E) specification
  [ ] Third-party peer review (structural, energy)
  [ ] Cost estimating (if owner elects independent estimator)
  [ ] Permitting fees, reproduction costs, travel (reimbursables, invoiced at cost)
  [ ] Construction-phase services beyond [# of site visits per month stated]
  [ ] Record drawings / as-builts beyond [format stated]
  [ ] Post-occupancy evaluation

Additional exclusions specific to this project:
  - _______________________________________________
```

**Do:**
- Customize the exclusions list for the project type — a residential project has a different standard exclusions set than an institutional lab building.
- Flag the two or three most likely additional-service requests on this specific project in the proposal narrative so the client is forewarned.
- Attach the exclusions list to the final signed contract, not just the proposal — the proposal may not be incorporated by reference.

**Don't:**
- Bury the exclusions at the end of the proposal in fine print; place them immediately after the scope narrative.
- Use catch-all language ("all items not listed above are excluded") without specific examples — vague exclusion language is unenforceable in most jurisdictions.
- Omit the out-of-scope rate (or a reference to the attached rate schedule) — the exclusion is incomplete without the client knowing what the additional service will cost if needed.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Design-build or integrated project delivery agreements may consolidate scope in a single contract document where the exclusions list is embedded in the contract scope matrix rather than the proposal. The discipline of explicit exclusions still applies; the format adapts.
