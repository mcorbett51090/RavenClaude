# Site Observation Reports Protect the Architect of Record

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Architecture/AEC
**Applies to:** `architecture-aec`

---

## Why this exists

A site visit without a written observation report is a liability event that happened without a defense. When a claim arises over construction quality, sequence, or a deviation from the documents, the first question is "what did the architect observe, and when?" An architect who visited the site but has no contemporaneous record is in a worse position than one who never visited — because they were there, they had an obligation, and they cannot document what they did. The site observation report (SOR) is the architect's professional record, their notice-to-contractor instrument, and their claims defense simultaneously.

## How to apply

Issue a written SOR within 24 hours of every site visit:

```
Site Observation Report
────────────────────────
Project:         ________________
Visit date:      ________________  Visit #: ___
Present:         ________________
Weather:         ________________
Work in place:
  Structural:    ________________
  MEP rough-in:  ________________
  Enclosure:     ________________
  Finishes:      ________________

Work observed in general conformance with contract documents:  [ ] Yes  [ ] No

Non-conforming work or concerns:
  | # | Location | Description | Action required | Due date |
  |---|----------|-------------|-----------------|----------|

Contractor response on prior open items:
  | Prior # | Status | Notes |
  |---------|--------|-------|

Overall construction quality assessment:  ________________
Next scheduled visit:  ________________
Distribution:  [ ] Owner  [ ] Contractor  [ ] File

Architect of record:  ________________  Date: ________
```

**Do:**
- Issue the SOR within 24 hours — memory degrades quickly on construction details; same-day is the standard.
- Issue non-conformance notices as a separate formal document (not just an SOR note) when a deficiency requires the contractor to stop, remove, or replace work.
- Keep the SOR in the project record and distribute it to the owner and contractor — the distribution record is part of the defense.

**Don't:**
- Write "work appears to be proceeding per documents" without specifying what was observed — the SOR must describe what the architect actually saw, not a generic approval.
- Use the SOR to express opinions about means and methods (that is the contractor's domain); observe conformance with the documents, not how the contractor achieves it.
- Skip the SOR on short visits or "punch-list walks" — those observations are among the most litigation-relevant.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Site visits in a design-build delivery where the architect of record is employed by or contracted to the contractor may have a different SOR distribution obligation under the contract; confirm the reporting chain before the first visit.
