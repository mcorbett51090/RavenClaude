# Brief every piece against a search intent.

**Status:** Absolute rule. **Constitution:** §3 #4, §3 #6, §4.

## Use when
Any marketing operations deliverable involving content creation, an editorial calendar, or SEO/AI-answer planning — read, applied, and cited whole.

## The rule
No piece ships without a brief that names the target search intent / job-to-be-done, the funnel stage, the differentiated angle, the cluster/pillar it belongs to, and the one CTA it earns. The brief — not the draft — is where content quality is won; a piece with no intent orphans and rarely earns or converts traffic.

## Why it matters
This is house opinion §3 #4 (outcome over activity) applied to content. Practitioners act on these deliverables, so a framing error here is not academic — it sends real operating decisions the wrong way. Volume of posts is activity; a piece briefed against a real intent and funnel stage is what produces measurable contribution.

## How to apply
- Apply this **before** drafting — it sets the framing, not the conclusion.
- Require every brief field; reject the slot if the intent is unclear.
- Set a leading success metric per piece, and name the attribution model before crediting it with pipeline (§3 #2).
- Cite a source + date for any external benchmark, or mark it `[unverified — training knowledge]` (§3 #8).
- When this rule and another both apply, route to [`marketing-ops-lead`](../agents/marketing-ops-lead.md) to sequence them.
- Keep customer/lead PII out of the deliverable; route professional/legal determinations to the qualified authority (§2).
- Close with a recommendation that has an owner, a date, and an expected metric movement.

## The anti-pattern this prevents
The §4 failure mode: producing posts by topic whim with no intent, no funnel stage, and no CTA — activity that looks like a content program but earns and converts little. The plugin's advisory hook flags a deliverable that reads as if this rule were ignored.
