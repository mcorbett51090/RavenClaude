# Retainage and cash flow can sink a profitable job

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Project finance and cash management
**Applies to:** `construction-general-contractor`

---

## Why this exists

A GC can win a profitable project on paper and run out of cash before it's over. Retainage
is the primary culprit: a 10% retainage on a $5M project ties up $500,000 throughout the
job. Add slow owner pay cycles (45–60 days from application to receipt), subcontractor
mobilization costs, and front-loaded material purchases, and the cash-flow requirement
can exceed the project's net profit margin. A job that earns 4% net profit on $5M ($200K)
can require $600K+ in cash to execute — three times the profit — before the retainage is
released. Know this before you sign the contract; manage it throughout.

## How to apply

- **At contract review:** model the cash flow before signing. Estimate monthly billings,
  retainage withheld per period, and projected release date. Compare the peak negative
  cash-flow month to your available credit.
- **At SOV setup:** structure the schedule of values to front-load cash where the contract
  allows (mobilization, general conditions, early trade completions). Do not inflate early
  line items dishonestly — but do load them to reflect when costs are actually incurred.
- **During the project:** track retainage separately from earned revenue. It is a receivable,
  not a rounding error.
- **At substantial completion:** pursue the retainage release immediately. Get the Certificate
  of Substantial Completion signed the day you achieve it. Every day of delay is a day of
  retainage withheld.

**Do:**

- Model project cash flow at contract award (monthly inflows, outflows, retainage withheld,
  projected release).
- Negotiate retainage reduction at 50% complete if the contract allows (many public contracts
  allow this; ask for it in private work).
- Track open approved COs as receivables — if it's approved and not yet billed, it's owed.
- Invoice every period, on time, with complete backup. A late pay app is a cash-flow self-
  inflicted wound.

**Don't:**

- Sign a contract with 10% retainage through final completion on a thin-margin job without
  modeling the cash-flow impact.
- Treat retainage as "bonus money at the end." It is your money in the owner's account.
- Allow a punch list to drag for months after substantial completion — every month of delay
  is a month of retainage withheld.
- Pay subcontractors before you've been paid if you have pay-when-paid language in your
  subcontracts (check state law — some states have pay-when-paid restrictions).

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

On GMP or cost-plus contracts with monthly reimbursement and low or no retainage, the cash-
flow risk is significantly reduced. Still model it: even a cost-plus contract has a GC fee
at risk, and slow owner payment cycles can delay reimbursement of out-of-pocket costs.
