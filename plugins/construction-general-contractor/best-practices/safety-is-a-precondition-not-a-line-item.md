# Safety is a precondition, not a line item

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Jobsite safety and project delivery
**Applies to:** `construction-general-contractor`

---

## Why this exists

When a GC treats safety as a discretionary cost — something to reduce when the bid is tight
or the job is over budget — it creates liability exposure, increases the EMR (Experience
Modification Rate) which raises insurance costs on every subsequent bid, and most importantly
puts workers at risk of injury or death. Fall protection, lockout/tagout, and confined-space
permits are not optional line items. They are legal requirements under 29 CFR 1926 and baseline
operating costs of running a construction business. A GC that "finds" safety savings in the
field is borrowing against future insurance premiums and workers' comp claims.

## How to apply

- Include a safety cost line in every bid: safety manager or safety officer time (for large
  jobs), safety equipment, fall protection systems, temporary barriers, fire extinguishers,
  first-aid supplies, safety orientation costs.
- Write a site-specific safety plan for every project before the first worker sets foot on site.
- Require every subcontractor to submit a safety plan and safety credentials before mobilizing.
- Hold a safety orientation for every new worker before they begin work.
- Conduct toolbox talks on a schedule (typically weekly or per-trade at scope start).

**Do:**

- Apply the hierarchy of controls: elimination → substitution → engineering controls →
  administrative controls → PPE. PPE is the last line of defense, not the first.
- Write a JHA for every high-hazard operation before work starts.
- Budget for safety as overhead. It does not come out of the profit margin.
- Treat near-miss reports as early warnings. Investigate and correct; do not suppress.

**Don't:**

- Value-engineer fall protection, confined-space permits, or lockout/tagout procedures.
- Allow work to start on a high-hazard scope without a completed JHA.
- Accept a subcontractor safety program that exists only on paper and is not enforced.
- Dismiss a near-miss as "nothing happened" without root-cause analysis.
- Include safety costs as a discretionary contingency that can be cut if the budget is tight.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

There are no edge cases that justify eliminating safety controls for hazardous construction
operations. Administrative burden can be right-sized for scope: a small reroofing project
does not need the same 100-page safety program as a hospital construction, but it still
needs fall protection, a JHA, and a site safety contact. The depth of the program scales
with the project; the principle does not.

## Provenance

OSHA 29 CFR 1926 is the legal framework. The CPWR (Center for Construction Research and
Training) and ASSP (American Society of Safety Professionals) provide industry best-practice
guidance. The hierarchy of controls is codified in NIOSH and OSHA guidance documents.
