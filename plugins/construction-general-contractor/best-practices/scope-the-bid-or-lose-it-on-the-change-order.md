# Scope the bid or lose it on the change order

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Estimating and project delivery
**Applies to:** `construction-general-contractor`

---

## Why this exists

Every dollar of scope ambiguity in a bid becomes a negotiation at change-order time —
and you negotiate with less leverage after the contract is signed than before. A scope gap
in the bid is a gift to the owner: the GC does the work, the owner argues it was in the
original contract, and the GC eats the cost. The time to resolve ambiguity is before the bid
is submitted, in the form of exclusions, qualifications, and RFIs during the bid period.

## How to apply

- Read the full bid package before pricing: drawings, specifications, addenda, bid form,
  and contract form. Not the scope summary — the full set.
- Write the exclusion list before you start the takeoff. It will grow as you read.
- For every ambiguity in the drawings or specs, either: (a) price your interpretation and
  state it as a qualification in the bid letter, or (b) submit a clarification RFI during
  the bid period.
- Every inclusion and exclusion in the bid letter is a future change-order foundation.
  If it's not in the bid letter, it's in the contract.

**Do:**

- Write explicit exclusions for everything you did not price (FF&E, permitting, testing,
  special inspections, hazmat, below-grade waterproofing, owner-furnished equipment).
- Note your schedule basis and any clarifications that affect sequence or phasing.
- Acknowledge all addenda by number and date.
- Flag scope overlaps between trades (e.g., "electrical rough-in by electrician; GC not
  responsible for coordination with MEP BIM model").

**Don't:**

- Submit a bid with no exclusion section.
- Assume the owner/architect will remember what you discussed verbally during the bid period.
- Price scope you're not sure is included "to be safe" without noting the assumption.
- Let a vague spec section slip by without a qualification.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Design-build or fast-track projects sometimes require a bid before complete drawings are
available. In this case: document the drawing completeness level in the bid letter, price
an allowance for incomplete scope with a named design-completion contingency, and build
a contract mechanism (target value design or GMP with defined scope) that allows scope
definition to be formalized post-award.

## Provenance

Standard GC industry practice; codifies the "bid it right or live with it" principle that
experienced estimators learn through loss. The bid letter as contract document is supported
by AIA A201 General Conditions and most standard contract forms.
