# Submittals gate procurement — sequence them early

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Submittal management and project scheduling
**Applies to:** `construction-general-contractor`

---

## Why this exists

A submittal approval is typically required before a piece of equipment or custom material can
be fabricated or ordered. The fabrication and delivery cycle for MEP equipment (air handling
units, switchgear, elevators, custom plumbing fixtures) runs 12–20 weeks. If the submittal
register is set up late, or submittals are transmitted in a bulk batch at project start that
overwhelms the architect/engineer's review queue, the approvals come late, procurement is
delayed, delivery is delayed, and installation is pushed — directly impacting the schedule.
This is one of the most common and most preventable causes of project delays in commercial
construction.

## How to apply

- Set up the submittal register within the first two weeks of project start.
- Identify required submittals from every spec section's Part 1 "Submittals" paragraph.
- For each submittal, calculate the required-by date: need-on-site minus delivery lead time
  minus fabrication time minus A/E review period (per contract, typically 14–21 business days)
  minus GC review (5 days) = submit-by date. Many MEP submittals need to go out 16–20 weeks
  before the planned installation date.
- Sequence submittals by procurement lead time — long-lead equipment first, commodity items last.
  Do not transmit all submittals in a single batch; the reviewer cannot process them.
- Integrate submittal chains into the CPM schedule. A submittal that gates a critical-path
  procurement is itself on the critical path.

**Do:**

- Identify long-lead items in the first project meeting and start the submittal process
  immediately (the sub should be preparing shop drawings even before the design is fully
  finalized, using preliminary drawings and flagging any pending design coordination).
- Monitor submittal review times against the contract period. If a reviewer is slow, send a
  formal written follow-up before the deadline passes.
- Route approved submittals to the subcontractor and the field within 24 hours of receipt.
- Track Revise-and-Resubmit actions immediately — a second review cycle adds 2–3 weeks to
  the procurement chain.

**Don't:**

- Set up the submittal register after the first pay application. Week 6 is too late for
  equipment with 16-week lead times.
- Transmit all submittals on Day 1 of the project. Sequence them by urgency.
- Allow a "Revise and Resubmit" to sit on the subcontractor's desk for two weeks.
- Accept a sub's claim that "we don't need to submit that" without verifying it against the
  spec section.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

For projects where the owner provides owner-furnished equipment (OFE) with shop drawings
already approved, the GC's submittal obligation for that equipment is reduced to coordination
drawings and installation procedures. Still track OFE delivery schedules in the CPM — they
carry the same procurement-delay risk even without the submittal review step.
