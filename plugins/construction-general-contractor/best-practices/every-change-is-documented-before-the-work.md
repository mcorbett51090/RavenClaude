# Every change is documented before the work starts

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Change-order management and contract administration
**Applies to:** `construction-general-contractor`

---

## Why this exists

An oral direction from an owner, architect, or owner's representative is not a change order.
It is a favor. A GC that performs out-of-scope work on a verbal direction and then tries to
collect on a change order faces the owner saying "that was within the original scope" or "I
didn't authorize that." The GC has done the work, spent the money, and has no written record
to stand behind. The only defense against this outcome is a written directive — obtained before
the work starts.

## How to apply

- When an owner or architect gives a verbal direction to perform out-of-scope work, immediately
  confirm it in writing: send an email restating what was directed, the scope, and that you
  will proceed only upon written confirmation.
- Issue a Proposed Change Order (PCO) or Request for Change before mobilizing crews to new scope.
- If the owner insists on starting work before paperwork is complete, document the verbal
  direction in writing (email, meeting minutes, daily report) and note that the GC is proceeding
  under protest pending a written change order.
- Log every PCO in the change-order log as soon as it is identified, even before pricing is
  complete.

**Do:**

- Treat every owner-directed scope addition as a potential CO until proven otherwise.
- Issue notice within the contract's required notice period (typically 3–21 days; check the
  contract). Late notice can waive the right to a CO even for valid compensable work.
- Price time impact separately from direct cost.
- Require a signed CO (or at minimum a signed PCO acknowledging the scope and the GC's right
  to price it) before mobilizing.

**Don't:**

- Start out-of-scope work on a verbal direction with no written record.
- Assume "we'll sort out the paperwork later" will result in a paid change order.
- Bundle multiple CO scopes into one undifferentiated package — price each change separately
  so the owner can evaluate and approve individually.
- Sign a "no-cost CO" for scope that has a real time impact without documenting the time
  impact explicitly (even if the CO is $0).

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Emergency conditions (life safety, imminent property damage) may require immediate action
without a written CO in hand. In this case: perform the emergency work, document it with
photos and daily reports in real time, issue a PCO immediately after the emergency is controlled,
and note in the PCO that the GC acted in good faith under emergency conditions. The emergency
exception is narrow and should never be used for routine scope additions.

## Provenance

Standard construction contract administration practice; supported by AIA A201 General
Conditions (Article 7 — Changes in the Work) and the Spearin doctrine. AACE International
cost-engineering best practices (RP 59R-10) address change-order documentation discipline.
