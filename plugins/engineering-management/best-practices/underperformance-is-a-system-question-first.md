# Underperformance is a system question first.

**Status:** Pattern. **Constitution:** §3 #5, §4.

## Use when
Diagnosing a struggling engineer. Read, applied, and cited whole.

## The rule
Before concluding 'it's the person,' check the system: unclear expectations, missing context, wrong-altitude work, a blocked dependency, or a health issue. Most 'performance problems' are expectation/clarity problems wearing a costume — this is the order of checks, not a denial that genuine misfit exists.

## Why it matters
This is house opinion §3 #5, distilled into a citable rule. Managers act on these deliverables and they concern real people and real systems, so a framing error here is not academic — it sends real decisions, careers, and codebases the wrong way. The rule is cheap to apply and expensive to skip.

## How to apply
- Apply this **before** reaching for a method — it sets the framing, not the conclusion.
- The system (expectations/context/altitude/blockers/health) is checked and ruled out before any individual conclusion; genuine gaps still route fairly (§3 #5, §2).
- A claim about a person is a hypothesis to test, never a verdict; management deliverables about a person are drafts for a human to own (§1, §2).
- Cite a source + date for any external benchmark, or mark it `[unverified — training knowledge]` (§3 #8).
- When this rule and another both apply, route to [`people-and-growth-manager`](../agents/people-and-growth-manager.md) to sequence them — overlapping signals usually mean two drivers at once.
- Keep sensitive personnel PII (health, protected-class, comp) out of the deliverable; route HR/legal/comp determinations to the qualified authority (§2).
- Close with a recommendation that has an owner, a date, and an expected change.

## The anti-pattern this prevents
The §4 failure mode: acting as if "underperformance is a system question first." weren't true — one of the most common ways a management decision quietly goes wrong for the person or team it affects. The plugin's advisory hook flags a deliverable that reads as if this rule were ignored.
