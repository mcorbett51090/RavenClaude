# Performance signal is specific, dated, and behavioral — or it's bias.

**Status:** Absolute rule. **Constitution:** §3 #4, §4.

## Use when
Writing a review, calibration input, or promo packet. Read, applied, and cited whole.

## The rule
"Great attitude" and "not a culture fit" are where bias hides. A defensible review cites observable behavior with dates and impact, gathered continuously, not reconstructed the week reviews are due.

## Why it matters
This is house opinion §3 #4, distilled into a citable rule. Managers act on these deliverables and they concern real people and real systems, so a framing error here is not academic — it sends real decisions, careers, and codebases the wrong way. The rule is cheap to apply and expensive to skip.

## How to apply
- Apply this **before** reaching for a method — it sets the framing, not the conclusion.
- Every vague adjective is replaced by the observable behavior, date, and impact behind it; evidence is continuous, not recency-reconstructed (§3 #4).
- A claim about a person is a hypothesis to test, never a verdict; management deliverables about a person are drafts for a human to own (§1, §2).
- Cite a source + date for any external benchmark, or mark it `[unverified — training knowledge]` (§3 #8).
- When this rule and another both apply, route to [`people-and-growth-manager`](../agents/people-and-growth-manager.md) to sequence them — overlapping signals usually mean two drivers at once.
- Keep sensitive personnel PII (health, protected-class, comp) out of the deliverable; route HR/legal/comp determinations to the qualified authority (§2).
- Close with a recommendation that has an owner, a date, and an expected change.

## The anti-pattern this prevents
The §4 failure mode: acting as if "performance signal is specific, dated, and behavioral — or it's bias." weren't true — one of the most common ways a management decision quietly goes wrong for the person or team it affects. The plugin's advisory hook flags a deliverable that reads as if this rule were ignored.
