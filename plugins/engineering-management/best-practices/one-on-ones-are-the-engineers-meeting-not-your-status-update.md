# 1:1s are the engineer's meeting, not your status update.

**Status:** Pattern. **Constitution:** §3 #2, §4.

## Use when
Designing or fixing a 1:1 cadence. Read, applied, and cited whole.

## The rule
A 1:1 spent reading status is a standup you double-booked. The durable value is growth, blockers, and trust — pull the agenda from them, and protect the time. Cancelling 1:1s is the most expensive cheap decision a manager makes.

## Why it matters
This is house opinion §3 #2, distilled into a citable rule. Managers act on these deliverables and they concern real people and real systems, so a framing error here is not academic — it sends real decisions, careers, and codebases the wrong way. The rule is cheap to apply and expensive to skip.

## How to apply
- Apply this **before** reaching for a method — it sets the framing, not the conclusion.
- The agenda defaults to the engineer's topics; the time is protected (rescheduled, never skipped); notes are dated and behavioral (§3 #2 #4).
- A claim about a person is a hypothesis to test, never a verdict; management deliverables about a person are drafts for a human to own (§1, §2).
- Cite a source + date for any external benchmark, or mark it `[unverified — training knowledge]` (§3 #8).
- When this rule and another both apply, route to [`people-and-growth-manager`](../agents/people-and-growth-manager.md) to sequence them — overlapping signals usually mean two drivers at once.
- Keep sensitive personnel PII (health, protected-class, comp) out of the deliverable; route HR/legal/comp determinations to the qualified authority (§2).
- Close with a recommendation that has an owner, a date, and an expected change.

## The anti-pattern this prevents
The §4 failure mode: acting as if "1:1s are the engineer's meeting, not your status update." weren't true — one of the most common ways a management decision quietly goes wrong for the person or team it affects. The plugin's advisory hook flags a deliverable that reads as if this rule were ignored.
