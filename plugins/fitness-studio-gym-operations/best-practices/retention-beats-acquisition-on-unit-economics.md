# Retention beats acquisition on unit economics

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Membership P&L
**Applies to:** `fitness-studio-gym-operations`

> Advisory operations rule. Churn/LTV benchmarks are `[ESTIMATE]` / `[verify-at-use]`. No member PII.

---

## Why this exists

A gym is a subscription business, and in a subscription business the cheapest revenue is the member you already have. Member LTV = ARPU / churn, so **halving churn does more for the P&L than doubling ad spend** — and a saved member also refers and buys ancillary, which an acquired stranger has not yet done. Pouring acquisition into a base that churns above benchmark is filling a leaky bucket: you pay CAC twice for the same seat.

## How to apply

- Read **net member change** (joins − churn) and the **retention curve** before you read the join count.
- If churn is above your benchmark, **fix churn before scaling acquisition** — the leaky-bucket diagnosis.
- Compute member LTV and hold LTV:CAC to target; if the ratio is under target, acquisition is unprofitable no matter how full the funnel looks.
- Date every churn and ARPU input (`[verify-at-use]`) against your own books.

**Do:** treat a saved member as cheaper and worth more than an acquired one.
**Don't:** celebrate gross joins while net movement is flat or negative.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

A brand-new studio with almost no base to retain must first acquire to critical mass — but the moment there's a base, retention re-takes primacy. A deliberately transactional class-pack concept optimizes differently (no recurring anchor to retain).
