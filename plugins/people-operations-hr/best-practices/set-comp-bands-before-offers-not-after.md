# Set comp bands before offers, not after

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Compensation / total rewards
**Applies to:** `people-operations-hr`

---

## Why this exists

When a comp band doesn't exist before the offer stage, one of two things happens:

1. The offer is anchored to the candidate's stated compensation history (illegal in many US
   states and most of the EU; and predictively perpetuates historical pay inequity).
2. The offer is improvised to close the candidate — and becomes the de facto comp benchmark
   for every subsequent hire into that role.

Both paths systematically disadvantage candidates whose prior comp was suppressed (women,
underrepresented minorities, career-changers, candidates from lower-cost markets) and create
internal equity problems that become visible — and expensive — at the next pay equity analysis.

A comp band anchored to a documented market percentile before any candidate is in play removes
the anchoring problem entirely: the offer range is set by the market, not by whoever walked
in the door first.

## How to apply

**The sequence that prevents anchoring:**

1. Design the role → assign it to a level → look up the band for that level.
2. Check the band is current (within 12 months; re-survey or benchmark if stale).
3. Screen candidates → identify finalists → make the offer from within the band.

The band exists before you know who the finalist is. The offer is a placement within the band
based on the candidate's level-relevant experience, not on their prior salary.

**Do:**

- Build or verify the comp band for a role before the job is posted.
- Document the band and its market basis (source, percentile, date surveyed).
- Train recruiters and hiring managers: "What were you making at your last job?" is
  off-limits in jurisdictions where salary history questions are banned, and unwise everywhere.
- Anchor the offer conversation with "our band for this level is $X–$Y" — the candidate
  knows the range; the negotiation happens within it.

**Don't:**

- Extend an offer without a documented band in place — even with the best intentions,
  the comp decision will be anchored on something other than market.
- Set a band "after we see who's interested" — by then, you've already been told several
  candidates' expectations, and the band will be rationalized around those data points.
- Communicate a comp band only to recruiters and hide it from candidates in markets that
  require disclosure — pay-transparency laws are expanding, and proactive disclosure builds trust.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

For genuinely novel roles (a role the company has never hired for, in a market where no
comp survey data exists), the band may require a preliminary market scan before it can be set.
That scan still happens before the offer — not as a response to a specific candidate's ask.
"We'll figure out comp once we find the right person" is never the right answer.
