# Performance is continuous feedback, not an annual event

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Performance management
**Applies to:** `people-operations-hr`

---

## Why this exists

A once-a-year performance review that introduces new information to employees has already failed.
By the point the "mid-year" or "annual" form is due:

- The employee has spent months operating without clear feedback — either continuing a behavior
  that should have changed, or under-investing in an area that was actually valued.
- The manager is writing from memory, anchored to the last 6–8 weeks (recency bias is
  structurally guaranteed in annual reviews without continuous documentation).
- The feedback is received as a verdict, not as actionable information — because the employee
  has no opportunity to respond in-cycle.

Annual reviews are, at best, a formal summary of a year of documented continuous feedback. At
worst, they are a once-a-year judgment delivered cold — which employees rightly experience as
unfair, and which provides no improvement mechanism until the next calendar year.

## How to apply

**The continuous feedback system has three components:**

**1. Regular 1:1s with a shared running document**
Weekly or bi-weekly 1:1s between manager and direct report, with a shared note documenting:
what was discussed, what was decided, what was agreed. The running note is the performance
record. By review time, the manager is not reconstructing the year from memory; they are
summarizing a documented record.

**2. Quarterly structured check-ins**
A more formal version of the 1:1 cadence with a fixed template:
- What's going well? (evidence)
- What needs to change? (specific, actionable)
- Development focus for the next quarter?
- Any blockers the manager owns?

The quarterly check-in is documented and stored in the HRIS (or equivalent). The annual review
is then a synthesis of four quarterly check-ins, not a reconstruction.

**3. Real-time issue flagging**
Performance problems are raised at the moment they occur or become visible — not saved for
the review. A manager who waits until the annual review to mention a performance problem:
- Has deprived the employee of the opportunity to improve.
- Has (in many jurisdictions) weakened the legal defensibility of any adverse action.
- Has almost certainly allowed the problem to worsen.

The rule: an annual review is never the first time an employee hears about a performance concern.

**Do:**

- Build the running 1:1 document before you design the review form.
- Make quarterly check-ins a calendar event, not an optional prompt.
- Document real-time: a 2-minute Slack message to the 1:1 doc beats a memory-reconstructed
  paragraph six months later.
- Design the review form to pull from documented check-ins — "cite a specific example from
  this quarter" removes the blank-page problem.

**Don't:**

- Build a performance system that assumes feedback exists only when the review form is open.
- Wait until the mid-year or annual review to tell someone their performance is below
  expectations.
- Design check-ins as box-checking exercises that get rescheduled — if they're postponed more
  than twice, they don't exist.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

For executives (CEO, C-suite), the 1:1 cadence with the board or lead investor replaces the
manager 1:1. The principle — continuous, documented feedback — applies equally; the mechanism
adapts.

## Provenance

Based on the continuous performance management literature (Kim Scott's "Radical Candor", Marcus
Buckingham & Ashley Goodall's "Nine Lies About Work"), the failure modes of annual-only review
systems as documented by SHRM and the Deloitte Human Capital research, and legal best-practice
for defensible adverse employment actions (continuous documentation is a necessary precondition
for a defensible PIP or termination in most employment relationships).
