# Structured interviews beat gut feel

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Talent acquisition / hiring process design
**Applies to:** `people-operations-hr`

---

## Why this exists

Meta-analyses of predictive validity in hiring (Schmidt & Hunter, 1998; updated Sackett et al.,
2022) consistently show that structured, competency-based interviews with pre-defined rubrics
have meaningfully higher predictive validity than unstructured conversations — and are
substantially less susceptible to affinity bias, halo effects, and in-group favoritism.
Unstructured interviews feel more natural but select primarily for social similarity to the
interviewer, not for ability to perform the job.

The practical consequence: companies that rely on unstructured "culture fit" conversations
reproduce the demographics and thinking styles of whoever is doing the interviewing — slowly
building a monoculture while believing they are evaluating "talent."

## How to apply

Before any interview loop is run, the following must exist:

- **A defined competency set** (4–7 competencies per role, marked must-have vs nice-to-have).
- **A scorecard per stage** with a rubric (behavioral anchors at each score point).
- **Structured question sets** — every interviewer goes in with questions written in advance,
  not improvised.
- **A structured debrief** — scorecards submitted independently before the debrief; reverse-
  seniority discussion order; evidence cited, not impressions.

**Do:**

- Design the competency set and scorecard before the JD is posted — know what you're hiring for
  before you start talking to candidates.
- Train interviewers before they go in; brief every interviewer on exactly which competency they
  own.
- Collect scorecards before the debrief starts; do not allow pre-debrief discussion.
- Make hire recommendations binary (hire / no-hire); "maybe" is a no-hire unless additional
  information is gathered.

**Don't:**

- Run a "let's get to know them" screen with no scorecard and no defined competency.
- Allow interviewers to cover the same ground (background story, career narrative) in every
  stage while no one covers a technical or domain competency.
- Weight "vibes" or "energy" as a tiebreaker when scores are split — go back to the evidence.
- Use "culture fit" as a standalone criterion; define what behaviors the "culture" actually
  requires and score those specifically.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

For very senior executive roles (CEO, C-suite), the structure of the process adapts but the
principle does not: the competencies and evidence-gathering remain structured even if the
conversations are less formulaic. "We always hire executives differently" is often a rationalization
for "we evaluate executives on social proof rather than job-relevant evidence."

## Provenance

Codifies Schmidt & Hunter (1998) "The Validity and Utility of Selection Methods in Personnel
Psychology" (Psychological Bulletin, 124(2), 262–274) and the Sackett et al. (2022) reanalysis
(Journal of Applied Psychology). The conclusion — structured interviews with behavioral anchors
outperform unstructured ones — is one of the most replicated findings in industrial-organizational
psychology.
