# Calibrate to fight rater bias

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Performance management / calibration
**Applies to:** `people-operations-hr`

---

## Why this exists

Without cross-manager calibration, performance ratings reflect three things in roughly equal
measure: actual performance, the manager's leniency or severity bias, and the team a person
happened to be on. Managers who are generous raters produce a team of "exceeds" regardless of
actual performance distribution. Managers who are strict produce a team of "meets" or "below"
regardless. The result:

- Merit increases are detached from performance — the 3% everyone gets reflects who lobbied
  hardest in the room, not who performed best.
- High performers on strict-manager teams lose trust in the system and leave.
- Promotions cluster around the most vocal managers' teams, not the highest-performing employees.

Calibration is the mechanism that makes ratings mean the same thing across managers — and
without it, every rating-adjacent decision (merit, promotion, PIP, reduction decisions) is
running on corrupted data.

## How to apply

**The three calibration disciplines:**

**1. Pre-calibrate (before ratings are communicated)**
The calibration session happens before any manager has delivered ratings to employees. Post-
communication "calibration" is rationalization theater — nobody will revise a rating they've
already told an employee.

**2. Evidence-first, reverse seniority**
- Every manager submits a written evidence summary per direct report before the session.
- In the session, junior managers speak first. The most senior person in the room speaks last.
  This is not a courtesy — it prevents the highest-authority voice from anchoring the group
  on the first impression.
- Ratings are challenged with evidence, not overridden by authority.

**3. Name the biases and prompt for them actively**
The calibration facilitator explicitly prompts the group to check for:
- **Recency bias:** "Is this based on the full year, or primarily the last quarter?"
- **Halo/horn effect:** "Are we rating the project, or the person on the project?"
- **Manager advocacy vs evidence:** "What specific evidence supports a 'Far Exceeds' here?"
- **Team-context blindness:** "How does this performance compare to what we'd expect of an IC4
  on a harder team?"

**Do:**

- Hold calibration before any rating conversations with employees.
- Require written evidence summaries from every manager as pre-work.
- Run sessions with a neutral facilitator (HR or a trained People BP).
- Document recalibrations: who changed, from what to what, why.

**Don't:**

- Run a calibration session where the VP speaks first and everyone converges in 20 minutes.
- Calibrate ratings after they've been communicated — you're doing public relations, not
  calibration.
- Accept "my team is just exceptional" from a manager whose entire team is rated "Exceeds"
  with no evidence review.
- Skip calibration for "small teams" — the biases are the same regardless of team size.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

For very small companies (<25 employees) where only one manager exists, formal cross-manager
calibration isn't possible. The compensating control is: ratings are reviewed by the CEO or CPO
against the leveling rubric, with evidence — the same evidence-first discipline, applied
without a committee.

## Provenance

Based on the industrial-organizational psychology literature on rater bias (leniency error,
halo effect, central tendency bias) and the practice of structured calibration as described in
the SHRM and WorldatWork total-rewards literature. Google's Project Oxygen and Project Aristotle
research supports the principle that manager quality variance is the dominant within-org
predictor of team-level performance.
