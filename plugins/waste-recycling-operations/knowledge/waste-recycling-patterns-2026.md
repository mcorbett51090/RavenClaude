## Route density — the profit driver (the core math)

Collection is a fixed-cost-per-truck-hour business; profit is won by **stops per hour**, not by a faster truck.

| Lever | What it is | Move it by |
|---|---|---|
| **Route density** | Stops (or tons) served per route-mile | Tighter service-area geography; sequencing; balanced routes |
| **Stops per hour** | Productive service rate | Cart-standardization + ASL automation; reducing dwell time |
| **Windshield time** | Unproductive drive time (to route, between clusters, to disposal) | Transfer stations to cut disposal round-trips; better sequencing; depot siting |
| **Cost per stop** | The KPI everything rolls up to | Raising density + stops/hour and cutting windshield time |

A route that serves 140 dense stops/hour at low windshield time beats a faster truck on a sparse route every time. **Balance** matters too: an unbalanced set of routes (one overloaded, one light) wastes capacity — re-balance static routes as the territory grows.

## Collection streams & the fleet that serves them

| Stream | Typical generator | Truck |
|---|---|---|
| **MSW (trash)** | Residential subscription / commercial | Side-load ASL (residential carts) · front-load (commercial) |
| **Single-stream recycling** | Residential / commercial | Side-load ASL · front-load; routed to a **MRF** |
| **Organics / food waste** | Residential (SB 1383 areas) / commercial / food service | Side-load / rear-load; routed to compost / AD / RNG |
| **C&D (construction & demolition)** | Job sites, temporary | **Roll-off** (10–40yd boxes) |

**Fleet types:** front-load (commercial dumpsters), rear-load (manual/alley residential), **side-load / automated (ASL)** (cart-based residential — the labor + safety win of a one-person crew), roll-off (C&D/temporary). **Alt-fuel:** **RNG** (renewable natural gas, often landfill/AD-sourced — a fit for the industry's own gas) and **EV** refuse trucks are growing; the call turns on duty cycle, route profile, fueling/charging infrastructure, and incentives. **Maintenance** is a first-class cost — refuse trucks run brutal duty cycles (constant stop-start, hydraulics, heavy loads); a disciplined PM cadence keeps the fleet available and safe.

## Disposal & landfill economics

- **Tipping fee** ($/ton) — what you pay at the landfill or transfer-station gate. It's the anchor of disposal cost and the avoided cost every diverted ton saves. _(Volatile — varies widely by market; retrieve a current figure.)_
- **Landfill airspace** — the finite, depleting (and appreciating) capacity of the cell. Every buried ton spends it; airspace value rises as it fills. A disposal plan that treats the cell as infinite is short-sighted.
- **Subtitle D landfill obligations** — RCRA Subtitle D governs non-hazardous solid-waste landfills: liners, **leachate** collection/treatment, **landfill gas** (methane) capture (often to RNG or flare), groundwater monitoring, and daily cover. These are operating obligations, not optional.
- **Transfer station** — consolidates collection-truck loads into high-capacity trailers for long-haul to a distant landfill/MRF. It pays where haul distance/volume make the extra handling cheaper than sending collection trucks the full distance (windshield-time + fuel savings).
- **Scale / weigh tickets** — every load is weighed in/out; the weigh ticket is the source of truth for tipping-fee billing, tonnage pricing, and **diversion measurement**. No estimated tonnage where a scale reading exists.

## MRF & recycling commodity economics (post-National Sword)

A **Materials Recovery Facility (MRF)** sorts single-stream recycling into baled commodities sold to end-markets. The program's P&L rides two things: **commodity prices** and **contamination**.

| Commodity grade | Role in the P&L | Note |
|---|---|---|
| **OCC / cardboard** | High-volume workhorse; usually positive value | E-commerce keeps supply high; price swings with mill demand |
| **Aluminum (UBC)** | The **value anchor** — highest $/ton | A small mass share funds a large share of revenue |
| **PET (#1) / HDPE (#2)** | Saleable plastics with real end-markets | Value depends on resin markets + bale cleanliness |
| **Mixed paper** | The **drag** — weak, low-value grade | Hit hardest by China's **National Sword** (2018) import ban; domestic mills now the outlet |

**China's National Sword** (2018) banned most imported mixed paper/plastics and set a ~0.5% contamination threshold, collapsing the offshore outlet for low-grade material. The durable consequence: **domestic MRF quality and end-markets are the reality** — you can no longer bale dirty low-grade material and ship it offshore. Design and price for clean, domestically-marketable bales. _(Bale/commodity prices are **volatile** — retrieve current figures before quoting.)_

## Contamination — the killer of recycling economics

Contamination (non-recyclables, food, tanglers, wrong plastics) is the single highest-leverage recycling variable:

- It **lowers bale value** (buyers pay for clean grades and dock or reject dirty ones).
- It **raises MRF residual/reject cost** (contaminants are pulled and landfilled — you pay a tipping fee to dispose of what you collected as "recycling").
- Above a threshold it **flips a load from revenue to expense** — a contaminated single-stream load can cost more to process+dispose than it earns.

**Reduce it at the source:** cart tagging / "oops" stickers, route audits, targeted customer education, and clear accepted-materials lists. Measure it as the MRF's **residual/reject rate**. Contamination reduction usually beats chasing a marginal bale-price uptick.

## Diversion rate — measured, not estimated

**Diversion rate = diverted tonnage ÷ total generated tonnage**, computed from **weigh tickets** — never estimated capture. What drags it down: low set-out/capture rates, organics still landfilled (until a mandate forces them out), and contamination rejected back to landfill at the MRF. Raising it means capturing more recyclable/organic tonnage *and* keeping it clean enough to actually be diverted (a contaminated ton "collected for recycling" but rejected at the MRF was never diverted).

## The mandate landscape (constraints, not aspirations — dated, volatile)

- **EPR (Extended Producer Responsibility)** — shifts packaging end-of-life cost/management onto producers; **expanding by state** (packaging EPR laws passed in several US states, following long-standing programs elsewhere). Changes who funds recycling and the reporting obligations. _(Statute coverage varies by state and changes — retrieve current status.)_
- **Organics-diversion mandates** — California **SB 1383** requires organic-waste diversion (with methane-reduction and edible-food-recovery targets); other jurisdictions have parallel mandates. Makes organics collection compulsory, not optional. _(Retrieve current thresholds/effective dates.)_
- **Landfill bans** — jurisdiction-specific bans on landfilling certain streams (yard waste, organics, cardboard, e-waste) force diversion regardless of commodity economics.

The rule: **run the diversion economics, then let the mandate override** — where a stream is compulsorily diverted, "landfill it" isn't a legal option regardless of price. _(All three carry a retrieval date — re-verify with `ravenclaude-core/deep-researcher` before a client commitment.)_

## The safety reality

Refuse/recyclable-material collection is consistently among the **highest-injury and highest-fatality occupations**. The hazards are specific and designable-out:

- **Hopper / blade** — packing-mechanism entanglement/amputation; lockout and hopper-safety protocols.
- **Backing incidents** — a leading cause of struck-by; backing cameras, spotters, and route design that minimizes reversing.
- **Repetitive lifting** — back/musculoskeletal injury on manual (rear-load) routes; ASL automation and load limits reduce it.
- **Noise** — hearing loss from trucks/compaction; hearing conservation.
- **DOT / CDL** — drivers are CDL-regulated; hours-of-service, vehicle inspection, and driver qualification apply.

Safety is the operating model, not a poster — an ASL conversion is as much a safety decision (fewer manual lifts, one person in the cab) as a labor one.
