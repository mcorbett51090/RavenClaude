# Fee Schedule Review Is Annual, Not When Costs Force It

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Veterinary practice / pricing management
**Applies to:** `veterinary-practice`

---

## Why this exists

Most veterinary practices raise fees reactively — when drug costs spike, when the practice management consultant arrives, or when the owner finally looks at the P&L and notices margin has eroded. By then, the practice has typically absorbed 12–36 months of cost increases without corresponding revenue recovery. The economics are asymmetric: a practice that raises fees 3% annually keeps pace with inflation and maintains client relationships smoothly; a practice that avoids increases for 3 years and then raises 9–12% in one cycle creates sticker shock, loses client trust, and still may not fully recover the lost margin. A disciplined annual review — built into the calendar, not triggered by crisis — is cheaper in client goodwill and more effective in financial recovery than irregular large increases.

## How to apply

Run the fee schedule review as a structured annual process:

```
Fee Schedule Review Worksheet — [Practice] [Year]
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
Review date: ___   Prior review date: ___   Months since last review: ___

Step 1 — Cost basis check
  Practice overhead rate (% of revenue): ___% (prior year: ___)
  Drug / supply cost as % of revenue:    ___% (prior year: ___)
  Labor cost as % of revenue:            ___% (prior year: ___)
  Total direct cost change (YoY):        +/-___% 
  CPI / veterinary CPI change (YoY):     ___% (source + date: ___)

Step 2 — Fee benchmarking (by service category)
  Source: [AVMA fee survey / AAHA benchmarking report — year/date]
  OVH (spay, feline):       Our fee: $___  Market 25th pctile: $___  Market median: $___
  OVH (spay, canine 20 lb): Our fee: $___  Market 25th pctile: $___  Market median: $___
  Annual wellness (dog):    Our fee: $___  Market 25th pctile: $___  Market median: $___
  [Add most-utilized 10 services from PIMS]

Step 3 — Gap analysis
  Services priced below our cost-of-service:            [List]
  Services priced below 25th pctile for market:         [List]
  Services not increased in > 24 months:                [List]
  High-volume, low-margin services (drag items):        [List]

Step 4 — Increase recommendations
  Target overall fee increase:  ___% (guideline: CPI + 1–2% if at or above median)
  Priority increases (below cost or >24 months unchanged):
    Service: ___  Current: $___  Proposed: $___  % change: ___
    Service: ___  Current: $___  Proposed: $___  % change: ___
  Deferred (client-sensitive; increase next cycle):     [List with rationale]

Step 5 — Revenue impact estimate
  Total annual encounters for services being increased:   ___
  Weighted average increase ($):                          $___
  Estimated annual revenue lift:                         $___
```

**Do:**
- Anchor every fee increase to a cost-of-service basis and a market benchmark — "we haven't raised it in two years" is not the explanation for a client; "our costs have risen X% and this service is priced below what it costs to deliver well" is.
- Sequence increases: raise the highest-volume, below-cost services first; defer discretionary / elective services where client price sensitivity is high.
- Communicate a fee increase proactively with key clients (long-standing, high-value) before they see it on an invoice — a letter or email explaining the reason converts a surprise into a respect-earning transparency moment.
- Load the new fee schedule into the PIMS on the same date every year — a practice with a defined "fee review date" trains the team and creates accountability.

**Don't:**
- Raise fees on emergency/critical care services disproportionately — the optics of a high emergency surcharge are a reputational risk; the routine-care fee schedule is the right place to recover margin.
- Benchmark only against the cheapest practices in the market — pricing to the bottom of the market signals low quality to clients who are choosing based on perceived value.
- Skip the review because "clients will push back" — clients who leave because of a 3% annual increase were not loyal to the practice; clients who stay and trust the team accept reasonable, explained increases.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Practices operating under corporate group agreements or franchise pricing schedules may not have discretionary fee-setting authority; the review still applies but the escalation path is through the group's pricing committee. Practices in highly price-sensitive rural markets with thin competitive alternatives may need to review more frequently than annually if input costs (especially drugs) are volatile, with smaller, more frequent adjustments.
