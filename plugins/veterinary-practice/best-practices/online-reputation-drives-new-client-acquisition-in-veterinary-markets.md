# Online Reputation Drives New-Client Acquisition in Veterinary Markets

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Practice growth / market position
**Applies to:** `veterinary-practice`

---

## Why this exists

New pet owners selecting a veterinary practice in 2025–2026 rely primarily on online reviews — Google, Yelp, and social media — to make their choice. [unverified — training knowledge] A practice with fewer than 50 Google reviews or a rating below 4.5 stars is at a structural disadvantage against competitors with 200+ reviews and a 4.8+ rating, regardless of actual clinical quality. Unlike price or geography, online reputation is both highly visible to the decision-maker and largely within the practice's influence. Practices that do not actively manage their online reputation are handing new-client acquisition to competitors who do.

## How to apply

Build online reputation management into the standard operations workflow, not a periodic project.

```
Online reputation operations:
1. Review solicitation (at discharge):
   - Train front desk and technicians: after every positive client interaction,
     prompt: "We'd really appreciate if you left us a Google review — it helps
     other pet owners find us."
   - Automate: configure client communication platform to send a review request 
     24–48 hours after discharge (triggered by visit close in PIMS)
   - Target: ≥5 new verified Google reviews per month per DVM [unverified — training knowledge]

2. Negative review response protocol:
   - Respond to every negative review within 48 hours — publicly, professionally
   - Formula: acknowledge the concern, thank for feedback, invite offline conversation
   - Never argue or share client PHI in a public response (AVMA ethics + HIPAA)
   - Assign a named responder (practice manager or owner) — do not route to front desk staff

3. Monitoring:
   - Set up Google Business Profile alerts for new reviews
   - Track rating trend monthly: current rating, reviews last 90 days, response rate

4. Quality signal:
   - Negative review themes that recur > 3 times → escalate to operations review
     (wait times, communication, billing surprise, staff attitude are common drivers)
   - Use review themes as a leading indicator of client experience issues before they reach churn
```

**Do:**
- Claim and verify the Google Business Profile, Yelp, and Facebook business listing before doing anything else — unclaimed profiles can be modified by anyone.
- Benchmark the practice's review count and rating against the nearest 3–5 competitors at least quarterly.
- Treat recurring negative review themes as operational data, not just reputation risk.

**Don't:**
- Offer incentives for reviews (discounts, free services) — this violates Google's and Yelp's policies and can result in review removal.
- Post fake or solicited reviews that misrepresent genuine client experience — this is both unethical and legally risky.
- Ignore a 1-star review because "it's probably a competitor" — readers see the response (or absence of one) and judge the practice accordingly.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Emergency and referral hospitals rely more on DVM-to-DVM referral networks than consumer online reviews for new-client acquisition. The review management protocol still applies but at lower priority than referring-DVM relationship management.
