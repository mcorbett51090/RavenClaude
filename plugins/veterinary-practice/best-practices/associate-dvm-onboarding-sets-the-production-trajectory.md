# Associate DVM Onboarding Sets the Production Trajectory

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Staffing / DVM economics
**Applies to:** `veterinary-practice`

---

## Why this exists

An associate DVM who is not fully integrated into the practice's clinical protocols, client communication standards, and scheduling template within their first 90 days will typically reach only 60–75% of their expected production potential — and may never reach full potential if the habits form wrong. [unverified — training knowledge] The ramp-up cost is real: an associate paid on a guaranteed base while producing below the break-even threshold is an operating loss that compounds monthly. Practices that invest in structured onboarding reach break-even faster and retain associates longer — retention is the other side of the onboarding equation, since turnover at 12–18 months resets the ramp cycle entirely.

## How to apply

Build a 90-day structured onboarding plan for every associate DVM before their start date.

```
90-day associate onboarding framework:

Week 1–2 (orientation):
  - Shadow the medical director and one experienced associate for full days
  - Review: all practice protocols (cases, diagnostics, anesthetic standards)
  - Review: PIMS workflow, controlled substance logs, prescription protocols
  - Client communication standards: how to present treatment plans, how to discuss cost
  - Meet all support staff and understand the technician-delegation workflow

Week 3–4 (supervised independent schedule):
  - Own a partial schedule (50–60% of full capacity) with medical director available
  - First-case debriefs daily (10 min): what was seen, what was decided, any protocol questions
  - Track: diagnostic utilization vs. practice standard for new associate

Month 2 (ramp to 75% schedule):
  - Expand to 75% schedule
  - Weekly check-in: production per hour vs. associate target and ACT
  - Identify: any case types the associate is avoiding or under-working (surgical, dental, derm)
  - Targeted protocol review for case types where utilization lags

Month 3 (full schedule + performance review):
  - Full schedule capacity
  - 90-day review: production per hour vs. plan, client satisfaction, compliance metrics
  - Establish: year-1 production target with monthly tracking vs. plan

Compensation note:
  - If associate is on a guaranteed base, calculate the monthly production needed to break even
    on that base. If Month 3 production is below break-even, investigate and intervene —
    don't wait for the Year 1 review.
```

**Do:**
- Assign a named mentor (medical director or senior DVM) for the full 90 days — not a rotating shadow schedule.
- Include client communication training explicitly — most production gaps in new associates are communication-driven, not clinical.
- Track production per hour vs. plan starting in Month 2, not Year 1 — catching the trajectory early allows a course correction.

**Don't:**
- Put a new associate on a full schedule in Week 1 — the speed gain is illusory; client complaints and protocol deviations in Week 1 are expensive.
- Assume a board-certified specialist-track graduate needs less onboarding — clinical competence and practice-specific workflow compliance are different skills.
- Confuse a slow production ramp with a motivation problem before confirming that the protocol and schedule-template training was complete.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Experienced associates (5+ years, strong protocol alignment) who have worked in a similar practice model may compress the 90-day plan to 30–45 days with expedited case-type confirmation. Still conduct a 90-day production review.
