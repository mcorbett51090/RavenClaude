# F&I must clear compliance — no payment packing, ever

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** F&I process, dealership compliance
**Applies to:** `automotive-dealership`

---

## Why this exists

Payment packing — including the cost of an undisclosed F&I product in a customer's
quoted monthly payment without their knowledge — is an unfair or deceptive act or
practice under the Federal Trade Commission Act § 5. It is not a gray area, a legacy
practice to gradually phase out, or a "how the industry used to do it" exception. It is
a per se violation that can result in:

- FTC enforcement action and significant civil penalties
- Loss of lender relationships (lenders terminate contracts with dealers found to be packing)
- State attorney general actions under state consumer protection laws
- Individual consumer litigation and class action exposure
- Franchise manufacturer relationship damage and potential franchise termination

The practice is also strategically self-defeating: a fully-disclosed menu-selling process,
documented across 20-group studies, consistently produces higher PVR than a non-disclosed
process. The compliance is not a constraint on F&I performance; it is the foundation of it.

## How to apply

Before any F&I process coaching, training, or PVR improvement discussion, clear the
compliance gate.

**Do:**

- Present every F&I product on a fully-disclosed menu that shows individual product prices
  simultaneously — not as a bundle, not hidden in a payment.
- Show the customer their payment both with and without each product.
- Document customer acceptance or decline with a signed/initialed menu retained in the
  deal jacket.
- Present to 100% of customers, 100% of the time — no exceptions for "they won't want it"
  or "it's a cash deal."
- Run an OFAC SDN check on every financed transaction and document the result.

**Don't:**

- Include any F&I product cost in a quoted payment without showing the product price
  separately. This is payment packing regardless of intent.
- Use "four-square" or similar payment-obfuscation methods that bundle trade allowance,
  down payment, rate, and F&I product into a single payment figure.
- Add a product to the RISC after the customer has signed without a separate documented
  agreement.
- Present products after funding has been confirmed (post-contract sales require separate
  compliance treatment and are restricted in many states [verify-at-use]).
- Frame compliance as an optional best practice — it is a non-negotiable operational standard.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

There are no exceptions to the prohibition on undisclosed payment packing. There is no
deal size, customer type, vehicle type, or market condition that creates an exception.
The only variance is in how disclosure is structured (menu format, digital vs paper,
payment-inclusive vs payment-exclusive presentation options) — all of which are compliant
structures. Consult qualified dealership counsel for jurisdiction-specific requirements.

FTC Act § 5; FRB/CFPB fair-lending guidelines; state consumer protection acts (vary by
state); NADA compliance resources; industry consensus documented in dealer counsel
publications and NIADA/NADA dealer compliance training programs. This rule is not based
on interpretation — the prohibition is explicit in FTC enforcement actions against dealers.
