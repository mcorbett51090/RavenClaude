# Fixed ops pays the bills — chase absorption

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Dealership operations, fixed ops
**Applies to:** `automotive-dealership`

---

## Why this exists

A franchise dealership's overhead — rent, payroll, floor plan, utilities — runs whether or
not a single unit is sold. Fixed operations (service, parts, body shop) generate gross that
offsets that overhead every day of the month, regardless of variable sales. A store with
100% absorption means fixed ops covers 100% of total overhead: every dollar of variable
gross becomes net profit. Most stores run at 70–80% [verify-at-use] — meaning variable
ops must cover 20–30% of overhead before earning a dollar of net profit. Closing that gap
is often the highest-ROI investment a dealership can make, yet it is frequently under-
managed relative to variable marketing spend.

## How to apply

Treat absorption rate improvement as the top-priority structural initiative for any store
below 90%. Before adding variable marketing spend, calculate: "what would it cost to add
10 percentage points of absorption, and what is the net profit equivalent in variable units
sold?"

**Do:**

- Track absorption rate monthly (and weekly via the DOR as a paced figure).
- Diagnose below-target absorption with the three-lever framework: RO count, ELR, overhead.
- Invest in service-advisor training, tech productivity, and CP customer retention as
  priority line items.
- Reprice internal ROs at posted rate — internal subsidies silently drain fixed gross.
- Set a written absorption target for the service director with a monthly review.

**Don't:**

- Chase variable marketing spend when absorption is below 85% — the structural fix delivers
  more net dollars than incremental unit volume.
- Accept warranty ELR as a lever — OEM caps are fixed; redirect energy to CP ELR and RO count.
- Measure fixed ops only as a line on the DOR without drilling into its components.
- Let internal recon pricing slide because "it's our own car" — it's real labor sold at
  a discount to yourself.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

In a new-point start-up (brand new franchise with no existing service customer base),
absorption will naturally be low for the first 12–24 months. The investment in variable
during the ramp phase is appropriate; the absorption focus intensifies as the service base
builds. Similarly, in a rapid new-vehicle unit-growth push (manufacturer incentive, large
fleet conquest), temporarily accepting lower absorption to capture share may be justified
with an explicit plan to convert those unit customers to service retention.
