## Door-to-door time — the product, and its segments

- **Door-to-door time** (a.k.a. total visit time / length-of-stay) = arrival → discharge. In walk-in / episodic urgent care this *is* the product the patient buys — convenience — and the primary driver of the review score and repeat/word-of-mouth demand.
- Split it into **door-to-triage**, **triage-to-provider** (often the largest and most variable — the "wait to be seen" the patient perceives), and **provider-to-discharge**. Attack the *longest* segment; the fix differs by segment (registration/greeter → door-to-triage; flow/provider coverage → triage-to-provider; ancillary turnaround/documentation → provider-to-discharge).
- **Directional benchmark:** operators commonly target a total visit time in the sub-hour range for low-acuity fast-track visits and longer for visits needing imaging/labs; the perceived "wait to be seen" is what most drives satisfaction. _(Directional, not a fixed rule — targets vary by market/acuity mix; re-verify, 2026-07-13.)_

## Split-flow / fast-track — the throughput lever

- **What it is:** separating the patient stream by acuity/complexity — a **fast-track** for low-acuity, quick visits (simple complaints, no imaging) running in parallel with a **main flow** for workups needing x-ray/labs/procedures — so simple visits aren't stuck behind complex ones in a single queue.
- **Why it beats headcount:** it raises throughput per square foot and per provider by matching resource to acuity; **design the flow before buying labor** — the staffing ask usually shrinks once the flow is right.
- Supporting patterns: provider-in-triage / rapid-medical-evaluation at the front, results-waiting areas, and vertical (chair, not bed) flow for low-acuity patients. _(Consensus operating practice; the specific model varies by footprint — verify.)_

## Provider productivity & demand-curve staffing

- **Provider productivity** is commonly measured in **patients per provider-hour** — the throughput a single provider (physician / NP / PA) sustains per hour. It sets how many provider-hours a given visit volume needs. _(Typical ranges vary by acuity mix, ancillary support, and EMR efficiency — re-verify against the site, 2026-07-13.)_
- **The demand curve has two shapes:** an **intraday** curve (a pronounced late-afternoon / early-evening peak, and often a secondary weekend peak) and a **seasonal** curve (a strong respiratory-season / winter surge, sometimes 2–3× the summer trough).
- **Staff to both curves.** Flat staffing over-pays the trough and buckles at the peak. Match the **provider + MA/tech matrix** to the hourly and seasonal curve; contract a **flex pool** (per-diem/PRN providers, cross-trained MA/tech) ahead of respiratory season. Labor is the largest controllable operating line — this is where an operator wins or loses it.

## Occupational medicine — a distinct high-margin contracted line

- **What it is:** employer-paid, directly contracted services — **pre-employment physicals, drug/alcohol screens, injury care, workers'-compensation care, DOT physicals, and employer wellness/screening**.
- **Why it matters:** it is contracted (paid by the employer or the workers'-comp carrier on a set schedule), **insulated from payer-mix erosion**, often **higher-margin and more predictable** than episodic acute care, and it can absorb the **morning trough** of the acute demand curve — a capacity synergy.
- **Its own business:** a **sales motion** built on employer (HR/safety-manager) relationships, its own **scheduling/appointment** model, and its own **capacity** demands. Plan and price it as a separate P&L line, not a byproduct of acute volume. _(Contract terms and pricing vary by market and employer — verify with a retrieval date.)_

## Point-of-care testing (POCT), on-site x-ray & procedures — ancillary services

- **On-site x-ray / imaging** keeps imaging-needing visits in-house (fracture/sprain evaluation, chest complaints), raising revenue per visit and in-house retention — but adds capex (equipment), staffing (a rad tech or trained staff), scope, and **provider-to-discharge time** (the turnaround).
- **POCT / on-site labs** (rapid strep, flu, COVID, urinalysis, glucose, etc.) speed diagnosis and keep the visit in-house; each test has a capture-and-billing tail (an ancillary run but not recorded is lost revenue).
- **Procedures** (laceration repair, splinting, incision & drainage, foreign-body removal) raise revenue per visit and depend on the **scope of service** the medical director sets.
- **Discipline:** each ancillary is a **capex-and-throughput decision with a payback** — model the revenue-per-visit lift *and* the door-to-door-time cost; don't add for prestige. _(Equipment/reagent costs and reimbursement vary — verify.)_

## Scope of service — a clinical-and-operational line, drawn with the medical director

- **What it sets:** the **acuity ceiling** (the upper bound of what the center treats before transfer/referral), the **procedure menu**, the **pediatric age floor**, and the **transfer/EMTALA-adjacent protocol** for patients beyond scope.
- **Why it's not an operations solo call:** scope drives staffing, malpractice exposure, equipment, and clinical protocol — it is set **with the medical director**, a clinical-and-operational decision. This team frames the *operational and economic* trade-offs; the clinical line is the medical director's. **Not clinical advice.**

## Payer mix & in-network contracting

- **Payer mix** — the distribution of visits across commercial, Medicare, Medicaid, workers'-comp/occ-med, and self-pay — is a primary revenue driver: revenue per visit follows the mix and the **contracted rates** more than raw volume. A center full of a low-reimbursing payer can out-volume and under-earn a better-mix center.
- **In-network contracting** is a **rate-vs-volume portfolio decision** — which payers to join, which to renegotiate, and which to stay out of, prioritized by revenue-per-visit impact and the local patient base. _(Contract norms and rates are market-specific and volatile — verify with a retrieval date.)_

## Visit economics — revenue per visit's three drivers

- **Revenue per visit = E/M level distribution × ancillary capture × contracted rate.** Diagnose which is the constraint before prescribing:
  - **E/M level distribution** — the spread of visit levels billed; an under-leveled distribution depresses revenue. **The code on any given claim is a `medical-revenue-cycle` determination — this team diagnoses the *distribution*, never assigns the code.** _(Coding is OUT OF SCOPE here.)_
  - **Ancillary capture** — leakage between the service delivered (x-ray, POCT) and the revenue recorded.
  - **Contracted rate** — a stale/low payer contract caps revenue regardless of level or ancillary.

## Self-pay / price transparency

- **Posted self-pay pricing** is both a **regulatory/price-transparency expectation** and a **demand lever** for the uninsured segment. Set it deliberately against the local market and the acute + ancillary menu — a transparent, defensible schedule, not an afterthought. _(Price-transparency rules and their scope are volatile and jurisdiction-specific — verify with a retrieval date; not legal advice.)_

## EMR/PM & industry landscape (dated — volatile, re-verify before quoting)

- **Urgent-care-specific EMR/PM platforms:** **Experity** (formed from the merger of **Practice Velocity** and **DocuTAP** — the dominant urgent-care-purpose-built lineage), and **Athenahealth** among broader ambulatory platforms; these handle registration, charting, e-prescribing, the occ-med workflow, and reporting. _(Retrieved 2026-07-13; the vendor landscape and feature parity change — verify.)_
- **Industry association / benchmarks:** the **Urgent Care Association (UCA)** publishes benchmarking (visit volumes, payer mix, staffing, wait-time norms) that sets the industry reference — use it as a directional benchmark, not a target to match. _(Specific figures move — retrieve current UCA data before quoting.)_
- **Demand / local-marketing spine:** Google Business Profile, reviews (the door-to-door-time output), online check-in / reserve-a-time, and local SEO drive walk-in demand; occ-med demand comes from employer relationships, a different channel.
