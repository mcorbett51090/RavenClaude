## Estimate types — binding, non-binding, not-to-exceed, hourly

- **Binding estimate** — a **fixed price** for the described shipment and services. It doesn't change with actual weight unless the shipment or services materially change. The **mover** carries the under-count risk. Good for customer certainty; dangerous if the inventory was under-counted.
- **Non-binding estimate** — an **estimate of likely cost**; the final charge is based on **actual weight/services**. Federal rules limit how much *more* than the estimate a mover can require at delivery (a percentage-over cap, with the balance billed later — verify the current rule). The **customer** carries the upside risk.
- **Not-to-exceed (guaranteed-not-to-exceed)** — the customer pays the **lower** of the estimate or the actual final cost — customer-favorable. Increasingly common as a competitive offer.
- **Local hourly** — **crew-hours × hourly rate + travel/trip charge + materials**, the typical structure for local moves. Runs over when the cube or access was under-estimated — buffer and communicate.

**Discipline:** the estimate type is a **risk-allocation decision**. Recommend it deliberately (operations lead) and disclose it as required (specialist). _(Estimate-type rules and the non-binding over-collection cap are federal and volatile — verify + retrieval-date, 2026-07-13.)_

## Cube sheet vs weight — how the number is built

- **Cube sheet** — a room-by-room inventory where each item carries an estimated **cubic-foot** volume; the totals give the shipment's cube. A common industry rule of thumb converts cube to weight at roughly **7 lb per cubic foot** _(rule of thumb — verify against the actual shipment, 2026-07-13)_.
- **Weight basis** — long-distance/interstate pricing is commonly **weight-and-distance** (actual weight via certified scale × a tariff rate for the mileage). The estimate uses cube→weight; the **actual** weight is confirmed by weighing the loaded truck (origin/destination or tare/gross).
- **Why it matters:** an estimate built without an inventory is a coin flip — it shows up as a loss (binding) or a delivery-day dispute (non-binding). The cube sheet is the spine of the whole job.

## Local vs long-distance pricing

- **Local** — typically **hourly**: crew size × hourly rate × hours, plus a travel/trip fee and materials. Priced on labor and time.
- **Long-distance / interstate** — typically **weight-and-distance** (or cube) against a **tariff**, plus accessorials (long carry, stairs/elevator, shuttle, bulky items, packing). Priced on the physics of the shipment and the miles.
- **Don't cross the wires:** pricing a long-haul like a local hourly job (or vice versa) is a structural error, not a rounding one.

## Valuation coverage — released value vs full-value protection (a liability level, NOT insurance)

> **Valuation is the mover's liability, not third-party insurance.** Federal rules require interstate movers to offer these; the exact figures and rules are volatile — verify + retrieval-date.

- **Released value** — the **default** liability limit, historically **~60¢ per pound per article** _(figure volatile — verify against current FMCSA rule, 2026-07-13)_. No separate charge. Minimal protection: a 40-lb TV pays out ~$24 regardless of its real value.
- **Full-value protection (FVP)** — the mover is liable to **repair, replace, or settle** for the item's value; **priced separately** and the customer elects it. Much broader; the standard "full" option.
- **Actual insurance** — a **separate third-party product** bought from an insurer. If a customer asks about *insurance*, that's a different question — route it out; valuation ≠ insurance.
- **Why the election matters at booking:** a later **claim settles on whichever valuation the customer elected** — released 60¢/lb and full-value produce very different payouts. The claim outcome is fixed the day coverage is chosen.

## FMCSA operating authority — USDOT & MC numbers + intrastate variance

- **Interstate (across state lines)** household-goods moves fall under **FMCSA**. A for-hire interstate mover needs a **USDOT number** (registration/identifier) and **MC operating authority** (the "motor carrier" authority to transport regulated commodities for hire). Without them, the move can't legally be performed. _(FMCSA registration specifics are volatile — verify + retrieval-date, 2026-07-13.)_
- **Intrastate (within one state)** moves are governed by **that state's** licensing/authority and tariff/rate rules, which **vary materially by state** — some states run their own household-goods tariff and licensing regime; others are lighter-touch. Never assume one state's rule applies elsewhere. _(State-by-state — verify per state.)_
- **The first question is always interstate vs intrastate** — it selects the whole regulatory regime, the paperwork, and the tariff basis.

## Required federal disclosures (interstate)

> The federally-mandated consumer-protection documents for interstate household-goods moves. Titles/requirements are volatile — verify + retrieval-date (2026-07-13).

- **"Your Rights and Responsibilities When You Move"** — the FMCSA-mandated booklet a mover must provide to interstate customers.
- **"Ready to Move?"** — the companion FMCSA tips brochure.
- **Order for service** — the document listing the services and rates the mover agrees to provide.
- **Bill of Lading (BOL)** — the **contract of carriage + receipt** for the shipment; the governing document for the move (and, with the inventory, the reference for a claim).
- **Inventory** — the itemized condition record at loading.

_(The mandated disclosure set and its titles are FMCSA-defined and change — verify against current FMCSA guidance before relying on it; route the compliance determination to counsel / FMCSA.)_

## Tariffs

- A **tariff** is the published schedule of rates, rules, and charges a carrier applies. **Interstate** household-goods carriers must **maintain and apply a tariff**; **intrastate** pricing follows the **state's** tariff/rate rules.
- The operations lead builds the *number*; the specialist confirms it rests on a **valid, current tariff** and that the estimate type is disclosed as required. _(Tariff conventions and filing requirements vary and change — verify + retrieval-date.)_

## Van-line vs independent

- **Van-line agent** — an operator affiliated with a national **van line** (the brand/network); the van line provides the interstate authority umbrella, national booking/interline, brand, and back-office; the agent runs local operations. Trades autonomy and margin share for reach and a ready interstate channel.
- **Independent** — the mover holds its **own** authority (USDOT/MC for interstate) and brand; keeps more margin and control but carries its own compliance, booking demand, and long-distance interlining. _(Model economics vary — a strategic choice, not a default.)_

## Lead & booking economics

- **Lead sources:** organic/GBP + reviews, paid search, lead marketplaces/brokers (pay-per-lead), van-line referrals, and repeat/referral. Cost-per-lead and close rates vary widely by channel and season.
- **The funnel:** lead → estimate (in-home / virtual-survey / self-survey) → booked job → completed move → review. The **estimate quality and speed of response** are the biggest booking levers; a slow or inventory-free quote loses the job or loses money on it.
- **Discipline:** track **cost-per-booked-job** (not just cost-per-lead) against the job's margin; the campaign *creative/strategy* is `marketing-operations`, the *economics and the estimate* are this team's.

## Seasonality

- **Summer peak** — the household-goods move season concentrates heavily in **late spring through summer** (school calendar, leases, weather); month-end and weekends peak within that. Capacity, crew hiring, and pricing all flex to it.
- **Off-season** — slower fall/winter; a lever for corporate/commercial work, maintenance, and crew retention. Plan capacity and pricing around the curve rather than being surprised by it.

## The claims process — settled on the elected valuation basis

> **Claims mechanics vary by state/federal law and are operational guidance, NOT legal advice** — attach a state/federal + retrieval date and route the legal question to counsel.

1. **Governing basis** — the claim settles on **whichever valuation the customer elected** (released ~60¢/lb vs full-value protection). This is set at booking, not at the claim.
2. **Filing window** — the customer files within the window the BOL/regulations allow (interstate has a federal minimum filing period — verify current rule + retrieval date).
3. **Documentation** — the claim references the **inventory** and **BOL**, with the item, damage/loss, and value/weight documented.
4. **Mover's response / settlement** — the mover acknowledges and responds within the required timeline, then pays/repairs/replaces/denies on the governing valuation basis.
5. **Dispute path** — unresolved interstate claims may go to **arbitration** (movers must offer a neutral arbitration program for certain disputes — verify current requirement); state rules govern intrastate disputes.
6. **The point:** run it as a documented, timeline-driven process on the correct valuation basis — a mishandled or out-of-window claim is a liability and a review problem. Prevention (accurate inventory, careful packing, clear valuation election) is cheaper than any claim.
