# Close the project formally — a project without a close-out is an obligation that never ends

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Project lifecycle
**Applies to:** `project-management`

---

## Why this exists

Projects without a formal close-out generate three common failure modes: (1) resources are never officially released, so team members sit in limbo between the project and their next assignment; (2) open risks and issues from the RAID register are never resolved or formally accepted, becoming orphaned obligations; (3) lessons learned are never captured, so the organisation repeats the same mistakes in the next similar project. The close-out process is also the moment the customer/sponsor formally accepts the deliverable — without it, the project remains "in flight" indefinitely and the PM retains a vague ongoing responsibility with no clear scope.

## How to apply

**Project close-out checklist (deliverables-first):**

| Step | Action | Owner | Done? |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Deliverable acceptance — obtain written sign-off from the sponsor/customer on all in-scope deliverables | PM / Sponsor | [ ] |
| 2 | Open RAID items resolved or formally transferred — every open risk, issue, and action is either closed, reassigned to BAU, or explicitly accepted as residual | PM + RAID Analyst | [ ] |
| 3 | Resources formally released — team members' end dates confirmed and communicated to line managers; any continuing roles formally defined | PM | [ ] |
| 4 | Financial close — final cost vs budget reconciliation; any open purchase orders closed or formally transferred | PM / Finance | [ ] |
| 5 | Lessons-learned workshop conducted — at least one structured session with the core team before dispersal | PM / Scrum Master | [ ] |
| 6 | Lessons-learned report published and shared — sent to the project sponsor, the programme, and the relevant PMO library | PM | [ ] |
| 7 | Project documentation archived — all artefacts (charter, baseline, register, steering packs, close-out report) in the organisation's designated repository | PM | [ ] |
| 8 | Close-out report issued — brief document: objectives vs outcomes, final cost/schedule performance, lessons learned summary, residual risks accepted | PM | [ ] |
| 9 | Formal sign-off from sponsor — the sponsor confirms the project is closed | Sponsor | [ ] |

**Lessons-learned workshop — minimum questions:**

1. What went well that we should repeat on every project?
2. What went badly that we should change or avoid?
3. Were there risks we did not foresee that we would log earlier next time?
4. What would we do differently in the delivery approach?
5. What would we do differently in stakeholder engagement?

**Do:**
- Schedule the lessons-learned workshop before the team disperses — once people have moved on, attendance is nearly impossible.
- Distribute the lessons-learned report to the PMO or the programme management community, not just within the project team.
- Formally release resources in writing — a conversation is not a release.

**Don't:**
- Treat the Go-live or final deployment as the project end without a formal close-out; go-live is a milestone, not a closure.
- Skip the lessons-learned step because the project was "a success" — successful projects have lessons too, and the positive patterns are as important to capture as the failures.
- Let the close-out report become a lengthy bureaucratic exercise; two to four pages with the five checklist areas is sufficient for most projects.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

When a project is cancelled before completion, a close-out is still required — it documents the cancellation rationale, preserves whatever was delivered, formally releases resources, and formally closes obligations. The artefacts are different from a successful close-out but the discipline is identical. Early termination without formal close-out leaves the same orphaned obligations as a drift to BAU without sign-off.
