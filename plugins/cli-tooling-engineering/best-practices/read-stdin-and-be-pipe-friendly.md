# Read stdin and be pipe-friendly

A CLI earns its place in the Unix toolbox by composing — being something you can drop into the middle of a pipeline. A tool that processes input should **read from stdin when it's piped** (the convention: treat a `-` argument, or the absence of a file argument, as "read stdin"), write its result to stdout, and stay quiet on stdout otherwise. Then `cat data | tool | next` just works. Conversely, don't *demand* interactivity: when stdin isn't a TTY, never block on a prompt — accept the answer via a flag or env var instead. Line-oriented output (one record per line, or NUL-delimited via a `-0`/`--print0` option for filenames) lets `grep`, `awk`, `xargs`, and `sort` operate on your output without special-casing. The measure of a good CLI is how well it disappears into a pipeline.
