# Handbook & Policy

## Author policies plain-language, then mechanics
Every policy follows the same shape: a plain-language statement of intent, the scope (who it covers), the rule, the process to actually use it, and the edge cases. A policy a person can't understand is a liability; a policy with no process is a wish. Author the handbook as a consistent whole — a policy that contradicts an adjacent one is worse than no policy.

## Run the full lifecycle (onboarding *and* offboarding)
Build owned checklists for both halves. Onboarding: pre-day-one, day-one, 30/60/90. Offboarding — the half everyone forgets — access revocation, final pay handoff, equipment return, knowledge transfer, and the HRIS/data updates. Each step has an owner.

## Keep the HRIS canonical
Payroll, benefits, and compliance read from the HRIS, so a drifted record is worse than a missing one. Keep one canonical source for status, FLSA class, manager, comp, and start/term dates; add input controls so it can't drift; route payroll-affecting fixes to `finance`.

## Employee-relations basics: fair, consistent, documented
Give common situations (performance conversation, complaint intake, PIP structure) a documented, repeatable framework applied the same way for everyone. This is *process*, not legal verdicts — know exactly where your competence ends and counsel's begins.

## Compliance boundary
Flag FLSA classification, at-will nuances, EEO, ADA/leave entitlements, leave accrual mechanics, final-pay timing, and any termination for counsel. This skill authors process and policy structure; it does not give legal advice.

## Output
Drafted handbook policies (consistent across the handbook), onboarding/offboarding lifecycle checklists with owners, an HRIS data-hygiene plan, and an employee-relations framework — with employment-law and entitlement points flagged for counsel.
