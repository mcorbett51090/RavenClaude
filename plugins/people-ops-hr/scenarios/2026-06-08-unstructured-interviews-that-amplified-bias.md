## Problem

A ~60-person company was hiring fast and proud of its "high bar," but every interview loop was different: interviewers asked whatever they wanted, debriefs were a room of opinions ("I liked them" / "not sure they'd fit"), and the same role got wildly different loops depending on who was free. Two near-identical candidates got opposite outcomes a week apart, and a hiring manager noticed the no-hires skewed toward people who interviewed differently from the existing team. Separately, the funnel was a mystery — they tracked applicant *count* (which looked great) but had no idea where in the process candidates were actually being lost, so "hiring is slow" had no diagnosable cause.

## Constraints context

- No interview kit, no scorecards, no anchored rating levels — every assessor improvised.
- Debriefs recorded conclusions, not evidence, so a confident voice could swing the room.
- The ATS (Greenhouse) had scorecard and stage-conversion features the team wasn't using; they watched applicant volume, a vanity number.
- A real worry that the inconsistency was creating EEO exposure — which nobody could evaluate internally.

## Attempts

- Tried: telling interviewers to "be more rigorous." Failed — without a rubric, rigor meant different things to different people and the vibes problem persisted.
- Tried: adding more interviewers per loop to "average out" bias. Failed — more unstructured opinions is more noise, not more signal, and it hurt candidate experience (slower, more disjointed).
- Tried: building a structured loop — level the role, map one competency to one assessor, anchor 1-4 rating levels per competency, and force an evidence-cited debrief before any hire/no-hire verdict; then instrument the funnel (conversion + time-in-stage per stage) instead of counting applicants. This worked.

## Resolution

One competency per assessor with anchored scorecards made two candidates comparable for the first time, and the evidence-forced debrief stopped the confident-voice swing. Funnel instrumentation showed the real bottleneck was screen-to-onsite conversion, not sourcing — they'd been widening the top of a funnel that leaked in the middle. Candidate experience improved as a side effect (a consistent, communicative loop is a conversion lever, not just a courtesy). The EEO / disparate-impact question raised by the old inconsistency was flagged for counsel rather than self-assessed.

## Lesson

Structure beats intuition: one competency, one assessor, one anchored scorecard, an evidence-forced debrief — assessed the same way for every candidate. Measure the funnel (conversion + time-in-stage), not the applicant count. Any EEO/disparate-impact determination goes to counsel, never answered in the hiring room.
