## Problem

A ~120-person company asked for "salary bands" because every offer and raise was a one-off negotiation and people had started comparing notes. The People team proposed bands first — pull market data for each title, set a min/mid/max, done. But when they tried to place existing employees into bands, half the roles didn't map cleanly: two people with the same title were doing very different-scope work, and there was no consistent answer for who belonged where. Separately, leadership wanted a one-line answer to "do we have a pay gap?" and someone had produced a raw average showing women paid 11% less — which several managers immediately (and correctly) argued was confounded by seniority.

## Constraints context

- No existing job-architecture / leveling framework — titles were inflated and inconsistent across teams.
- A real fear that publishing bands would trigger raise demands the budget couldn't absorb.
- The 11% raw gap was being treated as either proof of discrimination or proof of nothing, depending on who was talking — no controlled analysis.

## Attempts

- Tried: setting bands directly off title + market data. Failed — without a level under each band, there was no logic for who lands where; same-title/different-scope people couldn't be placed.
- Tried: answering the pay-gap question with the raw 11% average. Failed — it conflated legitimate seniority differences with inequity and convinced no one; it generated heat, not signal.
- Tried: building the leveling ladder first (levels × job families, a competency definition per level), mapping each band to a level, then running the equity review *controlled* for level/tenure/location/performance and surfacing the unexplained residual. This worked.

## Resolution

The ladder gave every band a level underneath it, so placement became a defensible mapping instead of a negotiation. The controlled pay-equity review shrank the headline gap to a small unexplained residual concentrated in two specific cohorts — a fixable, fundable problem instead of an unfalsifiable accusation. The legal certification ("are we equal-pay compliant?") was explicitly flagged for counsel rather than answered in the deck. Merit and promotion budgets were then split into separate pools so the cycle stopped under-rewarding real promotions.

## Lesson

No band without a level under it — build the leveling ladder first or the bands have no logic. A pay-equity "review" that doesn't control for legitimate factors proves nothing; surface the *unexplained* residual, and leave the legal compliance determination to counsel.
