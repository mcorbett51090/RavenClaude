## Problem

A company refreshing its handbook asked People Ops to "just write the leave and overtime policies so managers stop asking." A well-meaning draft went further than it should have: the PTO/leave section stated that a category of roles "are exempt and therefore not eligible for overtime," and the leave section asserted specific eligibility ("employees qualify for protected leave after 12 months") as settled fact. A manager relied on the exempt statement to deny an overtime claim; the role turned out to be misclassified, and the written policy was now evidence that the company had asserted a legal conclusion it wasn't qualified to make. The handbook also contradicted an older standalone PTO memo still circulating, so two "official" answers existed.

## Constraints context

- The team conflated *describing a company process* (how to request leave) with *determining a legal entitlement* (who is exempt, who qualifies) — only the first is theirs.
- Entitlement and accrual mechanics are jurisdiction-specific, and the company operated in more than one state.
- A contradicting older memo meant there was no single canonical answer, which is worse than no policy.

## Attempts

- Tried: writing the policies with the legal conclusions stated outright so managers had a "clear answer." Failed — it created liability by asserting classifications and entitlements the company wasn't positioned to certify, and a manager acted on a wrong one.
- Tried: softening with "we believe" / "generally" hedges. Failed — a hedge on a legal conclusion is still a legal conclusion, and it didn't fix the contradiction with the old memo.
- Tried: re-authoring to the house format (statement → scope → rule → process → edge cases), describing the *process* for requesting leave and reporting hours, and flagging every classification/eligibility/jurisdiction point for counsel rather than stating it — plus retiring the contradicting memo so one canonical answer remained. This worked.

## Resolution

The handbook now tells employees *how* to request leave and *how* hours are reported and approved — the company-owned process — while exempt status, leave eligibility, and overtime entitlement are explicitly "have counsel confirm for your situation/jurisdiction" rather than asserted. The contradicting memo was retired so there was one source of truth. Counsel reviewed the classification and multi-state entitlement questions; People Ops owned the plain-language process and consistency.

## Lesson

Plain language, then mechanics — and the cardinal rule: the moment a policy reaches an entitlement, eligibility, or classification (exempt status, protected leave, overtime), the output is "flag for counsel," not an answer. Describe the company process; never opine on the legal determination. A contradicting policy is worse than none — keep one canonical answer.
