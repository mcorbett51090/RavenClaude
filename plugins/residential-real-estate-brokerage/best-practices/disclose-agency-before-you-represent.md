# Disclose agency before you represent

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Compliance / agency
**Applies to:** `residential-real-estate-brokerage`

> Advisory operations rule, **not legal advice.** Agency rules and required disclosures are jurisdiction- and brokerage-policy-specific — `[verify-at-use]`. No client PII.

---

## Why this exists

Whom you represent, and what that means for confidentiality and loyalty, must be **established in writing before you act as anyone's agent.** An undisclosed or ambiguous agency relationship — especially a dual-agency situation where the brokerage touches both sides — is a compliance and liability exposure that a commission never justifies. The disclosure protects the client and the brokerage, and it has to happen first, not at closing.

## How to apply

- At the **start of representation**, disclose the agency options and establish the relationship in writing per jurisdiction rules (`[verify-at-use]`).
- If the brokerage would represent **both sides**, proceed only where dual/designated agency is permitted and only with **informed written consent from both** — otherwise refer out or assign separate designated agents per policy.
- Maintain **strict confidentiality** of each side's negotiating position in any dual/designated arrangement.
- Never let the size of a commission drive an undisclosed conflict — traverse the agency decision tree before proceeding.

**Do:** establish agency in writing first; handle dual agency by informed consent or refer out.
**Don't:** let a deal proceed on an undisclosed or convenient agency relationship.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Pre-representation informational conversations aren't yet agency — but the moment advice crosses into representation, the disclosure obligation attaches. When in doubt, disclose earlier, not later.
