# Goal-Based Financial Plan

> **Not investment advice.** This skill produces an educational planning framework, not a personalized recommendation. The licensed adviser applies it to a specific client after confirming the flagged facts; a CPA / attorney owns the tax and legal conclusions.

## Start from goals, quantify them
List the client's goals (retire, education, home, legacy), each with a horizon, a funding target, and the savings it requires. The goal sets the target; everything downstream serves it. A plan that leads with a product is a sale, not a plan.

## Surface every assumption
Inflation, expected return, longevity, horizon, and the savings rate dominate the answer. Return them as a named, editable list — never bury an assumed return inside a confident number. Present a scenario range (good / expected / poor sequence), not a false-precision point.

## Cash flow and the account-funding order
Establish income → expenses → surplus → savings rate. Then the funding order: capture the employer match → clear high-interest debt → emergency fund → HSA (if HDHP-eligible) → tax-advantaged (Roth vs Traditional by now-vs-later bracket) → taxable. Eligibility and bracket are client-specific facts to confirm with a CPA.

## Retirement & withdrawal strategy
The 4% rule is a planning heuristic, not a guarantee. On a tight plan, prefer dynamic guardrails or a cash buffer to survive sequence-of-returns risk (bad early years hurt most). Name longevity risk and pair the rate with a review trigger.

## Estate basics
Flag beneficiary designations (they override the will), account titling, and the will/trust question — then route drafting to an estate attorney.

## Output
A goal-based plan: prioritized goals + targets, the assumptions list, the cash-flow / funding order, the withdrawal strategy, and the estate flags — with the not-investment-advice disclaimer and the client-specific facts to confirm. Hand the allocation + IPS to `portfolio-construction-and-ips` and the suitability gate to `client-review-and-suitability`.
