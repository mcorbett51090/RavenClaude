# Fair-housing language in every listing and every reply

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Fair-housing compliance
**Applies to:** `property-management-residential`

---

## Why this exists

The Fair Housing Act prohibits statements that indicate a preference for or against any person
because of race, color, national origin, religion, sex, familial status, or disability. State and
local laws extend this to source of income, sexual orientation, marital status, and other classes
in many jurisdictions.

Listing copy and reply language that implies a preferred resident profile — even without the intent
to discriminate — creates legal exposure. HUD enforcement and private fair-housing testers actively
monitor listings. A single phrase ("no kids," "perfect for a young professional," "quiet neighborhood
ideal for retirees," "close to [religious institution] — great for [religious group]") can trigger
a complaint investigation, a conciliation agreement, or litigation.

The rule is simple: describe the unit, not the ideal resident.

## How to apply

- Write every listing to describe the **property** — square footage, bedrooms, amenities, lease
  terms, pet policy, utility responsibility — not the type of person who should live there.
- Review every reply to an inquiry for language that expresses or implies a preference or
  exclusion based on a protected class.
- Use `pm-compliance-advisor` to review listing copy and template reply language before publishing.

**Do:**

- Describe physical features, amenities, and lease terms.
- State pet policy factually ("no pets," "cats and small dogs under 25 lbs. allowed with deposit").
- Use neutral language for neighborhood: "located in [neighborhood name]," "walkable to [amenity]."
- Say "two-bedroom apartment" not "cozy apartment — ideal for a couple."

**Don't:**

- Use phrases that imply a preferred resident: "perfect for a single professional," "ideal for a
  young couple," "great for empty nesters," "perfect for families," "quiet — great for retirees."
- Mention proximity to religious institutions in a way that implies a preferred religion.
- Use phrases that imply exclusion of families with children: "no kids," "adults only" (unless a
  lawful senior housing exemption applies — verify jurisdiction), "perfect for someone without
  children."
- Use phrases implying national origin or language preference: "English-speaking preferred,"
  "great for [nationality] families."
- Describe a neighborhood in ways that code for race, national origin, or religion.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- **Senior housing exemptions:** Housing for persons 55+ or 62+ may lawfully restrict to those
  age groups under the Housing for Older Persons Act (HOPA) — but the exemption has strict
  requirements (qualifying facility, 80% of units occupied by at least one person 55+, published
  age verification policies). Verify the exemption before using age-restricted language. Familial
  status (children) restrictions remain unlawful in non-qualifying properties.
- **Disability accommodations:** a unit description may not exclude persons with disabilities, but
  it may accurately state physical limitations of the property (e.g., "no elevator, third-floor
  walk-up") as factual information, not as a preference or exclusion.

## Provenance

Codifies the Fair Housing Act (42 U.S.C. §3604(c)) and HUD's implementing regulations (24 C.F.R.
Part 100), which prohibit discriminatory advertising and statements. Applies to all media:
listings, ads, social media, and oral or written replies to inquiries. Not legal advice — verify
current HUD guidance and retain qualified counsel for enforcement questions.
