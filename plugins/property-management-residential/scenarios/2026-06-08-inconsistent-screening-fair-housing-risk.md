## Problem

A small property manager screened applicants "by feel" — each leasing agent applied a slightly different bar for income, credit, and prior evictions, and denials were communicated verbally with no recorded reason. A denied applicant alleged discrimination. The manager had no consistent written standard and no contemporaneous record of why this applicant was denied while others with similar files were approved — which is exactly the pattern a fair-housing complaint feeds on, regardless of anyone's actual intent.

## Constraints context

- Multiple leasing agents, no single documented criteria; "we just know a good tenant."
- Denials given over the phone, nothing written down.
- The team kept asking the agent to *rule* on whether their practice was legal — which it cannot and must not do.

## Attempts

- Tried: writing a one-line "be consistent" policy. Failed — without concrete criteria, "consistent" was still per-agent judgment.
- Tried: asking the agent whether their criteria were fair-housing-compliant. Correctly refused — the agent FLAGGED it and routed the legality question to counsel rather than adjudicating it.
- Tried: a single documented standard (income multiple, credit threshold, eviction lookback, occupancy standard) applied identically to every applicant, with each decision recording the criterion it rested on and the date — and the criminal-history / source-of-income elements explicitly flagged to counsel before use. This worked operationally.

## Resolution

The documented, uniformly-applied standard plus a per-decision record turned "trust us" into "here is the same criterion applied to everyone, dated." The agent never opined on legality — it produced the operational standard and routed every protected-class-adjacent element to counsel. The contemporaneous record became the defense, and the per-agent variance (the real exposure) disappeared.

## Lesson

Screen by one consistent, documented standard applied identically to every applicant, and record which criterion each denial rests on. Fair-housing legality is a flag-and-route to counsel, never an opinion the agent gives — the agent's job is uniformity, documentation, and escalation, not adjudication.
