# Overhead Allocation Rate Must Be Built Before Pricing Any Job

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Job costing / pricing
**Applies to:** `skilled-trades-contracting`

---

## Why this exists

Most trade contractors calculate their labor rate as wages plus burden (payroll taxes, benefits, workers' comp). They forget overhead — rent, truck payments, insurance, dispatch software, office salaries, owner's compensation — which must also be recovered from billable revenue. A contractor who prices jobs without a built overhead allocation is setting a price that covers field labor cost but not the office, the yard, or the owner's draw. The overhead allocation rate turns total overhead into a per-hour (or per-revenue-dollar) cost that is included in every estimate. Without it, revenue can grow while profit shrinks, and the contractor won't understand why.

## How to apply

Build the overhead allocation rate annually before the estimating season:

```
Overhead allocation rate model:
  Annual overhead expenses:
    Rent / facility:                    $______/year
    Vehicle payments (non-field):       $______/year
    Office / admin labor:               $______/year
    Owner / manager salary (market):    $______/year
    Software, subscriptions, tools:     $______/year
    Marketing / advertising:            $______/year
    Insurance (non-field, non-vehicle): $______/year
    Other G&A:                          $______/year
    Total annual overhead:              $______/year

  Allocation base (annual billable labor hours or annual revenue):
    Method A — per billable hour:
      Total billable hours (tech count × annual billable hrs):   ______
      Overhead rate: total overhead / total billable hours = $______/hour

    Method B — % of revenue:
      Budgeted annual revenue:     $______
      Overhead rate: total overhead / revenue = ______% overhead margin target

  Pricing check:
    Loaded labor rate = wage + burden + overhead allocation = $______/hour
    This is the rate used in estimates; anything below this loses money.
```

**Do:**
- Rebuild the overhead rate annually; headcount changes, rent increases, and software costs shift overhead significantly year-to-year.
- Include owner's salary at market rate (what you'd pay someone to do your job) — not paying yourself is not cost-free.
- Present the overhead rate in the pricing training for any estimator; if they don't know it exists, they can't include it.

**Don't:**
- Use only wages and burden as the labor cost basis in flat-rate or T&M pricing — overhead recovery is what the business runs on.
- Set the overhead rate to match the competition's pricing without first building your own cost structure — matching a competitor who is undercapitalized or under-insured is a losing position.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Subcontractors working under a general contractor's umbrella who have their overhead reimbursed separately under a cost-plus contract may allocate differently — but the identification and tracking of overhead is still required.
