# Change Order Discipline Protects Margin on Every Construction Job

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** New construction / project management
**Applies to:** `skilled-trades-contracting`

---

## Why this exists

Scope creep — doing extra work outside the contract scope without a signed change order — is one of the most common causes of profitable-looking construction jobs ending with negative margins. A general contractor or homeowner who says "while you're here, can you also..." and gets a yes without a price change has received free labor. Over a construction job lasting 3–4 weeks, cumulative unreimbursed scope additions can total 10–25% of the original contract value [unverified — training knowledge]. The signed change order is the mechanism that keeps scope additions priced and agreed to before work begins — not after, when leverage has evaporated.

## How to apply

Build change order discipline into every construction and new-install job:

```
Change order protocol (per project):
  Pre-job:
  [ ] Original scope is written, itemized, and signed by the customer
  [ ] Change order procedure is included in the contract: "All changes to scope must be documented
      in a written change order signed by both parties before work begins"
  [ ] Change order template is in the technician's dispatch kit or app

  During job (field protocol):
    Any request for work outside the original scope:
    1. Technician says: "I can absolutely do that. Let me put a price on it and get your approval."
    2. Technician completes the change order form (description, estimated hours, materials, price)
    3. Customer signs (digital or paper) before work begins
    4. Technician keeps a copy; dispatcher or office is notified

  Change order form (minimum fields):
    Job ID: ______   Date: ______   Customer signature: ______
    Change description: ______
    Labor added (hours × rate): $______
    Materials added: $______
    Total change order amount: $______
    Impact on completion date (if any): ______

Monthly change order audit:
    Number of change orders issued: ______
    Number approved by customer: ______%
    Total value recovered: $______
    Estimated unreimbursed scope (verbal agreements, uninvoiced additions): $______
```

**Do:**
- Train every technician and project manager on the change order script — the customer response is almost always positive when the technician frames it as "getting you a price before we start."
- Review any job that came in below estimated margin for unreimbursed scope; it is often the explanation.
- Set a minimum dollar threshold for formal change orders (e.g., any change > $50 requires written approval) — below that, use a quick verbal acknowledgment documented in the job notes.

**Don't:**
- Do extra work first and try to add it to the invoice after the job is complete — the customer will dispute it, and you have no leverage.
- Waive change orders for "small" additions that the technician judges will not cause a dispute — the cumulative effect across all jobs is the problem, not any individual instance.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Lump-sum contracts with a clear defined scope and a "no extras" clause (common in commercial bid work) require the change order to be submitted and approved through the GC's formal change order process — the discipline is the same but the approval path is longer. Cost-plus contracts where the customer pays actuals have a different change-scope mechanic; the change order is replaced by a scope-revision notice.
