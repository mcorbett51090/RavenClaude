# Service Agreement Renewal Rate Is a Leading Revenue and Margin Indicator

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Skilled trades contracting / recurring revenue
**Applies to:** `skilled-trades-contracting`

---

## Why this exists

A maintenance agreement or service contract is not just a retention tool — it is the single most predictable revenue stream a trade contractor has. The renewal rate on existing agreements predicts next year's recurring revenue more reliably than any single-month sales number, and the economics are superior: agreement customers have 2–4× the annual revenue of non-agreement customers (pre-sold access, preventive visits, priority service), and their first-call profitability is higher because the relationship is established. A contractor who tracks close rate and average ticket but ignores service-agreement renewal rate is watching the wrong scoreboard. A declining renewal rate is a leading indicator of revenue erosion 12–24 months before it shows up in total sales.

## How to apply

Track agreement renewal rate and agreement revenue contribution monthly:

```
Service Agreement KPI Dashboard — [Company] [Month]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Total active agreements:             ___
Agreements up for renewal this month: ___
Agreements renewed this month:        ___
Renewal rate (month):                 ___% (target: ≥ 85%)

Agreements lapsed (not renewed):      ___
  Reason codes:
    P = Price / budget objection:     ___
    S = Sold / moved:                 ___
    C = Competitor:                   ___
    U = Unresponsive:                 ___
    Q = Quality concern:              ___

Trailing 12-month renewal rate:       ___%
YoY trend:                            [ ] Improving  [ ] Stable  [ ] Declining

Agreement revenue (monthly):          $___  (___% of total service revenue)
Average agreement value:              $___/year
New agreements sold this month:       ___  ($___/month revenue)
Net agreement count change (mo/mo):   ___
```

**Revenue value calculation:**

```
Agreement vs. Non-Agreement Customer Value
───────────────────────────────────────────
Agreement customer:
  Annual agreement fee:              $___
  Average additional service calls:  ___ × $___/call = $___
  Total annual revenue:              $___

Non-agreement customer:
  Average service calls/year:        ___ × $___/call = $___

Agreement premium (% uplift):        ___% (target: > 100% of non-agreement)

Renewal rate impact:
  If renewal rate drops from 90% to 80% on 500 agreements × $250 avg:
  = 50 agreements × $250/yr = $12,500/yr in lost recurring revenue
  + estimated additional-service revenue lost = $___
  Total estimated impact of 10pp renewal drop: $___
```

**Do:**
- Call every agreement customer 30–45 days before renewal — not to renew, but to check in; the renewal call is a service touchpoint first, a renewal conversation second.
- Track the reason code on every lapsed agreement — a cluster of "price" codes is a pricing problem; a cluster of "quality" codes is an operational problem; a cluster of "competitor" codes is a market positioning problem.
- Set a target for new-agreements-sold-per-tech per month and track it alongside renewal rate — a contractor who lets lapsed agreements exceed new sales will shrink the agreement base regardless of renewal rate.
- Report agreement revenue as a separate line in the monthly P&L — it is a qualitatively different revenue stream and deserves a separate view.

**Don't:**
- Use renewal rate as a lagging metric only — waiting for the end of the year to count renewals means 12 months of erosion before the diagnosis.
- Price agreements aggressively at sale and then auto-escalate at renewal without communicating value — the renewal rejection spike is predictable and avoidable.
- Treat an agreement as a price-only competition — customers who cancel because a competitor is $30 cheaper often return after one bad service experience; the agreement customer's total value is not the annual fee alone.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

New-construction contractors without a service division do not have a maintenance agreement model; this rule does not apply to their revenue stream. Commercial service contractors with multi-year facility maintenance contracts (3- or 5-year agreements) use contract-expiration management rather than annual renewal tracking — the renewal rate concept applies at contract renewal dates, which may be infrequent.
