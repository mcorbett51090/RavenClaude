## Problem

A mid-size nonprofit with a strong literacy program saw a large federal NOFO for workforce-development services — far bigger than any grant they'd won. Leadership got excited by the dollar figure and the team spent six weeks writing a strong-sounding proposal. They won. Eighteen months in, the award was a millstone: the workforce program required staff competencies they didn't have, pulled their best literacy people onto an unfamiliar mission, carried a 25% match they scrambled to cover every quarter, and had no plausible continuation once the period of performance ended. The literacy program — their actual mission and their best outcomes — quietly atrophied.

## Constraints context

- The org had never run a workforce program; the NOFO rewarded incumbency and demonstrated capacity they didn't have.
- The 25% match on a ~$1.2M award was ~$300K of real money they had to find each year.
- "Sustainability after the grant" was a single hand-waved paragraph in the proposal; no one had pressure-tested it.

## Attempts

- Tried: hiring to fill the workforce-competency gap mid-award. Failed — onboarding lag meant they missed early performance targets, and the new hires didn't share the org's mission.
- Tried: re-budgeting to ease the match strain. Failed — the match was a fixed condition of the award, not a line they could cut.
- Tried (next cycle, the fix): a disciplined go/no-go *before* writing. They built a fit screen (mission, eligibility, capacity), an honest probability-of-award read (new applicant vs. incumbent), the true cost of the match, and a sustainability tail. Two big "exciting" opportunities got a clear no-go; a smaller, on-mission literacy expansion got a go — and they won it and sustained it.

## Resolution

The org adopted go/no-go as a real gate with a named decision-maker, not a formality. The fit screen and the sustainability question now kill off-mission pursuits before the writing team is committed. They pursue fewer grants, win a higher share of the ones that fit, and the flagship program stopped being cannibalized by chase-the-money awards.

## Lesson

Fund the mission, don't chase the money — and make go/no-go a real decision. A won grant that pulls the org off-mission, can't be staffed, carries a match you scramble to cover, and dies at the end of the period of performance is a loss. Name the cost-to-apply, the probability of award, the strings, and the sustainability tail before writing a word; a disciplined "no" is a win.
