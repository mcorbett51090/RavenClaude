## Problem

To smooth its cash flow, a grantee drew down a large block of federal funds early in the period of performance and parked it in the operating account, intending to spend it over the following months. The drawdown wasn't tied to immediate disbursement need — it was a convenience. At the Single Audit the auditor flagged a cash-management finding: federal cash must be drawn only to the minimum needed for actual, imminent disbursement, and the gap between the draw and the spend (including the interest the idle balance earned) was the violation. The org also discovered some of the early-drawn money had paid for costs that crossed the *end* of the period of performance, compounding the problem.

## Constraints context

- The draw-to-need rule isn't about whether the costs were otherwise allowable — these were; it's about the *timing* of the cash relative to disbursement.
- Federal financial reporting (the FFR/SF-425) made the mismatch visible: cash drawn far exceeded outlays for the period.
- Period-of-performance is a hard boundary — costs are allowable only when incurred in-period, and some of the parked cash was spent after the period closed without explicit authority.

## Attempts

- Tried: explaining the early draw as prudent cash management. Failed — "draw to need" is the rule; minimizing the org's float doesn't override the time-between-draw-and-disbursement standard.
- Tried: re-dating the late expenditures to fall inside the period. Failed — costs are allowable when *incurred* in-period; re-dating documentation is not a fix and raises a worse problem.
- Tried (the fix): switching to drawing federal cash only against imminent, documented disbursement need, reconciling draws to outlays each FFR cycle, and tracking the period-of-performance end date as a hard stop with pre-/post-period costs requiring explicit prior authority.

## Resolution

Drawdowns moved to a draw-to-need cadence reconciled against actual outlays every reporting cycle, and the period-of-performance end became a tracked hard boundary. Idle federal balances — and the interest exposure that came with them — went away. The next FFR showed cash and outlays in step, and the cash-management finding did not recur.

## Lesson

Draw federal cash only to need, and treat the period of performance as a hard boundary. Drawing ahead of disbursement is a cash-management finding even when every underlying cost is allowable — the violation is the timing, plus any interest the idle balance earns. Reconcile draws to outlays each FFR cycle, and never let early-drawn cash pay for costs incurred outside the period without explicit prior authority.
