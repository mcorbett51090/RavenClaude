## Problem

A pass-through entity won a federal award and gave a sizable chunk of the program to a partner organization that would actually deliver services to beneficiaries. The grants office processed the partner as a **vendor** — a simple contract, an invoice, payment — because that's how they handled all outside spend. No risk assessment, no sub-award terms flowing down the federal requirements, no ongoing monitoring. At the Single Audit, the auditor reclassified the partner as a **sub-recipient** (it was carrying out the program and making programmatic decisions), and the complete absence of sub-recipient monitoring became a finding — which, as the pass-through, was *their* finding, not the partner's.

## Constraints context

- The partner made programmatic decisions and was measured against the program's objectives — the substance of a sub-recipient, regardless of the "contract" label.
- The grants office had treated the classification as a paperwork formality and applied a remembered rule of thumb instead of the 2 CFR 200 criteria.
- The expenditures put them over the single-audit threshold that year, so the gap was actually examined.

## Attempts

- Tried: arguing the label — "the agreement says contractor." Failed — substance over form governs; the relationship, not the document's title, decides the classification.
- Tried: reconstructing monitoring evidence after the auditor flagged it. Failed — monitoring rebuilt at audit time isn't contemporaneous; the finding stood.
- Tried (the fix): classifying every outside relationship at the sub-award stage using the 2 CFR 200 criteria, and for sub-recipients standing up the full chain — pre-award risk assessment, sub-award terms that flow down the federal requirements, ongoing monitoring, and single-audit follow-up — with each step cited to the current 2 CFR section, not memory.

## Resolution

Classification moved to the budget/sub-award stage and became a documented decision against the 2 CFR 200 criteria. Sub-recipients now get a risk assessment and a monitoring plan from day one; contractors get procurement compliance. The next Single Audit was clean on this point because the obligation was built contemporaneously instead of reconstructed.

## Lesson

The sub-recipient is your liability, and the classification is structural, not clerical. Decide sub-recipient-vs-contractor at the sub-award using the 2 CFR 200 substance test (does the partner carry out the program and make programmatic decisions?), then owe the full monitoring chain for sub-recipients. Cite the current 2 CFR authority, not a remembered rule — and build the documentation as you go, because monitoring reconstructed at audit time is already a finding.
