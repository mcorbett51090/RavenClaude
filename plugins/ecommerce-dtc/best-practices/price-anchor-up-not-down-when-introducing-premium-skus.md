# Price anchor up, not down, when introducing premium SKUs

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** DTC merchandising and pricing
**Applies to:** `ecommerce-dtc`

---

## Why this exists

When a brand introduces a premium tier, the natural instinct is to justify the price by listing features. The problem is that features don't establish value — context does. Anchoring the new premium price against a lower existing price trains customers to see the premium as expensive rather than as a step up. Anchoring against a higher reference point (a market equivalent, a limited bundle, or a flagship SKU) creates the same price gap but reads as a deal. The anchor you place first in the funnel sets the interpretive frame for every price below it.

## How to apply

Design the pricing display order so the highest-priced option appears first or is the most visually prominent. Place the premium SKU next to a market-rate equivalent or a higher-priced bundle, not next to your entry price.

```
Wrong order (anchors DOWN):
  Basic — $18
  Standard — $32
  Premium — $58   ← reads as expensive

Correct order (anchors UP):
  Bundle (3-month supply) — $149
  Premium — $58   ← reads as reasonable
  Standard — $32
```

**Do:**
- Lead product page layouts with the highest-value option.
- Show a "most popular" badge on the mid-tier only after the premium is visible.
- Compare the premium SKU to a market equivalent in copy ("vs. $90 retail equivalent").

**Don't:**
- Display prices in ascending order on a product listing page.
- Introduce premium pricing without a higher anchor already on the page.
- Use "only $X more" language that implicitly anchors to the cheaper SKU.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Price-sensitive acquisition channels (Google Shopping, deal aggregators) sort by lowest price — for those surfaces the entry SKU must be front. The anchoring discipline applies to owned product pages and email flows where you control layout. Early-stage brands with a single SKU have no internal anchor; use a market comparison or a multi-unit bundle as the anchor before expanding the line.

## Provenance

Codifies standard behavioral-economics anchoring practice (Ariely, *Predictably Irrational*, 2008; Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow*, 2011) applied to DTC product-page merchandising. The premium-anchor direction (high to low) is the team's standing opinion on merchandising conversion; the ascending-price display is the dominant anti-pattern seen in operator-submitted funnels.
