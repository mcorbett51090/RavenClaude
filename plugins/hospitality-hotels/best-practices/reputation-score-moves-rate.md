# Reputation score moves rate

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Hotel guest experience — reputation-revenue relationship
**Applies to:** `hospitality-hotels`

---

## Why this exists

Online review scores are a pricing signal, not just a feedback mechanism. Research from Cornell's
Center for Hospitality Research and subsequent industry studies has documented a statistically
significant relationship between a hotel's average online review score and its ability to command
an ADR premium versus its competitive set — independent of brand affiliation and physical star
rating [verify-at-use].

The practical consequence: a hotel that manages reputation as a PR function, separate from revenue
strategy, is leaving rate headroom on the table. Conversely, a hotel that actively manages review
response rates, service-recovery, and category-level score drivers is building a long-term
pricing asset.

## How to apply

- **Do:** Track overall review score and category sub-scores (cleanliness, service, value, F&B,
  location) as revenue leading indicators, reviewed in the weekly revenue meeting alongside OTB,
  pick-up, and comp-set rate position.
- **Do:** Set a reputation score target alongside RevPAR and ADR targets. Quantify the ADR
  headroom: for a ~1-point improvement on a 10-point scale, the typical ADR uplift range cited
  in research is 1.4–2.7% [verify-at-use]. Apply this to the property's current ADR to estimate
  the dollar value of the improvement.
- **Do:** Route negative reviews to the operational department that owns the failure category
  (cleanliness → `rooms-and-housekeeping-analyst`; service → front office; F&B →
  `restaurant-operations`) — reputation management is operationally owned, not marketingly owned.
- **Do:** Respond to reviews (target: ≥80% response rate within 72 hours [verify-at-use]). An
  empathetic, specific response to a negative review signals quality management to future
  readers and partially mitigates the score impact.

**Don't:**

- Treat reputation management as a standalone function disconnected from pricing and revenue.
- Set a score target without a category-level driver analysis — a 4.0 overall score driven by
  a 2.8 cleanliness sub-score has a different fix path than one driven by a 3.0 value sub-score.
- Offer room-rate discounts or free nights in public review responses — this signals to gaming
  reviewers and sets an expectation that complaints yield compensation.
- Ignore volume: a score based on 12 reviews in a year is too volatile to act on; wait for a
  statistically meaningful review count before investing in a specific category fix.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- **Location sub-score:** location is not operationally controllable. It should be managed in
  the booking experience (setting accurate expectations pre-arrival) rather than through service
  improvement programs.
- **Brand-mandated scoring tools:** branded properties using brand-required satisfaction survey
  platforms (e.g., GSS, brand-specific NPS) may use those as the primary metric; cross-reference
  to OTA/TripAdvisor scores as a sanity check and public-reputation supplement.

## Provenance

The reputation–ADR correlation is empirically documented by Anderson (2012, Cornell CHR) "The
Impact of Social Media on Lodging Performance" and subsequent replications [verify-at-use — the
specific 1.4–2.7% figure is from that literature and should be re-verified against current
research before citing in a deliverable]. Industry platforms TrustYou and ReviewPro/Shiji
operationalize this with proprietary weighting models (TrustScore, GRI); the directional
relationship is widely accepted in hospitality RM practice.
