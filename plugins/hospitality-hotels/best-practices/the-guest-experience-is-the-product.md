# The guest experience is the product

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Hotel operations — revenue-experience relationship
**Applies to:** `hospitality-hotels`

---

## Why this exists

Hotels sell an experience, not a room. The physical room is the minimum viable product; the
experience — the quality of the welcome, the cleanliness, the responsiveness of the staff, the
F&B, the ease of departure — is what generates loyalty, word-of-mouth, and the willingness to
pay the ADR premium that separates a well-regarded property from a commodity.

A revenue strategy that consistently erodes the guest experience is eating the asset. Short-term
RevPAR gains from aggressive discounting to fill (which trains the guest to expect low rates),
from overbooking without a recovery playbook (which turns a valued guest into an angry one), or
from cost-cutting that degrades cleanliness and service (which shows up in review scores and then
in rate compression) all sacrifice the long-term rate and loyalty base for short-term occupancy
metrics.

## How to apply

- **Do:** Treat the guest-satisfaction score (NPS, CSAT, review score by category) as a revenue
  leading indicator, not a lagging soft metric. Score trends predict rate headroom and repeat
  rates.
- **Do:** Involve `guest-experience-lead` in any revenue decision that materially affects the
  guest experience: overbooking policy (walk risk), aggressive last-minute discounting
  (value-perception signal), or cost reductions in the rooms department (cleanliness impact).
- **Do:** Use the known correlation between review score and ADR premium to quantify the revenue
  value of guest-experience investment. The investment case for better service is a revenue
  case, not a soft-benefit case.
- **Do:** Treat repeat-guest rate as a revenue metric — a guest who returns costs $0 in OTA
  commission and has a known lifetime value.

**Don't:**

- Run a revenue strategy that routinely fills the hotel via overbooking without a walk-recovery
  playbook — the walked guest is a loyalty liability, not just a logistics problem.
- Cost-cut the rooms or housekeeping department without modeling the cleanliness-score impact and
  the downstream ADR effect.
- Treat reputation management as a PR or marketing function separate from revenue management.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- **Distressed inventory:** a last-minute deep discount on a soft date to a price-sensitive
  leisure segment who would not be a repeat guest anyway has a low guest-experience cost and a
  real RevPAR benefit. Segment correctly before applying the rule.
- **Renovation disruption:** a short-term guest-experience degradation during a planned
  renovation (noise, restricted facilities) is expected and manageable if communicated pre-
  arrival. The rule applies to systematic, avoidable experience erosion, not temporary
  operational disruption.
