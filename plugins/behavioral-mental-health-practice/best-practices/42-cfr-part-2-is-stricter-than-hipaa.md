# 42 CFR Part 2 is stricter than HIPAA

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Behavioral health regulatory compliance
**Applies to:** `behavioral-mental-health-practice`

---

## Why this exists

42 CFR Part 2 (Confidentiality of Substance Use Disorder Patient Records) is a separate federal
regulation that protects records of patients receiving SUD diagnosis, treatment, or referral from a
federally assisted program. It is **not** a subset of HIPAA — it is an independent law with its own
consent requirements, its own penalty regime, and its own rules on re-disclosure.

The most common and consequential error practices make: they treat SUD records as ordinary HIPAA-
protected PHI and rely on the HIPAA TPO (treatment, payment, operations) carve-out to share records
without patient consent. **That carve-out does not exist under 42 CFR Part 2.** A disclosure of
Part 2-protected records for billing, coordination of care, or referral — without a specific written
Part 2 consent — is a federal violation, even if it would be perfectly legal under HIPAA alone.

## How to apply

Every time a disclosure of patient records is being considered, ask first: **could these records be
SUD records from a federally assisted program?** If yes, stop and apply the Part 2 framework before
HIPAA.

**Do:**

- Include a 42 CFR Part 2-compliant consent-to-disclose form in every intake packet for practices
  that offer SUD services.
- Require a valid, patient-signed Part 2 consent before disclosing SUD records to any third party
  (including insurance companies, referring providers, and other treating clinicians).
- Include the prohibited re-disclosure notice with every authorized disclosure.
- Train front-desk and billing staff on which records are Part 2-protected vs. HIPAA-only.
- Consult counsel before relying on a Part 2 exception (emergency, audit, court order).

**Don't:**

- Rely on HIPAA's TPO carve-out for SUD records from a federally assisted program.
- Disclose SUD records for billing purposes without a valid Part 2 consent.
- Treat a general HIPAA ROI as sufficient for a Part 2 disclosure.
- Assume that because a disclosure is routine for non-SUD records, it is also permissible for SUD records.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- If the practice does not treat SUD patients and is not a federally assisted SUD program, Part 2
  does not apply to its records. However, practices should confirm their program status — "federally
  assisted" includes accepting Medicaid, Medicare, or other federal funding, which covers most
  outpatient practices.
- The 2020 Part 2 amendment aligned some uses (certain research, audits, and program evaluations)
  closer to HIPAA, but the specific-written-consent requirement for most disclosures remains.
  Verify current regulatory status at use [verify-at-use].
- Records that are BOTH HIPAA-covered and Part 2-covered must satisfy **both** frameworks; the more
  restrictive standard (Part 2) governs.
