# Document medical necessity for every billable service

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Clinical documentation and billing compliance
**Applies to:** `behavioral-mental-health-practice`

---

## Why this exists

Payers do not pay for services because they were delivered — they pay for services that were
**medically necessary** and **documented as such**. A clinician who delivers excellent care but does
not document medical necessity in the clinical record is creating a billing compliance risk on every
claim. If the documentation does not demonstrate medical necessity, the claim can be denied,
recouped after payment, or flagged in a compliance audit.

In behavioral health specifically, "medical necessity" means documenting the link between the
diagnosis, the specific functional impairment the diagnosis causes, and why the proposed service
(type, frequency, duration) is the appropriate treatment. "Patient has depression" is not medical
necessity documentation. "Patient presents with MDD causing significant occupational impairment
requiring weekly individual therapy to restore functioning" is.

## How to apply

**Do:**

- Include a medical-necessity statement in **every** treatment plan and **every** progress note.
- Follow the documentation pattern: diagnosis → functional impairment → treatment rationale →
  why this frequency and duration is appropriate.
- Use measurement-based care (MBC) scores as longitudinal evidence of continued medical necessity
  (a downward PHQ-9 trend supports continued treatment; stagnant scores may indicate a need to
  reassess the treatment approach).
- Update the treatment plan at each authorization renewal to re-document continued medical necessity
  with current MBC scores and functional status.

**Don't:**

- Write progress notes that describe session content but omit medical-necessity justification.
- Assume the payer can infer medical necessity from the diagnosis code alone.
- Submit an authorization renewal without an updated treatment plan that re-documents medical necessity.
- Use vague language ("patient continues to benefit") without tying it to functional outcomes.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- Self-pay patients are not subject to payer medical-necessity requirements, but documentation is
  still required for the clinical record and for liability protection.
- Some Medicaid programs have specific medical-necessity criteria that differ from commercial payers —
  the documentation language should be tailored to the applicable standard.
- The rule applies to all licensed behavioral health service types: individual therapy, group therapy,
  family therapy, psychiatric evaluation, medication management, and crisis services.

## Provenance

Codifies the payer medical-necessity documentation standards common to commercial insurance,
Medicaid managed care, and Medicare behavioral health services. The specific criteria vary by payer;
the pattern of diagnosis → impairment → rationale is the widely accepted minimum. Practices should
confirm specific payer requirements in their contracts and payer-specific documentation guidelines.
