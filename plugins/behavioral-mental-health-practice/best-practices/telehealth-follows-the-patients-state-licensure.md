# Telehealth follows the patient's state licensure

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Telehealth compliance and clinician licensure
**Applies to:** `behavioral-mental-health-practice`

---

## Why this exists

The most common and costly licensure error in telehealth behavioral health practice: a clinician
licensed in State A sees a patient who is physically located in State B during the session, without
holding a license (or compact authority) in State B. The governing principle is clear and not subject
to interpretation: **the clinician must be licensed in the state where the patient is physically
located at the time of the session, not just the state where the practice is located.**

This rule exists in every US state's professional licensing law and has been consistently enforced
by state licensing boards, sometimes with serious consequences (license discipline, civil liability).
Interstate telehealth compacts (PSYPACT, Counseling Compact, LCSW Compact, APRN Compact) provide
pathways for cross-state practice under specific conditions, but they are not universal — each
compact covers a specific license type, and not all states participate.

The COVID-19 public health emergency generated a wave of state-level telehealth waivers that
temporarily relaxed cross-state licensure requirements. Most of those waivers have expired. Practices
that built telehealth workflows around PHE waivers need to audit their current licensure exposure.

## How to apply

**Do:**

- Build a provider licensure matrix: for each clinician, list the states where they hold a current,
  active license or compact authority.
- Capture and document the patient's physical location at the **start of every telehealth session**
  (not just at intake).
- Confirm that the clinician holds licensure or compact authority in the patient's state before
  scheduling any telehealth session.
- If a patient will be traveling out of state, flag that session for licensure review before it occurs.
- Verify interstate compact participation and enrollment status directly with the compact authority —
  states join and leave compacts; compact enrollment requires active maintenance [verify-at-use].

**Don't:**

- Assume that a license in the practice's state covers telehealth services to patients in other states.
- Treat PHE-era waivers as still in effect without confirming current state law [verify-at-use].
- Assume an interstate compact covers all license types or all states — check specifically.
- Fail to document patient location at each session — location is the audit evidence.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

- If both the clinician and the patient are located in the same state at the time of the session,
  this rule is satisfied by the clinician's home-state license.
- Some federal facilities (VA, Indian Health Service, military) have different jurisdictional rules
  for telehealth — verify the specific program's authority [verify-at-use].
- Peer-support and non-licensed services may have different licensure frameworks — verify per state.
