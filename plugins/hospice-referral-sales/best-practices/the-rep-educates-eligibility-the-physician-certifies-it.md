# The representative educates eligibility; the physician certifies it

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Eligibility / clinical boundary
**Applies to:** `hospice-referral-sales`

---

## Why this exists

The single most consequential line in hospice referral work is the line between *education* and *certification*. A liaison who tells a referral source, a patient, or a family that a patient "qualifies for hospice," "is eligible," or "is covered" has crossed from sales into a clinical determination they are not authorized to make — and has created a compliance and liability exposure. Hospice eligibility (a prognosis of six months or less if the disease runs its normal course) is certified by the **attending physician and the hospice medical director** on clinical judgment. The rep's job is to make a clinician able to *recognize* a patient who warrants that conversation — never to make the call.

## How to apply

**Do:**
- Teach the published criteria: "patients with this decline picture often warrant a hospice conversation with their physician."
- End every eligibility-related deliverable with the explicit line: *the attending physician and medical director certify eligibility.*
- When asked "is this patient eligible?", answer with the indicators present and route the determination to the physician — never with "yes."
- Use the `eligibility-indicators` tally and the patient-ready decision tree, both of which end at "route to physician."

**Don't:**
- Say "qualifies," "is eligible," or "is covered" about a specific patient.
- Let an in-service hand a non-physician a checklist that functions as a certification tool.
- Imply that your recognition of indicators is the eligibility decision.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Teaching the *general* criteria ("FAST stage 7 plus a complication anchors the dementia LCD") is education and is encouraged. The rule bites the moment a *specific patient* is labeled eligible. Even a physician asking you to confirm eligibility gets the criteria and a "your clinical judgment certifies it," not a verdict.
