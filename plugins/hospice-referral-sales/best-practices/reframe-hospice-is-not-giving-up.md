# Reframe "hospice is giving up" — once, with empathy, never with pressure

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Goals-of-care conversation
**Applies to:** `hospice-referral-sales`

---

## Why this exists

"Hospice is giving up" is the most common objection and the most common reason families decline a benefit that would help them. It is a fear, not a logical position, and it is answered with empathy and accurate reframing — not with a counter-argument or pressure. The reframe: hospice is not giving up; it is choosing how to live this time with the most support possible, shifting the focus to comfort and to what matters most to the patient. Named once, gently, it lands; pushed, it confirms the fear.

## How to apply

**Do:**
- Acknowledge the feeling first ("I understand why it feels that way").
- Reframe to "choosing how to live this time, fully supported."
- Name the revocability — electing hospice is not a one-way door; a patient can resume curative care.
- Then listen; let the family sit with it.

**Don't:**
- Argue, repeat, or pressure.
- Manufacture urgency against a grieving family.
- Treat a "not yet" as a failure rather than a step.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Some families are ready and need information, not reframing — don't impose an objection they don't hold. And when the honest answer is palliative care (not yet hospice), say so; it builds the trust that brings the referral later.
