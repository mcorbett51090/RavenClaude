# Earlier is better — the too-late referral is the core failure to fix

**Status:** Pattern
**Domain:** Education / timing
**Applies to:** `hospice-referral-sales`

---

## Why this exists

A large share of hospice patients are referred so late that length of stay is measured in days — sometimes hours. A very short stay denies the patient and family the actual benefit of hospice (symptom management, support, planning, bereavement) and is the clearest signal of an upstream education gap. The highest-value work a liaison does is not winning the next referral; it is moving a referral source's *average* referral earlier in the disease, so patients reach hospice while there is still time to help.

## How to apply

**Do:**
- Read a short average/median length of stay as a late-referral / education signal, not an intake problem.
- Target in-service education at *earlier recognition* (the non-disease-specific decline picture).
- Use a "patient died before admission" decline as the strongest teaching case for earlier referral.
- Track length of stay alongside conversion as a quality-of-access measure.

**Don't:**
- Accept a short length of stay as normal.
- Optimize only for referral volume while ignoring how late those referrals arrive.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Some referrals are genuinely late because the patient's decline was rapid and unforeseeable — not every short stay is an education failure. Read the *pattern* across a source, not a single case.
