# Never guarantee admission or promise Medicare coverage

**Status:** Absolute rule
**Domain:** Marketing truthfulness / clinical boundary
**Applies to:** `hospice-referral-sales`

---

## Why this exists

A vulnerable family at end of life is the least appropriate audience for an over-promise, and a guarantee of admission or coverage is both untrue and a marketing-compliance violation. Admission depends on physician certification of a terminal prognosis and the patient's election; coverage depends on the benefit rules. A liaison who says "we'll get them admitted" or "Medicare will cover everything" has made a promise they cannot keep and may have misled a beneficiary. The honest posture is to explain the benefit accurately and route the determination to the clinicians.

## How to apply

**Do:**
- Explain what hospice provides and how the benefit generally works, accurately.
- Say "the physician determines eligibility; the team will assess" rather than "they'll be admitted."
- Describe coverage in the conditional ("the Medicare Hospice Benefit generally covers…") and route specifics appropriately.

**Don't:**
- Guarantee an admission, a timeline, or a clinical outcome.
- Promise that "everything is covered" or that there will be no cost.
- Manufacture urgency to push an election.

## Edge cases / when the rule does NOT apply

Accurately describing the benefit's general structure (the four levels of care, the team, 24/7 availability) is not a guarantee — it is education. The rule bites on *promises of a specific result* (this patient will be admitted / fully covered).
