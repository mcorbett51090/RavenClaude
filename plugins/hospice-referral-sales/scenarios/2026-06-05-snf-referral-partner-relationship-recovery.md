## Problem

A reliable skilled-nursing referral partner went quiet. Over two months its referrals to the hospice fell to near zero with no explanation. The liaison's instinct was to bring the building a thank-you lunch and "remind them we're here." The real cause surfaced only on a direct, humble conversation: a weekend admission had gone badly — a slow after-hours response had left a declining resident and a distressed family without timely hospice support, and the building's DON had quietly redirected referrals to a competitor she trusted to show up.

## Context

- Segment: a SNF that had been a steady referrer; the relationship was single-threaded on one nurse-liaison contact, not the DON.
- Constraint: a service failure handled invisibly *loses* a partner; handled visibly and fast, it can *rebuild* the relationship stronger. The lever was responsiveness and honesty, not a gift — and a gift offered to "win them back" would also have raised an anti-kickback question.
- The liaison had also mis-diagnosed the cooling as a relationship-warmth problem rather than a service-trust problem, and had no multi-threaded relationship to surface the truth.

## Attempts

- Tried: ran the declined-referral root-cause tree, which separated a *service/relationship* cause from an eligibility or competitor-price cause and pointed at responsiveness. Outcome: the team stopped guessing and asked the DON directly.
- Tried: a candid recovery conversation — acknowledge the failure fast, explain the specific fix (an after-hours admission protocol with a named on-call path), and commit to prove it with the next case. No gift, no defensiveness. Outcome: the DON agreed to a single trial referral.
- Tried: multi-threaded the relationship (DON + social worker + the unit charge nurses, not just the one contact) and brought a short, de-identified quarterly review led by *patient outcomes* (time-to-admit, after-hours responsiveness, family feedback), not referral volume. Outcome: referrals resumed and then exceeded the prior baseline as trust rebuilt.

## Resolution

The cooling was a **service-trust** failure, not a relationship-warmth one — and the fix was visible responsiveness and honesty, not a thank-you gift (which would also have been a compliance question). Diagnosing the real cause, owning the failure, proving the after-hours fix on a trial case, and multi-threading the account recovered and grew the partner. The lesson: *a service failure handled visibly is a retention moment*, and you never try to buy a referral relationship back.

**Action for the next consultant hitting this pattern:** when a steady partner goes quiet, **diagnose the cause directly** (run the root-cause tree, then ask) before reaching for a gift or a lunch. Own any service failure fast, fix it with a named protocol, prove it on a trial case, and multi-thread so you are never blind again. Lead the recovery review with patient outcomes, not referral counts (§3 #3), and route any value exchange to compliance first (§3 #2). Keep all case detail de-identified (§3 #7).
