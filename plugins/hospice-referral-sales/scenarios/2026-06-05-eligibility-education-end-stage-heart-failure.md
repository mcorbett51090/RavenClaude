## Problem

A cardiology practice with a large panel of advanced heart-failure patients almost never referred to hospice. When patients did arrive, it was via the hospital in their final days. The physicians weren't opposed to hospice — they simply weren't *recognizing* their end-stage heart-failure patients as hospice-appropriate, because they (like many) associated hospice with cancer and waited for a clearer "terminal" signal that heart failure rarely gives until very late.

## Context

- Segment: a high-eligibility-density cardiology practice (the kind of source the prioritization tree ranks at the top), cold to hospice.
- Constraint: the majority of hospice patients today have **non-cancer** diagnoses, and end-stage heart disease has published LCD decline indicators (NYHA Class IV, optimally treated, with supportive markers). But the rep can only **educate** these criteria — the cardiologist certifies the prognosis. [confirm against the current cardiac hospice LCD, retrieved 2026-06-05 — figures `[example]`]
- The "hospice is cancer-only" misconception and the absence of a single dramatic terminal signal in heart failure were jointly producing late referrals.

## Attempts

- Tried: built an in-service specifically on **recognizing the end-stage heart-failure patient** — the NYHA Class IV picture in an optimally-treated patient, plus the non-disease-specific decline overlay (recurrent decompensation admissions, weight loss, falling function). Delivered as recognition education, ending every time at "your clinical judgment certifies the prognosis." Outcome: the physicians had a concrete picture to watch for.
- Tried: corrected the cancer-only myth head-on and named the **revocability** of the benefit (a patient who stabilizes can revoke) — which removed the "it feels too final for a heart-failure patient who might rally" hesitation. Outcome: the perceived risk of an early conversation dropped.
- Tried: offered to support the goals-of-care conversation with families (continuity, not abandonment framing) so the cardiologist wasn't carrying it alone. Outcome: referrals began arriving earlier in the disease.

## Resolution

The gap was **recognition and a myth**, not opposition. Diagnosis-specific recognition education (the cardiac LCD picture plus the non-disease-specific decline overlay), an explicit correction of the cancer-only misconception, and naming the benefit's revocability moved a cold, high-density cardiology source toward earlier referrals — without the rep ever certifying a single patient. The lesson: *the richest territory is often a high-eligibility-density source that simply isn't recognizing its own eligible patients.*

**Action for the next consultant hitting this pattern:** with a cold, high-density non-cancer source (cardiology, pulmonology, nephrology, dementia-heavy SNFs), lead with **diagnosis-specific recognition education** plus the non-disease-specific decline overlay, correct the cancer-only myth, and name revocability to lower the perceived risk of an early conversation. Educate only — route certification to the physician (§3 #1), cite the LCD and date (§3 #8), and keep cases de-identified (§3 #7).
