## The statute in one line

It is a **criminal** offense to knowingly and willfully offer, pay, solicit, or receive **any remuneration** to induce or reward referrals of items/services reimbursable by a federal healthcare program. Intent-based; "remuneration" is anything of value. One purpose of the payment being to induce referrals is enough.

## Safe harbors a liaison's arrangements most often invoke

### Personal services and management contracts

All of the following, or no protection:

- Written, signed agreement specifying the services.
- Term of **at least one year**.
- Aggregate compensation **set in advance**, consistent with **fair market value**, in an arm's-length transaction.
- Compensation **not** determined in a manner that takes into account the **volume or value of referrals**.
- Services do not exceed those reasonably necessary for a commercially reasonable business purpose.

### Space and equipment rental

- Written, signed lease, **≥1-year** term.
- Rent **set in advance**, at **fair market value**, **not** volume/value-of-referrals based.
- Space/equipment limited to what is reasonably necessary.

(This matters when a hospice is offered, or asked for, an office or space in a referring facility — a classic OIG-scrutinized arrangement.)

### Employment

Bona fide **employees** (W-2) are protected — which is why a hospice's own employed liaisons are not the AKS exposure; the exposure is in arrangements with **outside** referral sources.

## Bright lines (never safe-harbor-able)

- **Cash or cash-equivalents** (including gift cards) to a referral source.
- Anything **tied to referral volume or value**.
- **Free or below-fair-market-value** goods/services to a referral source that offset their costs (free staffing, free supplies, free services they'd otherwise pay for).
- Patient inducements outside the beneficiary-inducement nominal-value exception.
