# Hospice eligibility — LCD reference (EDUCATION, not certification)

> **Last reviewed: 2026-06-05 by `claude`.** The canonical reference for the published hospice eligibility criteria a liaison teaches referral sources, so a clinician can **recognize** a potentially eligible patient. **This is education. It is not a certification or determination tool.** The attending physician and the hospice medical director certify eligibility — a prognosis of six months or less if the disease runs its normal course — on clinical judgment. ## How to use this with a referral source

1. **Teach the non-disease-specific decline guidelines first** — they apply across every diagnosis and catch the multi-morbidity and failure-to-thrive patients single-diagnosis LCDs miss.
2. **Then the diagnosis card** — the specific decline indicators for the patient's condition.
3. **Always close with the line:** _the attending physician and medical director certify eligibility; this education helps you recognize a patient who warrants that conversation._

Never convert this into a checklist a non-physician uses to tell a family a patient "qualifies."

## The non-disease-specific decline guidelines

The decline picture that supports eligibility regardless of primary diagnosis:

- **Functional decline** — a falling Palliative Performance Scale (PPS), increasing dependence in activities of daily living, progression to mostly bed/chair-bound.
- **Nutritional decline** — unintentional weight loss, falling BMI, declining serum albumin, decreasing oral intake, dysphagia.
- **Recurrent acute events** — repeated infections (aspiration pneumonia, UTIs/pyelonephritis, sepsis), repeated hospitalizations or ED visits.
- **Progressive symptom burden** — dyspnea, pain, or other symptoms worsening despite optimal treatment.
- **Comorbidity burden** — multiple serious conditions compounding the trajectory.

Teach clinicians to see the **trajectory over time**, not a single value.

## Diagnosis cards

> Each card lists the published decline indicators **as education**. Confirm against the current LCD; treat every number as an example.

### Heart disease (end-stage)

NYHA Class IV (symptoms at rest), already optimally treated. Supportive: EF ≤ ~20%, treatment-refractory angina at rest, history of cardiac arrest/syncope/cardiogenic embolic stroke.

### Pulmonary disease (end-stage)

Disabling dyspnea at rest, poorly responsive to bronchodilators; declining function; recurrent pulmonary infections/respiratory failure. Supportive: FEV1 < ~30%, hypoxemia/hypercapnia, cor pulmonale, resting tachycardia, weight loss.

### Dementia (Alzheimer's and related)

FAST stage 7 (non-ambulatory, dependent in dressing/bathing, incontinence, speech limited to a few words) **plus** a recent complication: aspiration pneumonia, pyelonephritis, sepsis, multiple pressure ulcers, recurrent fever, or inability to maintain intake with weight loss.

### Renal disease (end-stage)

Not pursuing (or discontinuing) dialysis/transplant; creatinine clearance < ~10 mL/min (< ~15 with comorbidity); serum creatinine > ~8.0 (> ~6.0 for diabetics); signs of uremia, oliguria, intractable hyperkalemia, hepatorenal syndrome. `[example — confirm]`

### Liver disease (end-stage)

PT > ~5 sec over control or INR > ~1.5 **and** serum albumin < ~2.5 g/dL, plus one of: refractory ascites, hepatic encephalopathy, spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, hepatorenal syndrome, recurrent variceal bleeding.

### Stroke / coma

Poor functional status (PPS ≤ ~40%), inability to maintain hydration/nutrition, post-stroke dysphagia with weight loss not amenable to (or the patient declining) artificial feeding. Coma: persistent abnormal brainstem/withdrawal responses with decline.

### ALS / neuromuscular

Rapid progression with critically impaired respiratory function (declining vital capacity, dyspnea at rest, declining ventilatory support) **or** critical nutritional impairment (declining a feeding tube with weight loss/dehydration).

### Cancer

Metastatic or progressive disease with declining performance status (PPS trending down) and/or a decision against — or failure of — further disease-directed treatment.

### HIV

CD4 < ~25 or persistent viral load > ~100,000 with a defining opportunistic condition and declining function. `[example — confirm]`

### Adult failure-to-thrive

The non-disease-specific decline picture itself: BMI < ~22, declining functional status (PPS ≤ ~40%), and inability/refusal to take adequate nutrition.

## The myths to correct (the top causes of late referrals)

| Myth | Accurate framing |
| --- | --- |
| "Need a DNR" | A DNR is **not** required to elect hospice. |
| "Cancer-only" | The **majority** of hospice patients have non-cancer diagnoses. |
| "Stop all treatment" | Curative treatment **for the terminal illness** is forgone; comfort care and treatment of unrelated conditions continue. |
| "Last 48 hours" | Hospice is for a **six-month-or-less** prognosis; earlier election gives the full benefit. |
| "It's irreversible" | Hospice is **revocable** — a patient can resume curative care at any time. |

## The standing line

Education up to the conversation; **the physician certifies.** A liaison who recognizes the decline picture and prompts an earlier physician conversation has done the highest-value work in hospice — without ever certifying a patient.
