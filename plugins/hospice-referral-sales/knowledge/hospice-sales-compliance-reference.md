## 1. Anti-Kickback Statute (AKS)

A **criminal** prohibition on knowingly and willfully offering, paying, soliciting, or receiving **any remuneration** to induce or reward referrals of items/services reimbursable by a federal healthcare program. Intent-based; "remuneration" is anything of value; one improper purpose is enough. Hospice referral-source relationships are squarely in scope.

**Safe harbors** protect specific structures **only if every element is met** (partial compliance ≠ partial protection). The liaison-relevant ones — personal services & management contracts, space/equipment rental, and bona fide employment — and their elements (written, ≥1-year term, fair market value, **not** volume/value-of-referrals based) are detailed in `../skills/hospice-sales-compliance/resources/aks-safe-harbors.md`.

## 2. Stark law (physician self-referral)

A **strict-liability civil** law: a physician may not refer designated health services to an entity with which the physician (or an immediate family member) has a financial relationship unless an exception is met. Where a referral source is a **physician**, Stark applies **in addition** to AKS — and because there is no intent requirement, a technical violation still counts. Physician arrangements go to counsel.

## 3. Beneficiary-inducement CMP

Prohibits offering a Medicare/Medicaid **beneficiary** remuneration likely to influence their choice of provider. Patient/family gifts must fit the **nominal-value** exception (small per-item and annual caps, `[example — confirm the current CMS figures]`) and never be cash or cash-equivalents.

## 4. Gifts, meals, sponsorships — the nominal-value discipline

"Small and infrequent" is a **documented structure**, not a judgment call:

- **Non-cash; never cash or cash-equivalents** (gift cards included).
- **Within the published per-item and annual nominal-value limits** — cite them; treat any remembered figure as example-until-confirmed.
- **Never tied to referral volume or value.**
- **Documented.** A pattern of undocumented meals is itself a risk signal.

## 5. High-risk arrangements

Placing staff in a facility, providing free or below-FMV services, renting space — each is an OIG-scrutinized arrangement that needs a **fair-market-value, written, non-volume-based** structure and counsel review. Free services that offset a referral source's costs are a classic AKS red flag.

## 6. Truthful, non-misleading marketing

For any piece or message, read for the four failures:

- An **eligibility or coverage guarantee** (the physician certifies; nobody promises coverage).
- A **misleading or unsubstantiated** claim about the benefit or outcomes.
- **Competitor disparagement** with unverified facts.
- **Pressure** on a vulnerable patient or family.

## 7. OIG hospice risk areas

The published enforcement themes that raise scrutiny on referral relationships: **ineligible patients / inappropriately long lengths of stay**, **improper financial relationships with nursing facilities and physicians**, and **GIP (general inpatient) misuse**. These shape the cautious default.

## 8. HIPAA / PHI for a liaison

A liaison handles real patient data — diagnoses, prognoses, names, facilities. The discipline:

- **Minimum-necessary** — only the data needed for the task.
- **A HIPAA-safe boundary** — PHI stays inside approved systems and channels.
- **Never patient-identifying** in a CRM note shared out, an email, an example, a scenario, or any deliverable this plugin produces.
- Route anything beyond minimum-necessary framing to the **privacy officer**.

## The operating rule

For any value exchange, marketing piece, or PHI question: **name the rule → locate the line → give the safe-harbor structure → state the question for the compliance officer → never green-light.** The `## Decision Tree: Gift / meal / arrangement anti-kickback gate` in `hospice-sales-decision-trees.md` ends every such question at exactly that route. When in doubt, **stop and ask the compliance officer** — the cost of asking is minutes; the cost of a finding is the program.
